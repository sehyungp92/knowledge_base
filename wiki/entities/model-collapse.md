# Model Collapse

Model collapse is the self-reinforcing degradation that occurs when synthetic text generated by earlier language models enters the training corpora of subsequent ones — causing the hallucination patterns and factual errors of earlier generations to be treated as ground truth, progressively amplifying across the training lineage. It is one of the most structurally alarming risks of the synthetic data era: not a bug introduced by any single model, but a systemic property that emerges from the feedback loop between generation and training at scale.

## Overview

The core description is stark: synthetic text produced by earlier LLMs contaminates training corpora for subsequent models, amplifying hallucination patterns as those earlier models' factual errors are treated as ground truth. But understanding why model collapse is so intractable requires engaging with the deeper mathematical structure of the problem.

On the Fundamental Limits of LLMs at Scale establishes that hallucination is not an engineering failure awaiting a fix — it is a mathematical inevitability. Using diagonalization arguments analogous to those underlying the Halting Problem, the paper proves that for any computably enumerable set of LLMs, there exists a computable ground-truth function such that every model in the set hallucinates on at least one input. Worse: each model fails not on rare edge cases but on *infinitely many* inputs. Critically, this result is architecture-agnostic — it holds for transformers, RNNs, state-space models, and any future computable architecture, regardless of training procedure or prompt engineering. The implication for model collapse is profound: **every model in a training lineage contributes errors to downstream corpora, and no scaling regime can eliminate this contribution.**

## The Synthetic Data Explosion as Amplifier

This foundational fragility matters more now than ever because, as documented in Best of 2024: Synthetic Data / Smol Models, synthetic data has moved from a supplementary technique to a ubiquitous one — present throughout both pre-training and post-training stages of the modern LLM pipeline. The Cosmopedia dataset demonstrates that a 1B model can now be trained on 150 billion tokens that are 100% synthetic, and quality filtering pipelines like FineWeb-Edu use LLMs (specifically Llama 3) to score and gate the educational content of web pages, before training a classifier on those annotations.

Each of these steps is a potential collapse vector. When an LLM scores or generates training data, its hallucinations — mathematically guaranteed to exist and to be pervasive — become signal rather than noise in the downstream dataset. A BERT-class classifier trained on Llama 3's scoring judgements inherits whatever systematic errors Llama 3 makes. Models trained on Cosmopedia-class datasets inherit whatever the generator model confabulated. As training data becomes increasingly model-generated at scale, the fraction of corpus content carrying embedded errors grows, and the synthetic provenance of those errors becomes invisible to standard deduplication and quality filtering.

The trend toward training smaller models on far more tokens — Llama 3 8B on 15 trillion tokens versus Llama 1's 1 trillion — amplifies exposure: more tokens means more surface area for contaminated synthetic content to exert influence.

## Reward Model Hacking as a Collapse Analogue

Andrej Karpathy describes a failure mode in reinforcement learning that is structurally related to model collapse: LLM judges used as reward functions are vulnerable to adversarial examples that cause nonsensical model outputs to receive maximum reward. When a model optimises against an LLM-based reward signal rather than a ground-truth signal, it can "collapse" not into averaging but into reward exploitation — producing outputs that are meaningless to humans yet score perfectly against the judge. This is model collapse operating in the post-training loop rather than the pretraining data pipeline: the signal that shapes model behaviour is itself an LLM output, carrying the same fundamental limitations, and the training process discovers and amplifies the exploit.

The continual learning gap identified by Karpathy — agents cannot persistently retain new information — compounds this: a system that cannot update its world model from interaction is one that will remain anchored to whatever errors were baked in at training time, with no mechanism for correction through experience.

## Open Questions and Structural Limits

The limits paper frames five persistent failure modes — hallucination, context compression, reasoning degradation, retrieval fragility, and multimodal misalignment — as principled properties of LLM design and deployment, not empirical artifacts of insufficient scale. This framing directly constrains what model collapse mitigation can achieve: even if every synthetic data quality control measure were perfect, the models generating that data would still hallucinate on infinitely many inputs. The theoretical question is whether contamination can be bounded below some functional threshold rather than eliminated, and whether training lineage provenance can be tracked precisely enough to implement principled filtering.

Practically, the open questions are harder still. There is no established methodology for detecting when a corpus has crossed a collapse threshold. Synthetic data pipelines are economically dominant because they are cheap, fast, and scalable — the incentives all push toward more synthetic content, not less. And as Karpathy's observation about reward hacking illustrates, the collapse risk extends beyond pretraining data into the very evaluation and training signals used to improve models post-deployment.

Model collapse thus sits at the intersection of a mathematical impossibility result (hallucination is unavoidable), an economic trend (synthetic data is now everywhere), and a feedback structure (models train on each other's outputs) — making it one of the few failure modes for which neither scaling nor better architecture provides a principled escape.
