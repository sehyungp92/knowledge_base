### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- RLVR (Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards) is structurally constrained to domains where outputs can be deterministically verified, such as mathematics and competitive programming, making it inapplicable to the vast space of open-ended, subjective, or multi-dimensional tasks.
  - The supply of question–answer pairs with objectively verifiable solutions is finite, imposing a hard ceiling on RLVR scalability even within its native domains.
  - Tasks involving creative writing, emotional intelligence, stylistic control, and humanities-centric reasoning lack ground-truth signals compatible with existing RLVR pipelines, leaving a large portion of real-world LLM use cases outside the reach of RL-based post-training.
- Prior RLHF-style approaches (e.g., learned reward models) do not provide structured, interpretable criteria and are susceptible to reward hacking without the explicit, auditable rubric structure that enables reliable scoring of subjective outputs.

---

### Proposed Approach
- The paper proposes Rubicon (RUBrIC aNchOrs), a rubric-based RL framework that extends RLVR to open-ended tasks by replacing binary verifiable rewards with structured, multi-dimensional rubrics that serve as model-interpretable scoring criteria.
  - Each rubric R is formally defined as a set of K critic dimensions, where each dimension specifies a criterion description, an ordered set of score tiers with quantitative mappings, and a relative weight — unifying general and fine-grained evaluation under a single abstract representation.
  - Reward aggregation goes beyond weighted summation: the framework incorporates veto mechanisms (critical dimension failures nullify all other rewards), saturation-aware aggregation (diminishing returns for single-dimension excellence), pairwise interaction modeling (synergistic/antagonistic dimension effects), and targeted reward shaping (non-linear amplification in high-performance regions to improve gradient discriminability).
- Rubicons are constructed at multiple scopes — dataset-level, task-level, and per-sample — using human experts, LLMs (Qwen3-30B-A3B self-critique and Gemini 2.5 Pro), and hybrid human–LLM collaboration, resulting in over 10,000 rubrics, the largest rubric reward bank reported to date.
- Training follows a two-stage RL protocol to address the "seesaw effect": Stage 1 establishes a constraint-handling foundation using verifiable checks and static multi-dimensional rubrics; Stage 2 targets open-ended and creative tasks using reference-based and instance-specific rubrics generated by stronger agentic workflows.
- An adaptive Reward Hacking Defense Rubric is synthesized empirically from observed failure modes in early training rollouts and integrated as a supervisory veto constraint in all subsequent RL stages, preventing policy collapse into reward-exploiting behaviors.
- Data filtering operates on score distributions: only instances within a calibrated central quantile are retained, excluding trivially easy or noisy samples to maximize learning signal per training step.

### Results & Capabilities
- Rubicon-preview (Qwen3-30B-A3B base) achieves a +5.2% absolute average improvement across seven open-ended and humanities-centric benchmarks (Creative Writing V3, WritingBench, Judgemark V2, EQ-Bench, IFEval, Collie, IFScale) using only ~5,000 training samples.
  - It outperforms DeepSeek-V3-671B — a model more than 22× larger — by +2.4% on the same benchmark suite, demonstrating exceptional token and parameter efficiency.
  - The largest individual gains appear on Judgemark V2 (+13.0%) and EQ-Bench (+6.2%), indicating strongest impact on evaluative reasoning and emotional intelligence tasks.
- Rubric-based RL provides fine-grained stylistic control: models trained with style-anchored rubrics produce responses that are demonstrably more human-like, emotionally expressive, and less formulaic, avoiding the "AI-like" or didactic tone characteristic of base instruction-tuned models.
  - A qualitative case study shows Rubicon generating a vivid, first-person narrative with grounded sensory detail in response to a personal question, while the base Qwen3-30B-A3B deflects by noting it is an AI without personal experience.
- General and reasoning capabilities are largely preserved: Rubicon-preview matches or exceeds the base model on MMLU, HellaSwag, StoryCloze, CoQA, and SocialIQA, and shows unexpected gains on math reasoning — AIME 2024 (+4.17%) and AIME 2025 (+0.83%) — despite rubrics being entirely non-STEM in focus.
- The seesaw effect is empirically confirmed: training exclusively on creativity rubrics improves EQ-Bench (+6.2%) but degrades Collie (−6.0%) and IFEval (−5.9%), while instruction-following-only training shows the inverse pattern, validating the necessity of multi-stage training.

### Implications
- Rubicon demonstrates that the RLVR paradigm can be extended beyond verifiable domains by treating structured rubrics as a substitute for ground-truth signals, potentially unlocking RL-based post-training for the majority of real-world tasks that lack binary correctness criteria.
- The observed token efficiency — large benchmark gains from ~5K samples paired with a large rubric bank — raises a novel hypothesis about post-training scaling laws: rubric quantity and diversity may represent a new axis of scaling that is complementary to, or partially substitutable for, training data volume.
- Style controllability via rubric anchors suggests a new direction for behavioral alignment: rather than optimizing toward a single reward signal, RL training with multi-dimensional rubrics could serve as a principled mechanism for steering model outputs toward specific communicative registers, personas, or cultural norms.

## Key Claims

1. RLVR is inherently constrained by its reliance on question-answer pairs with objectively verifiable solutions, imposing a hard ceiling on scalability.
2. Rubric-based reward enables RL training on open-ended tasks by defining structured, interpretable criteria that capture multi-dimensional aspects of response quality beyond binary correctness.
3. Relying on a single rubric risks reward exploitation, whereas indiscriminately scaling the number of rubrics yields only marginal gains.
4. Rubric-based RL success depends tightly on diversity, granularity, and quantity of rubrics, as well as proper training routine and meticulous data curation.
5. Rubicon-preview (30B-A3B) outperforms DeepSeek-V3-671B by +2.4% on open-ended humanities benchmarks despite being 22x smaller.
6. Rubric-based RL mitigates the 'AI-like' and didactic tone, producing responses with greater human-likeness and emotional expressiveness.
7. Rubric-based RL design follows the principle of evaluative asymmetry: verifying a candidate output should be substantially easier than generating it.

## Capabilities

- Rubric-based RL (Rubicon) can train LLMs on open-ended, subjective tasks — creative writing, emotional intelligence, humanities — by using structured multi-dimensional rubrics as reward signals, extending RLVR beyond verifiable domains for the first time at scale.
- Rubric-based RL enables fine-grained, controllable LLM output style — directing models toward specific narrative voices, emotional registers, and tonal qualities, demonstrably reducing 'AI-like' or didactic tone in favour of human-like, emotionally expressive responses.
- A 30B MoE model trained with rubric-based RL on only 5,000 samples outperforms a 671B dense model on humanities and open-ended benchmarks — demonstrating extreme token efficiency in post-training via rubric diversity rather than data scale.
- Adaptive reward hacking defense via empirically-derived defense rubrics — offline analysis of rollout anomalies yields a Reward Hacking Defense Rubric that, when integrated as a supervisory constraint, substantially stabilises RL training and enables longer, more productive training epochs.

## Limitations

- RLVR is structurally confined to tasks with programmatically verifiable outputs (math, code); the supply of such training data is finite, creating a hard scalability ceiling that blocks expanding RLVR to the vast majority of real-world tasks.
- Joint RL training on tasks with conflicting objectives (e.g. instruction-following vs. creative/empathetic generation) causes systematic 'seesaw' performance trade-offs — improving one capability degrades the other, and a single combined RL run intensifies this conflict.
- Rubric-based RL training on humanities/creative tasks causes notable regression on STEM coding tasks: LiveCodeBench v5 drops 4.34 percentage points (63.77→59.43) and GPQA-Diamond drops 2.65 points (63.00→60.35), suggesting capability interference across knowledge domains.
- Rubric-based RL has no principled theory for optimal rubric design: quantity, diversity, granularity, and quality all critically affect outcomes, but determining the optimal hierarchical rubric structure requires extensive empirical ablation with no systematic guidelines.
- The precise mechanisms behind reward hacking in rubric-based RL remain unknown, making it impossible to systematically prevent or predict exploitation patterns without iterative offline analysis of training rollouts.
- High-quality rubric generation depends on access to very powerful external models (Gemini 2.5 Pro API); weaker self-critique models alone or indiscriminate scaling of rubric quantity yields only marginal gains, creating a dependency on frontier proprietary APIs.
- Training data is drawn from a proprietary 900K+ instance corpus with undisclosed composition; results cannot be fully reproduced or attributed to the methodology alone, as corpus curation choices are a confounding variable.
- Current standardised benchmarks cannot accurately measure open-ended, anthropomorphic LLM abilities — benchmark scores do not consistently reflect human feedback evaluations, leaving rubric-based RL gains partially invisible to automated evaluation.
- Token efficiency observation (5K samples + large rubric bank achieving 671B-level performance) is posed as a speculative question rather than a validated scaling law — the claim is preliminary and may not generalise across tasks, base models, or rubric configurations.
- Rubric-based RL produces a minor but consistent regression on strict instruction-following (IFEval: -1.85%), suggesting that the open-ended creativity rubrics partially erode precise constraint adherence even with staged training.
- How to combine RLVR (verifiable rewards) and rubric-based RL in a single training framework — and how the seesaw effect would manifest in such a combined setup — remains entirely unresolved.

## Bottlenecks

- The 'seesaw effect' in multi-objective RL training blocks the development of models with balanced, simultaneous strengths across instruction-following, creativity, and social/empathetic capabilities — joint RL training on conflicting task types causes systematic performance regression, forcing seque
- No principled rubric design methodology exists for rubric-based RL: success depends critically on rubric diversity, granularity, and quantity, but these must be determined through extensive empirical ablation for every task domain, blocking systematic scaling of rubric-based training.
- Absence of reliable automated benchmarks for open-ended, anthropomorphic LLM abilities — current standardised benchmarks do not capture the capabilities that rubric-based RL improves, forcing reliance on expensive human evaluation and blocking systematic progress measurement.

## Breakthroughs

- Rubric-based RL (Rubicon) extends RLVR to open-ended, subjective task domains — creative writing, emotional intelligence, humanities — using structured multi-dimensional rubrics as reward signals, breaking the hard constraint that RL training requires programmatically verifiable ground truth.
