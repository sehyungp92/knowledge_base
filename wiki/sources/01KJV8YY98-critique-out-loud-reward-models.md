# Critique-out-Loud Reward Models

CLoud reward models bridge the longstanding gap between classic RLHF reward models and LLM-as-a-Judge by preserving the LLM's language modeling head alongside the scalar reward head, enabling models to generate interpretable natural language critiques of responses before predicting a reward score — achieving state-of-the-art pairwise preference classification on RewardBench and Pareto improvements in Best-of-N selection on ArenaHard while opening a path toward compute-adaptive reward models.

**Authors:** Zachary Ankner, Mansheej Paul, Brandon Cui, Jonathan D. Chang, Prithviraj Ammanabraju
**Published:** 2024-08-21
**Type:** paper

---

## Expert Analysis

### Motivation & Prior Limitations

Classic [[themes/reward_modeling|reward models]] used in RLHF are trained as LLM-based classifiers that predict scalar preference scores in a single forward pass. This forces all reasoning about response quality to remain implicit, establishing a hard architectural ceiling on their performance.

The language modeling head of the underlying LLM is discarded in classic reward models, preventing any form of chain-of-thought reasoning about why one response is better than another. The severity of this bottleneck is evidenced empirically: on RewardBench, classic 70B reward models still underperform CLoud 8B models on Chat and Safety categories — scale alone cannot compensate for implicit reasoning constraints.

The [[themes/alignment_methods|LLM-as-a-Judge]] framework demonstrates that explicit reasoning about response quality improves interpretability, but it consistently underperforms classic reward models at pairwise preference classification. It lacks a trained scalar reward head and relies only on ad-hoc rubrics at inference time. Neither paradigm captured both trainable preference modeling and explicit reasoning simultaneously, leaving a structural gap.

---

### Proposed Approach

Critique-out-Loud (CLoud) reward models augment classic reward models by preserving the LLM's language modeling head alongside the scalar reward head. This enables the model to first generate a natural language critique of the assistant's response, then predict a reward conditioned on that critique.

Unlike LLM-as-a-Judge, CLoud maintains a Bradley-Terry trained scalar reward head and does not rely on user-provided rubrics. Unlike prior work conditioning rewards on critiques from external models, CLoud generates its own critiques at inference without needing a larger oracle model at test time.

**Training proceeds in three stages:**

1. SFT the base LLM on oracle critiques generated by Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct
2. Replace oracle critiques in the dataset with critiques sampled from the now-finetuned model, constructing a self-generated dataset
3. Jointly minimize a combined SFT critique loss and Bradley-Terry preference modeling loss on the self-generated dataset — *on-policy training*

The on-policy step is critical. Training the reward head on oracle critiques (off-policy) rather than self-generated ones causes a distribution shift between training and inference that significantly degrades performance: a **5.60 pp drop** for 8B models and **3.03 pp drop** for 70B models in average RewardBench accuracy.

At inference, CLoud optionally supports **self-consistency decoding** by sampling N (critique, reward) pairs and averaging the scalar rewards to marginalize over critique stochasticity, trading added compute for a potentially better reward estimate.

### Results & Capabilities

**RewardBench pairwise preference classification:**

| Model | Improvement over classic RM |
|---|---|
| CLoud 8B | +4.65 pp average across all categories |
| CLoud 70B | +5.84 pp average across all categories |

Notably, the **8B CLoud model surpasses the 70B classic reward model** on Chat and Safety categories — explicit critique reasoning can partially substitute for scale.

**Best-of-N on ArenaHard:**

CLoud produces a strict Pareto improvement over classic reward models at every value of N from 2 to 16. At Best-of-16, CLoud improves win rate by **1.84 pp** (8B) and **0.89 pp** (70B) over classic reward models, evaluated against greedy generations from Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct.

**Self-consistency decoding:**

[[themes/test_time_compute_scaling|Test-time compute scaling]] via self-consistency is narrowly beneficial:

- Improves pairwise accuracy by up to **0.70 pp** (8B) and **0.49 pp** (70B) on RewardBench Reasoning
- Benefit concentrates on short-horizon problems (1–2 reasoning steps)
- Problems with 5+ steps *degrade* in accuracy past 8 critique samples — over-averaging introduces noise on complex responses
- **Zero improvement** in Best-of-N win rate on ArenaHard — the benefit does not generalize to open-ended generation

## Limitations & Open Questions

**Architectural limitations of the prior paradigm.** Classic reward models are structurally incapable of [[themes/chain_of_thought|chain-of-thought reasoning]] — all preference modeling must occur implicitly within a single forward pass. This is not a training data problem or scale problem; it is an architectural constraint.

**Oracle dependency at training time.** CLoud training requires a 405B-parameter frontier model to generate oracle critiques as supervision. This is a major compute and access barrier that concentrates the technique at well-resourced labs. The paper frames this as a limitation but does not evaluate whether smaller oracle models could substitute. Trajectory: improving as capable smaller models emerge, but currently significant.

**Circularity risk.** The same Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct model serves as both the preference labeler and the critique generator across the entire training pipeline. Potential Goodhart-style bias entrenchment — where the reward model learns to prefer outputs that satisfy the oracle's implicit preferences rather than human preferences — is not discussed or evaluated.

**Inference latency unquantified.** CLoud reward models generate a full natural language critique before every reward prediction. The paper never reports this overhead. For RLHF pipelines processing millions of responses, this is a practically significant omission.

**Self-consistency is task-type contingent and unpredictable.** Non-reasoning categories (chat, safety) show zero benefit and potentially negative effects from self-consistency. Effective use requires knowing the task distribution in advance — a constraint that may not hold in diverse real-world deployments.

**Narrow evaluation scope.** All experiments use Llama-3 8B and 70B on a single training dataset (UltraLlama). Generalizability to other architectures, scales, training distributions, and non-English tasks is entirely unvalidated.

## Key Claims

1. Classic reward models cannot explicitly reason about response quality — all reasoning must occur implicitly within a single forward pass without generating reasoning traces.
2. CLoud improves RewardBench pairwise accuracy by **4.65 pp** (8B) and **5.84 pp** (70B) over classic reward models.
3. CLoud produces a Pareto improvement in Best-of-N win rate on ArenaHard at both 8B and 70B scales.
4. At Best-of-16, CLoud improves ArenaHard win rate by **1.84 pp** (8B) and **0.89 pp** (70B).
5. On-policy training on self-generated critiques is essential — off-policy training on oracle critiques causes 5.60 pp (8B) and 3.03 pp (70B) performance drops.
6. Self-consistency improves reasoning accuracy by up to **0.70 pp** (8B) and **0.49 pp** (70B) but provides zero improvement in Best-of-N on ArenaHard.
7. The 8B CLoud model outperforms the 70B classic reward model on chat and safety categories.
8. LLM-as-a-Judge systematically underperforms classic reward models at pairwise preference classification despite its interpretability advantages.
9. Self-consistency degrades accuracy on long-horizon reasoning problems (7+ steps) past 8 critique samples.
10. Oracle critiques are generated by Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct as a proxy for human critiques.

## Landscape Position

### What This Advances

CLoud establishes that the classic/judge dichotomy is a false constraint. The paradigm shift — from "reward model OR generative judge" to "reward model WITH generative critique" — is orthogonal to the choice of preference modeling objective (Bradley-Terry, Plackett-Luce, contrastive, multi-objective). CLoud can in principle be composed with any of these for further gains.

The demonstration that [[themes/test_time_compute_scaling|test-time compute scaling]] via self-consistency selectively improves reward quality connects reward modeling to the broader scaling literature. This opens a path toward compute-adaptive reward models that allocate more inference budget to harder or more ambiguous inputs — though the current finding that this only works for short-horizon reasoning problems is a concrete constraint on its applicability.

By producing interpretable natural language critiques alongside scalar scores, CLoud makes reward model behavior auditable — a step toward [[themes/alignment_and_safety|alignment and safety]] goals where understanding *why* a reward model prefers certain responses matters as much as the preference itself.

### Bottlenecks Addressed

- **Implicit reasoning ceiling in classic RMs** — CLoud directly resolves the architectural constraint by adding a language modeling head. The bottleneck is not fully eliminated (oracle dependency remains), but the architectural barrier is removed.
- **Fixed inference compute for reward models** — CLoud introduces the first practical mechanism to trade compute for reward quality (self-consistency), though with significant caveats about task distribution.

### Bottlenecks Introduced / Remaining

- **Oracle dependency for critique supervision** — democratization of critique-based reward modeling remains blocked by frontier model access requirements. Horizon: 1–2 years.
- **Unpredictable inference compute benefits** — the task-type contingency of self-consistency makes compute-adaptive deployment complex without a-priori task distribution knowledge.
