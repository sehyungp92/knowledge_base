# Vertical AI Shows Potential to Dwarf Legacy SaaS | State of the Cloud 2024

> This source argues that Vertical AI will dramatically exceed the market size of legacy Vertical SaaS by productizing high-value language-intensive service tasks across professional industries. Drawing on portfolio company comparisons (notably EvenUp vs. Litify in personal injury law), it maps three emerging business models (copilots, agents, AI-enabled services), challenges the conventional view that services businesses cannot achieve venture-scale economics, and identifies incumbent response patterns and multimodal capability maturity as key uncertainties for new entrants.

**Authors:** Bessemer Venture Partners
**Published:** 2024-06-20
**Type:** Video

---

## Core Argument

The central claim is that Vertical AI companies are not competing for a slice of existing SaaS spend; they are competing for a far larger pool: the services spend that incumbents never could productize. Because language-heavy professional tasks were historically too unstructured for traditional software, entire verticals remained dominated by expensive human labor. AI dissolves that barrier, converting third-party outsource spend into software-like products with software-like economics.

The magnitude is illustrated by a direct ACV comparison: personal injury law firms are paying [[entities/evenup|EvenUp]] (an AI demand letter drafting service) approximately the same as they pay Litify (their core system-of-record workflow software), despite EvenUp being a single-purpose, newer product at an earlier stage of maturity. If this ratio holds across verticals, the effective TAM may be roughly 2x previous estimates, because AI inserts a second layer of spend on top of existing core workflow software rather than cannibalizing it.

---

## Three Business Models

The source distinguishes three discrete waves, not merely a spectrum:

**1. Copilots** (first wave, ~2023): Augment an existing worker rather than replace them. The worker remains in the loop; the AI accelerates throughput, parsing complexity, or decision quality. Sixfold AI (insurance underwriting) is cited as the portfolio exemplar. These products face the clearest incumbent threat, because vertical software players have natural motives to absorb copilot functionality.

**2. Agents** (current emerging wave): Autonomous execution of tasks previously done by a human employee, replacing internal headcount directly. Many are multimodal, spanning voice, text, and coding modalities.

**3. AI-Enabled Services** (productized outsource spend): The most structurally novel category. Rather than competing with software, these companies compete with the law firm associate, the medical transcription vendor, or the insurance adjustment bureau. Because they are benchmarked against labor costs rather than software costs, they can command pricing that surprises buyers accustomed to SaaS ACVs.

---

## Why AI-Enabled Services Overturn Prior Assumptions

Venture capital had long treated services businesses as structurally unattractive on three grounds: capped organic growth (constrained by hiring), low gross margins (30-50% in the best case), and undifferentiated delivery (human labor arbitrage). The source argues each assumption inverts for AI-enabled services:

- **Growth**: 100%+ year-over-year, because quality and cost advantages are decisive and the business is not constrained by headcount.
- **Gross margins**: 60%+ and improving, because the primary cost is model API usage, a fraction of labor costs. What remains in the cost base (workflow software, customer success, last-mile operations) is largely fixed and scalable.
- **Differentiation**: High, because delivering production-grade output requires deep vertical customization: proprietary workflows, domain expertise, exception-handling logic, and regulatory compliance layers. A thin wrapper around a generic LLM is insufficient and not defensible.

## Limitations and Open Questions

Several significant constraints are surfaced, and they are worth holding alongside the optimistic framing:

**Irreducible human residual.** Professional service workflows retain a ~10% tail of corner cases, exception handling, regulatory judgment, and liability scenarios that current AI cannot reliably handle. This tail is not shrinking quickly; it represents structural limits on full automation for high-stakes verticals.

**Customization depth required.** Competitive differentiation demands substantial investment in vertical-specific workflows, domain knowledge encoding, and last-mile operations. A generic LLM integration does not suffice. This creates a high build cost and makes the "move fast" advantage temporary without continued investment.

**Text modality dominance.** The first wave of AI-enabled services is predominantly text-based (document ingestion, document generation). Multimodal end-to-end automation (voice, video, structured data, physical workflow) remains emerging and not yet dominant, creating a ceiling on which verticals can be fully productized today.

**Distribution disadvantage for new entrants.** Incumbents possess customer relationships, brand equity, established sales forces, and capital. New entrants must overcome these advantages during the window before incumbents respond. The source notes the window exists but does not guarantee its duration.

**Incumbent response uncertainty.** Historical precedent from the on-premise-to-cloud transition is mixed: some incumbents successfully made the transition, others were displaced. The distribution of outcomes is wide and vertical-specific, making defensibility predictions unreliable at the portfolio level.

## Landscape Signals

### Breakthroughs

**Venture-scale economics proven for services delivery.** AI-enabled services businesses operating at 60%+ gross margins with 100%+ growth rates overturn the long-standing VC assumption that services cannot achieve software returns. This is not a projection; portfolio companies are exhibiting it now.

**Previously inaccessible professional verticals reopened.** Verticals dominated by language-intensive labor had no strong software incumbents precisely because software could not address them. AI unlocks these verticals simultaneously for investment and for productization, expanding the investable universe in ways the on-premise-to-cloud transition did not.

### Active Bottlenecks

**Multimodal capability maturity** is a gating constraint on full end-to-end professional workflow automation. The source explicitly frames multimodality as the next wave, with the current state dominated by text. Resolution horizon: 1-2 years.

**Go-to-market and distribution infrastructure** limits how quickly new vertical AI entrants can capture market share against incumbents with established sales motions. This is a structural disadvantage that compounds over time if not addressed early.

**Incumbent response timing** remains the most consequential and least predictable variable. Incumbents who move fast could neutralize copilot-category moats; incumbents who move slowly create a durable window. The historical record from the cloud transition provides range but not central estimate.

## Key Claims

1. Vertical AI companies are already commanding ~80% of the ACV of existing vertical software companies, despite being at earlier stages of maturity.
2. TAMs for professional service verticals may be 2x previously estimated, because AI inserts a second spending layer rather than displacing existing software spend.
3. AI-enabled services compete with labor budgets, not software budgets, enabling pricing that does not require creating new customer budget.
4. AI-enabled services remove labor constraints and allow customer businesses to expand output and take on more work rather than simply reducing headcount.
5. Three business models are emerging: copilots (augment), agents (replace), AI-enabled services (productize outsourced spend).
6. AI-enabled services businesses are growing 100%+ year-over-year at 60%+ gross margins, with model costs representing a small fraction of cost basis.
7. Defensibility requires deep customization: custom workflows, domain expertise, exception handling, and last-mile operations.
8. Many new AI-accessible verticals lacked strong software incumbents because the tasks were language-heavy and unsuited to prior-generation software.
9. Incumbents are more likely to partner with or acquire vertical AI companies than to directly compete, based on both current signals and cloud-transition precedent.
10. The first vertical AI IPO is anticipated within 3 years; at least 5 companies are expected to reach $100M+ ARR within 2-3 years.
