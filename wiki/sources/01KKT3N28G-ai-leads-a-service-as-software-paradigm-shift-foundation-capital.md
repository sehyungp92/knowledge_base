## Briefing

**Foundation Capital argues that AI is inverting the SaaS model by shifting outcome-responsibility from customers to vendors — a transition from selling tools to delivering results — creating a $4.6 trillion opportunity that dwarfs the entire enterprise software market by targeting services and labor budgets that SaaS never could reach.** The core claim is structural: AI agents don't just boost productivity within existing workflows, they absorb whole job categories, making the addressable market for AI companies many multiples larger than even the most successful traditional SaaS incumbents.

### Key Takeaways
1. **SaaS flips to "Service as Software"** — In the new model, the AI vendor owns the outcome (e.g., "tax services" instead of QuickBooks), shifting liability and value capture fundamentally toward the software provider.
2. **$4.6T opportunity splits evenly** — ~$2.3T in targeted white-collar salaries (sales & marketing, software engineering, security, HR) plus ~$2.3T in outsourced IT and business process services represent the full disruption surface.
3. **The Salesforce gap reveals the scale** — Salesforce earns $35B/year while global sales and marketing salaries total $1.1T, meaning AI targeting the salary budget has ~30x more headroom than the SaaS incumbent.
4. **Outcome-based pricing resolves the SaaS growth ceiling** — Charging per qualified lead or signed customer rather than per seat removes vendor incentive to limit usage and lets revenue scale directly with customer value delivered.
5. **More usage = better AI** — Unlike traditional SaaS, AI vendors actively want high usage because usage generates training data, creating a compounding competitive advantage tied to deployment scale.
6. **Agentic AI is the mechanism** — LLM-powered agents capable of reading unstructured content, setting priorities, and autonomous decision-making are what enables the jump from workflow automation to job execution.
7. **Software engineering is a ~$1T salary target** — With 30M+ engineers globally, AI tools like Cursor (autocomplete, auto-debug) represent an early wedge into a massive labor budget that traditional dev tooling barely touched.
8. **Cybersecurity is structurally broken for humans** — Chronic understaffing and alert overload make the security analyst role an ideal target for AI virtual analysts; AirMDR is delivering this at a fraction of cost for SMBs.
9. **SaaS coexists rather than dies** — Multiple business models can thrive simultaneously; some customers will keep functions in-house, others will outsource, and AI companies can complement or displace incumbents depending on the vertical.
10. **Human-in-the-loop persists even in deep automation** — Even in rote operations like invoice processing, humans retain workflow design authority and spot-check responsibility, limiting full displacement in practice.

### The Structural Inversion: From Tool Access to Outcome Ownership

- Traditional SaaS sells access to a platform; the customer bears responsibility for using it to achieve results.
  - Example: QuickBooks provides accounting software — the customer (or their accountant) still does the work.
  - In the new model, the AI company sells the outcome directly: **"instead of QuickBooks, you offer tax services — conducted by an AI accountant."**
- This inversion moves the vendor up the value stack from tool provider to service provider, capturing margin previously left with human labor.
- The phrase Foundation Capital uses — "software as autonomous service" — emphasizes that the software acts, not just assists.
- This is framed as "Automation 2.0": past waves automated efficiency for humans; this wave makes **bots into brains** that operate independently.

---

### The $4.6 Trillion Market Map

- The total addressable market is constructed from two buckets of approximately equal size:
  - **$2.3T in salaries**: sales & marketing, software engineering, cybersecurity, and HR workers globally whose functions AI can increasingly perform.
  - **$2.3T in outsourced services**: IT services ($1.5T in 2024, growing to $2.3T by 2028 per Gartner) and business process services ($303B by 2027 per Gartner).
- By comparison, the entire public market cap of enterprise software exceeds $1T — meaning the services disruption opportunity is multiple times larger than the existing SaaS sector's total value.
- The Salesforce comparison makes the asymmetry visceral: **$35B annual revenue (Salesforce) vs. $1.1T in global sales and marketing salaries** — AI targeting that salary base has ~30x the theoretical ceiling.
- India's IT services export economy ($240B/year) illustrates how deeply embedded outsourced service labor is in enterprise operations — and how large the displacement target is.
- Gartner projects AI and generative AI will inject **$28 billion into the business process services market alone over four years**, an early indicator of the disruption trajectory.

### Why Agentic AI Crosses the Threshold Prior Automation Could Not

- Earlier automation was bounded: tools helped humans go faster, but humans remained in the loop for judgment, context, and unstructured decisions.
- **LLM-powered agents unlock the missing capabilities**: reading and interpreting unstructured content, generating output, setting priorities, directing tasks — all skills that describe a skilled human service worker.
- The key qualitative difference: agents are capable of **complex decision-making and autonomy**, meaning software no longer stops at organizing a workflow — it executes the job.
- Business process services are the clearest target because they involve "repetitive, rule-based tasks" combined with judgment about context and intent — precisely the combination LLMs handle.

## Capabilities

- LLM-powered AI agents can read and interpret both structured and unstructured content, generate output, set priorities, and direct tasks autonomously — performing work previously requiring skilled human judgment across sales, HR, DevOps, and security functions
- AI virtual security analyst (AirMDR) can autonomously detect and respond to attacks, review and mitigate vulnerabilities, and provide security coverage for SMBs at a fraction of traditional analyst cost
- AI DevOps on-call agents can autonomously resolve software incidents, reduce mean time to repair (MTTR), triage technical support tickets, and reduce unnecessary escalations without human intervention
- AI systems can handle end-to-end business process services — including recruiting calls, answering buyer questions, data entry, and reading faxes — replacing what were previously human-staffed BPO roles
- AI coding tools (Cursor) provide autocomplete-level code generation, third-party library ingestion, and auto-debugging, materially accelerating developer workflows
- Outcome-based AI service delivery enables vendors to charge per qualified opportunity, signed customer, or resolved incident rather than per seat — aligning cost with delivered business value

## Limitations

- AI cannot replace the relationship-building, in-person social, and trust-based dimensions of human service work — the paradigmatic example being client dinner meetings in sales
- AI still requires human oversight and spot-checking even for the most repetitive, rule-based operations like invoice processing — full human-out-of-loop automation is not yet trusted or viable across enterprise tasks
- AI agents execute within human-designed workflows but cannot autonomously design enterprise workflows themselves — workflow design remains a human responsibility
- Whether AI will displace SaaS incumbents or merely complement them remains unresolved — the competitive outcome is hedged even in optimistic VC framing
- The $4.6 trillion opportunity is framed as a 'next five years' projection — the vast majority of the claimed addressable market is aspirational and not currently being captured by AI systems
- No reliability, accuracy, or failure rate metrics are provided for any of the named AI service companies — conspicuous absence of quality evidence for production deployments
- Security, privacy, and data governance risks of AI agents handling sensitive enterprise data (customer CRM records, medical faxes, financial documents) are entirely absent from the analysis
- Outcome-based pricing requires reliable instrumentation to measure AI-delivered business outcomes — but defining, attributing, and auditing outcomes like 'qualified opportunities' or 'resolved incidents' remains technically unsolved
- AI agents currently address specific narrow task categories (email outbound, fax reading, ticket triage) rather than the full breadth of complex judgment-intensive service work — general-purpose enterprise AI agents replacing entire job functions remain aspirational

## Bottlenecks

- Enterprise willingness to accept human-out-of-loop AI service delivery is a structural adoption bottleneck — even where AI is technically capable, organizational risk tolerance requires human oversight, limiting the realized cost savings of the service-as-software model
- Incumbent SaaS pricing and seat-based contracting norms create friction for outcome-based AI service pricing adoption — buyers and procurement systems are not structured to purchase services by outcome, blocking business model transition
- Chronic understaffing of security analyst roles creates both an opportunity and a validation bottleneck — AI security agents cannot be properly evaluated or deployed without skilled human analysts to calibrate them against

## Breakthroughs

- Agentic AI enables a structural shift from software-as-a-service (tool + human labor) to service-as-software (AI delivers the outcome) — redefining the unit of enterprise software value from workflow enablement to outcome delivery
