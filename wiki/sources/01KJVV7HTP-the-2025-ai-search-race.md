# The 2025 AI Search Race

**Authors:** 
**Published:** 2024-12-09 00:00:00
**Type:** video

## Analysis

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Bv36kG4bR3w

- Search monopoly, particularly around Google, may be coming to an end. 
	- Google controls ~90% of the market, but their grip is slipping. While the likes of ChatGPT and Perplexity are coming for them, Google faces a bit of an innovator's dilemma. 
- The search market is one of the largest out there. Virtually anyone with the internet uses search. 
	- To put some numbers around it, Google are the biggest game in town, and they are approaching ~$200 billion of annual revenue. They are highly profitable, still growing double digits. 
	- Microsoft Bing, which has been the number two player for a long time, has mid-single digit market share, so quite small. They have ~$12 billion of revenue. 
- To provide more context into Google’s position, in particular the mounting legal pressure, earlier this year, Google was declared a monopoly. The court ruled that they were spending billions of dollars to phone manufacturers – tens of billions of dollars in the case of Apple – to establish themselves as the default search engine on all these phone manufacturers, which is anticompetitive, preventing their competitors from gaining share in the marketplace. 
- The exciting technological change is of course around AI. The market has been dominated by Google for close to 25 years at this point, and as a monopoly, they have had limited incentive to innovate until now. 
	- The Google experience as it is today, consists of a long list of links. And the first few are sponsored, i.e., ads. And, as a user, when making a query on Google, you need to sift through the information on that site and find the answer that you are looking for. That's actually a quite a long process.
- Instead, with GenAI search providers, e.g., ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Poe or chatbots on Character AI, you can get an answer immediately, offering a much better user experience. 
- People are definitely moving over, behind which there are 4 main reasons: 
	- One is the aforementioned shift from links to answers. 
	- A second one would be how the answers feel personalised, interactive, and conversational. 
		- The average query on one of these new services has a follow-up question. So it's not just about that initial engagement, but also about the ongoing conversation. 
	- The third difference is about how these new services engage with complex queries. 
		- The average query on Perplexity is 10-11 words, while the average search on Google is 2-3 keywords. Their ability to leverage that information and get you what you want is much higher, versus just getting a list of links on Google. 
		- One of those links might have one side of the debate and another link may have the other side, but may never make it to that second side. On Perplexity or ChatGPT, they are going to synthesise both sides and give both perspectives. 
	- And then the fourth, they are not cluttered with ads, which may change over time. 
- Putting it all together, it should be no surprise that these AI native services are gaining share and growing rapidly.
	- For 10% of consumers, they're now referring to ChatGPT as their search engine of choice. 
	- Perplexity queries are growing 25-30% month over month. And I saw a survey last week that 
	- 60% of consumers for purchase decisions in the last 30 days used a chatbot. 
- It is perhaps not surprising that the last wave of search engines were not verticalised and the next wave may also result in a winner-take-most dynamic. Search is quite a monopolistic market, and all about distribution. Google has an incredible brand, and as the default search engine on most browsers and devices, they have incredible direct traffic. 
- It's important to note that search engines definitely benefit from network effects. More users coming to Google provides more data around preferences. 
	- For Google, that means the next search has more relevance and brings more users. 
	- More users also means more advertisers, more profit dollars that you can invest back in the service. 
- One framework for thinking about how search could fragment or verticalise is to just look at why vertical software does beat horizontal software in some cases. Typically, the vertical requires a specific user interface, proprietary data, features, workflows, compliance, etc. 
	- That's why Veeva in life sciences and pharma can beat Salesforce in horizontal CRM. 
- For the average query on ChatGPT, Perplexity or Google, you do not need a vertical specific application. But for deep domain research, that may be a world where standalone apps can thrive. 
	- For example, Hebbia have a unique interface. It looks like a spreadsheet, which is native to financial services and their customers. It brings in public filings, earnings, transcripts, research, but also private data, which can be queried or used to populate a meeting agenda
		- When it comes to vertical search or vertical apps, it's not just about the search, it's about everything around the search. 
- Some consolidation should be expected in general purpose search because of distribution and network effects. Google won the last time around in part because of their page rank algorithm which produced superior results. It was simple and had a minimalist UI. In contrast, Ask Jeeves, Altavista, they had tons of ads with cluttered links on the homepage. 
	- The irony is that today what we're all complaining about with Google is that their pages are cluttered with ads on the results, and that creates the opportunity for these new search engines. 
- Search historically has been thought of as a consumer product and for good reason. But professionals also make a lot of searches at work, and there is a category of enterprise search in a market that has existed, which can be thought of that as querying Box, Dropbox, Salesforce, all in one.

## Capabilities

- Conversational search providing direct answers instead of link lists, with follow-up interaction
- Complex natural language query understanding and multi-perspective synthesis
- Vertical domain-specific AI search with proprietary data integration and domain-optimized UX
- Hybrid search combining web data with proprietary enterprise information

## Limitations

- AI search services lack entrenched brand recognition, default OS/device placement, and the reinforcing network effects of incumbent search monopoly
- Current AI search services depend on subscription revenue model rather than ads, requiring users to pay for what was previously free
- Quality, accuracy, factuality, and hallucination characteristics of AI search results not discussed or addressed despite being known limitations of LLMs
- Inference latency, scalability, and cost-per-query challenges not addressed despite being critical constraints at global scale
- Vertical search requires proprietary domain data, custom interfaces, and compliance workflows—cannot be achieved through general-purpose foundation models
- User base of AI search services restricted to high-income, educated, early-adopter segments; not yet mainstream

## Bottlenecks

- Distribution lock-in via OS/device defaults and reinforcing network effects of incumbent search monopoly blocking new entrants
- Lack of ad integration and monetization infrastructure for AI search, forcing reliance on subscription model unsustainable at scale
- Per-query inference cost of LLM-based search orders of magnitude higher than indexed keyword search, limiting scalability

## Breakthroughs

- AI-native conversational search has emerged as a viable and rapidly-adopted alternative to link-based search, directly challenging Google's 25-year monopoly
