# Elad Gil on AI, Crypto, and What's Next

Investor and entrepreneur Elad Gil maps the current AI landscape through the lens of startup formation strategy, arguing that the market is structurally inverted — oversaturated with infrastructure and starved of applications — and that founders are making a systematic mistake by targeting hard problems too early. The source offers a practitioner's framework for navigating technology waves alongside pointed assessments of where AI's promise is overstated (drug discovery) and undersold (healthcare services, consumer applications, global equity).

**Authors:** Elad Gil
**Published:** 2023-12-12
**Type:** video

---

## The Four-Layer AI Stack

[[themes/ai_business_and_economics|AI Business & Economics]] Gil organizes the AI landscape into four layers: semiconductors, foundation models, tooling, and applications. The conventional wisdom from prior technology waves holds that infrastructure captures 30–40% of value while applications take the rest — and that both layers are attractive, with infrastructure being more predictable.

The current situation inverts this. The market is **highly saturated with infrastructure companies** and has produced relatively few application companies. Gil attributes this to the early stage of the wave: developer-centric products naturally precede user-centric ones, and the AI field was effectively academic until very recently. The flip into something with obvious end-user value has only just happened.

> "We are in this odd situation where it's almost the opposite, where we're highly saturated with infrastructure companies, and there aren't actually that many app companies in existence."

Notable tooling-layer activity includes companies like Braintrust, which offers AI evaluations, data management, and model comparison platforms — infrastructure for the infrastructure.

## Generative AI as a Discontinuity

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI & SaaS Disruption]] Gil makes an explicit claim that generative AI is **not** an incremental continuation of the ML curve through CNNs, RNNs, and GANs — it is a technology discontinuity offering fundamentally new capabilities, accessible via API, that enable things previously impossible. This framing matters for founders: discontinuities open windows that don't stay open.

The implications for [[themes/startup_and_investment|startup strategy]] are direct. Early in any technology wave, easy opportunities are abundant. Founders who skip past them to pursue hard problems are making a timing error — the hard problems will still be there in five years, when the easy ground is saturated and the underlying technology has matured enough to tackle them.

> "One of the mistakes I think people are making right now in AI when they start companies is they go after that super, super hard thing. Wait five years — when things are saturated, you can go do that."

## Application Opportunities: B2B and B2C

### Consumer

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI]] Gil sees enormous consumer opportunity being underexplored. Legal is an example: Harvey has captured B2B legal attention, but consumer legal — AI that reviews your employment agreement, mortgage, or lease — is largely untouched. Character.ai is cited as an early signal in social and communication products.

The social angle is particularly interesting. Over the last decade, social companies converged on **media** (content generation, likes, shares) rather than **networks** (identity, relationships, trust). Gil argues no one has seriously re-experimented with the network side. With generative AI providing new content formats and self-expression tools (Midjourney, Pika), the conditions for network-first social products may now exist.

Facebook is offered as a counterfactual: it had trusted identity, a friend graph, and deep personal data. It could have become a financial services or healthcare platform — as Apple is now beginning to do — but chose the media path instead.

### B2B: Services to Software

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI & SaaS Disruption]] The global professional services market — consulting, legal, accounting — is approximately $5 trillion. Gil's thesis is that generative AI will convert a meaningful portion of that **services revenue into SaaS revenue**. This is one of the highest-conviction structural claims in the source.

## Healthcare: High Confidence

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI]] Gil is bullish on AI in healthcare services specifically because so much of healthcare is manual labor: billing, insurance claim reconciliation, physician note documentation. These tasks are well-defined, high-volume, and currently expensive to staff. AI's fit is direct.

The broader claim is stronger: Gil describes AI as the **single biggest driver of global equity in education and healthcare**, framing it as a civilizational-scale technology rather than a market opportunity.

**Capability (narrow production):** AI can automate healthcare services tasks including billing, insurance claim reconciliation, and physician note documentation.

**Limitation (significant):** The automation is available but the market structure — fragmented payers, regulatory complexity, incumbent workflows — creates friction that slows deployment even where the technology works.

---

## EdTech: Structurally Hard

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI]] The US K-12 edtech market is historically resistant to software: school districts can't afford it, teachers resist it, and parents won't pay for it. This is a **willingness-to-pay problem**, not a capability problem.

AI may change the equation by enabling a different product entirely: a lifelong personalized learning agent that tutors a child from age three through adulthood. This shifts the value proposition from institutional procurement (broken) to parental or individual purchase (potentially viable). But this remains at demo maturity — no such product exists at scale.

**Limitation (blocking):** EdTech in the US faces a fundamental market WTP problem; end users (students/parents/schools) cannot or will not pay for AI-powered tutoring at present.

## Drug Discovery: Skepticism

[[themes/startup_and_investment|Startup & Investment]] Despite enormous capital inflows, Gil is explicitly skeptical about AI in drug development. He acknowledges he could be wrong, but the technical applicability and commercial viability remain unproven. This is notable because the sector attracts outsized attention.

The biotech landscape has deeper structural problems beyond AI: the global biopharma market cap — including all public and private companies — equals roughly only 3–4 of the largest technology companies by market cap. Gil attributes this partly to distorted founding structures: VC-driven incubations rather than founder-led companies, hired-gun operators with anti-dilution clauses, and R&D pipelines shaped by large pharma's narrow focus on cancer, cardiovascular, and select neurological conditions.

> "You end up with these very screwed up market structures and founding models... they are often built to flip into the arms of other Pharma or biotech companies."

**Bottleneck:** Uncertain technical applicability and commercial viability of AI in drug discovery despite billions in capital deployment. Resolution horizon: 3–5 years.

## Startup Formation Heuristics

[[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|Startup Formation & GTM]] [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC & Startup Ecosystem]]

Gil offers a three-part framework for choosing a market:

1. **Unique insight** — do you understand a market segment better than most?
2. **Technology or regulatory shift** — is there a 'why now'? Examples: generative AI enabling new information synthesis; regulations creating mandated demand (Samsara's growth traced to in-cab camera regulations for truckers; Anduril's rise tied to sensor networks and drone proliferation).
3. **Build what you or your friends genuinely want** — often underrated.

On learning as a founder: hiring people who are ahead of you on the capability curve is dramatically underrated. The Bill Gates model — repeatedly hiring a COO who could teach him what he needed to know at the next scale level — is offered as a paradigm.

---

## Agentic AI: Years Away, But the Backlog is Real

[[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Vertical AI]] Gil places genuinely agentic AI systems — multi-step autonomous task completion, persistent AI assistants — many years out. The foundational models may be in place, but the engineering work to build reliable agent infrastructure is substantial and largely undone.

This frames the current moment: foundation models have arrived, but the agentic applications they enable are not yet ready. Founders building for an agentic world are early; founders building for today's capabilities have abundant low-hanging fruit available.

**Bottleneck:** Agentic AI systems lack engineering maturity; multi-step task automation remains incomplete despite foundational model capabilities. Blocking: persistent AI assistants, embodied agents, autonomous workflows. Horizon: 5+ years.

## Open Questions

- Will the app layer of AI produce the 60–70% of value that historical tech waves suggest, or will model commoditization compress margins before applications can capture them?
- Can consumer legal, healthcare navigation, or lifelong tutoring agents find sustainable monetization outside institutional procurement?
- Is the US K-12 WTP problem solvable by changing the buyer (individual vs. institution), or does it require a regulatory catalyst similar to Samsara's in-cab camera moment?
- How much of the biotech structural dysfunction is addressable by AI, versus requiring changes to founding models and VC incentives?
- Will social-network-first products re-emerge, or has the media model become so entrenched that new entrants default to it regardless of technical capabilities?
