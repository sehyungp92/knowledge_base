### Motivation & Prior Limitations
RLVR was widely believed to enable LLMs to autonomously develop novel reasoning patterns — including enumeration, self-reflection, and iterative refinement — that surpass the capabilities of their base models, analogous to how traditional RL agents discover new strategies from scratch in environments like Go or Atari.
- Standard evaluation metrics (greedy decoding, nucleus sampling) only capture average-case behavior and systematically underestimate a model's true reasoning potential, masking whether capability gains from RLVR are genuinely novel or merely redistributions of existing capacity.
  - A model that fails a hard problem after one attempt may still be capable of solving it given more attempts; pass@1 is insufficient to distinguish "cannot solve" from "unlikely to sample the correct path."
- The analogy between traditional RL and RLVR is structurally misleading: traditional RL begins from scratch in bounded action spaces, while RLVR begins from a pretrained model with a prior over an exponentially larger language space, fundamentally constraining the exploration dynamics.

---

### Proposed Approach
This paper does not propose a new training method; instead it conducts a systematic empirical investigation into the reasoning capability boundaries of RLVR-trained LLMs, using pass@k at large k values as the primary evaluation lens to distinguish genuine capability expansion from improved sampling efficiency.
- Pass@k (borrowed from code generation evaluation) counts a problem as solved if any of k sampled outputs is correct, providing a measure of the model's maximum potential coverage over a problem set rather than its average-case behavior.
  - By comparing pass@k curves of base models vs. RLVR-trained models across a sweep of k values (up to 1024), the methodology directly tests whether RLVR-trained models can solve problems that base models fundamentally cannot.
  - Experiments span multiple LLM families (Qwen2.5-7B/14B/32B, LLaMA-3.1-8B, Qwen2.5-VL-7B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-14B), six RL algorithms (PPO, GRPO, Reinforce++, RLOO, ReMax, DAPO), and three task domains (mathematics, code generation, visual reasoning), with perplexity analysis and accuracy distribution analysis as supporting diagnostics.

---

### Results & Capabilities
RLVR-trained models consistently outperform their base models at small k (e.g., k=1) but are surpassed by their own base models as k grows large, across all benchmarks, model families, and RL algorithms tested.
- On the Minerva benchmark with Qwen2.5-32B, the base model outperforms its RLVR-trained counterpart by approximately 9% at k=128, meaning the base model can solve ~9% more problems when given sufficient sampling budget.
- As RLVR training progresses (measured at steps 150, 300, 450 of GRPO), pass@1 on the training set improves from 26.1 to 42.5, but pass@256 simultaneously decreases, indicating a tightening of the reasoning boundary as training continues.

Perplexity analysis reveals that reasoning paths generated by RLVR-trained models are already present in the base model's output distribution, confirming that RLVR sharpens the sampling distribution rather than expanding it.
- The distribution of PPLBase(YRL) closely matches the lower (more likely) portion of PPLBase(YBase), and PPLBase(YRL) progressively decreases as RL training steps increase, indicating the RL model's outputs become increasingly typical of the base model's prior.
- Solvable-problem coverage analysis on AIME24 shows that 63.3% of problems are solvable by both models, 13.3% are solvable only by the base model, and 0.0% are solvable only by the RLVR model — the RLVR-solved set is effectively a strict subset of the base-model-solved set.

All six RLVR algorithms (PPO, GRPO, Reinforce++, RLOO, ReMax, DAPO) perform similarly and remain far from optimal relative to the base model upper bound.
- The Sampling Efficiency Gap (ΔSE), defined as the difference between RL pass@1 and base model pass@256, ranges only from 42.6 (RLOO) to 43.9 (GRPO) on the in-domain test set, showing negligible differentiation between algorithms while remaining consistently above 40 points from the upper bound.

Distillation, by contrast, genuinely expands the reasoning boundary beyond the base model.
- DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (distilled from DeepSeek-R1 into Qwen2.5-Math-7B) shows a pass@k curve that is consistently and significantly above the base model across all k values on Minerva, unlike both RLVR and instruction-tuned variants which remain bounded by or below the base model.

The same pattern holds for near-frontier models: Magistral-Medium (trained with pure RL from Mistral-Medium-3) shows significant gains over its base at k=1 (solving ~7 more AIME24 problems and ~8 more AIME25 problems), but the gap narrows steadily as k increases toward 1024.

### Implications
Current RLVR methods function primarily as sampling efficiency optimizers — they make the model more likely to produce correct outputs it was already capable of generating — rather than as mechanisms for discovering genuinely novel reasoning strategies, which fundamentally challenges the narrative that RLVR drives self-improvement toward superhuman reasoning.
- This reframes the value of RLVR for test-time compute scaling: higher pass@1 from RLVR is largely equivalent to more sampling from the base model, raising questions about whether investment should shift toward improving base model priors (via pretraining or distillation) rather than post-training RL.

## Key Claims

1. RLVR-trained models outperform their base models at small k (e.g., k=1), but base models consistently surpass RLVR-trained models at large k across all benchmarks and model families.
2. Current RLVR training does not elicit fundamentally new reasoning patterns in LLMs; all reasoning paths exploited by RLVR models already exist within the base model's sampling distribution.
3. As RLVR training progresses, the reasoning capability boundary of LLMs narrows — average pass@1 improves but coverage of solvable problems (pass@256) decreases.
4. Six popular RLVR algorithms (PPO, GRPO, Reinforce++, RLOO, ReMax, DAPO) perform similarly and all remain far from optimal in leveraging the potential of the base model.
5. Distillation genuinely expands the model's reasoning capabilities beyond the base model by introducing new reasoning patterns from a stronger teacher, unlike RLVR which remains bounded by the base mod
6. The reasoning paths produced by RLVR-trained models have lower perplexity under the base model than even base model outputs, indicating RLVR sharpens the distribution within the base model's prior rat

## Capabilities

- RLVR improves average-case reasoning performance (pass@1) through more efficient sampling of correct paths already present in the base model — a confirmed sampling-efficiency gain, not a capacity expansion
- Knowledge distillation from a stronger reasoning teacher model genuinely expands the student model's reasoning scope beyond its base model — introducing new reasoning patterns unavailable to the base

## Limitations

- RLVR does not introduce fundamentally new reasoning capabilities — all reasoning paths generated by RLVR-trained models already exist in the base model's sampling distribution, making the base model the hard ceiling on RLVR reasoning capacity
- RLVR training progressively narrows reasoning coverage: pass@256 decreases as training steps increase even as pass@1 improves, meaning problems solvable by the base model become unsolvable after RLVR fine-tuning
- All six popular RLVR algorithms (PPO, GRPO, Reinforce++, RLOO, ReMax, DAPO) perform similarly and remain far from optimal — the sampling efficiency gap (∆SE) stays consistently above 40 percentage points for all of them
- RLVR training reduces model output entropy, and even restoring entropy to base-model levels via higher sampling temperature does not recover the full reasoning coverage lost during RLVR training
- KL regularization in RLVR severely degrades reasoning coverage: KL-regularized models achieve similar pass@1 to non-regularized variants but have much lower pass@128
- Scaling RLVR training rollouts from n=8 to n=32 per prompt yields only marginal improvement in pass@k coverage — the RLVR model is still surpassed by the base model at large k regardless
- The RLVR base-model bounding effect holds uniformly across mathematics, code generation, and visual (multimodal) reasoning — the limitation is not task-specific or modality-specific
- RLVR training increases the frequency of 'fully unsolvable' problems (accuracy=0): problems the base model could solve with sufficient sampling become impossible after RLVR, not merely harder
- The perplexity of RLVR model outputs under the base model decreases monotonically as RL training progresses — RLVR progressively constrains outputs to an increasingly narrow high-probability subset of the base model's distribution
- No current approach within the RLVR algorithm family provides the exploration signal necessary to discover reasoning strategies outside the base model's prior — the entire class is structurally unable to produce novel reasoning without fundamental paradigm change
- Binary outcome rewards (correct/incorrect) in RLVR provide no gradient signal to guide the discovery of new intermediate reasoning steps — only paths the model already probabilistically produces can be reinforced

## Bottlenecks

- RLVR lacks effective exploration mechanisms to discover reasoning strategies beyond what the base model can already sample — all current algorithms remain bounded by the base model's prior distribution, with ∆SE >40 points across the entire algorithm class
- Absence of fine-grained process-level reward signals in current RLVR blocks training from providing useful gradient information on intermediate reasoning steps — binary outcome rewards cannot guide discovery of new reasoning patterns
- On-policy training in all RLVR algorithms means the model can only learn from paths it already generates — multi-turn agent-environment interaction is identified as a necessary structural change to break this constraint
