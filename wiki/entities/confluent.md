# Confluent

> Confluent is an open-source data streaming platform built on Apache Kafka, notable in the venture capital world as both a landmark investment for Benchmark partner Eric Vishria and a cited example of Index Ventures' success in the open-source data infrastructure category. Its trajectory illustrates how the "wave-board-surfer" thesis that guides many top-tier VC firms plays out in practice — a strong market wave (real-time data streaming), a well-fitted product, and a technically credentialed founding team.

## Overview

Confluent is an open-source data streaming company built around Apache Kafka, occupying the data infrastructure layer that handles real-time event pipelines at scale. It sits in the broader category of open-source infrastructure businesses — alongside companies like Elastic, Databricks, and others — that Index Ventures partner Mike Volpi has identified as a repeating investment pattern: a technically rich open-source project, commercialised by its original authors, creating durable enterprise value.

For Benchmark's Eric Vishria, Confluent holds particular personal significance: it was his first investment at the firm. The founders — Jay Kreps, Neha Narkhede, and Jun Rao (the "Jay and June" referenced in the source evidence) — reached out to Vishria directly, and that early relationship shaped how he thinks about Series A conviction. The Benchmark partnership uses a 1–10 voting system where partners cannot vote 5, with 6+ constituting a yes — a structure designed to force explicit conviction rather than passive agreement. Confluent apparently cleared that bar decisively.

## Key Findings

The claims touching Confluent directly are sparse but revealing in context. Vishria recounts the founding team calling him before committing to Benchmark for the Series A — a detail that signals both his credibility as an early-stage operator (he had previously co-founded and sold Rockmelt to Yahoo in 2013) and the trust dynamic that matters at the point of term sheet.

Volpi's framing of Confluent as a successful open-source data infrastructure investment sits within a broader self-critique: his stated weakness as an investor is falling in love with technology over business fundamentals and people. That Confluent succeeded despite this potential bias suggests the founding team and market timing were strong enough to compensate — or that Volpi correctly identified it as a case where all three of his "wave, board, surfer" criteria aligned.

The venture framing around Confluent also surfaces a structural point both Volpi and Vishria converge on: the highest-value VC window is the $0-to-$100B journey, not the subsequent scaling phase. Confluent — having gone public in 2021 at significant scale — represents exactly the kind of exit trajectory that validates this thesis, though neither source discusses Confluent's post-IPO trajectory.

## Relationships and Open Questions

Confluent connects thematically to the broader open-source commercialisation pattern that recurs across [[themes/model_commoditization_and_open_source|model commoditization and open source]] — the question of whether open-source projects can sustain durable moats once the underlying technology becomes widely available. For data streaming, Confluent faced this acutely as managed Kafka alternatives proliferated. How it navigated that commoditisation pressure is not addressed in the source material.

The connection to AI is indirect but increasingly material: real-time data pipelines are critical infrastructure for AI applications that need fresh, low-latency signals. Whether Confluent has repositioned around this vector, and whether that repositioning affects its competitive durability, is a gap in the current coverage.
