### Motivation & Prior Limitations
Language model pretraining compute is scaling far faster than the supply of human-written text, creating a looming data bottleneck that current approaches cannot resolve through raw data accumulation alone.
- Standard LM pretraining treats web text as the direct learning target, ignoring the fact that written text is a compressed artifact of an underlying human thought process — background knowledge, intermediate reasoning steps, and deliberate inference are all stripped away before the model ever sees the data.
  - Kaplan et al. (2020) and Hoffmann et al. (2022) show that LMs require a large fraction of the entire human-written web to achieve strong performance, while Villalobos et al. (2022) and Muennighoff et al. (2024) document that pretraining compute growth far outpaces web text growth.
- Existing synthetic data approaches (paraphrasing, CoT-style rephrasing via WRAP) improve over raw data but fail to systematically recover the latent thought process underlying each document; they augment or replace text without modeling the joint relationship between thought and observed output.
  - WRAP-CoT, the strongest prior synthetic baseline, achieves 19.4% on MATH compared to 5.74% for raw-data training — a significant gap that the authors still surpass by modeling thoughts as a separate latent space rather than interleaving them with raw text.
- Self-improvement methods based on reinforcement learning (o1, R1, STaR) require domain-specific verifiable rewards or supervised data, making them inapplicable to general-purpose pretraining on task-agnostic corpora.

---

### Proposed Approach
The paper introduces **reasoning to learn**, a pretraining paradigm that treats web text as the observable outcome of a latent thought process and trains a model to both infer those latent thoughts and learn from the thought-augmented data, implemented as a latent variable model trained with standard next-token prediction.
- The core formalism models human-written data X as generated by a joint distribution p(Z, X) = p(Z)p(X | Z), where Z represents the latent thoughts (background knowledge, reasoning steps) underlying X; the model is trained simultaneously as the joint p(Z, X) and the approximate posterior q(Z | X) using a single shared-weight LM.
  - Training uses a random coin-flip formatting strategy: latent Z is placed as a suffix after X to train q(Z | X), and as a prefix before X to train p(Z, X), with special tokens `<StartOfLatent>` / `<EndOfLatent>` / `<Prior>` / `<Post>` to demarcate latent from observed content — requiring minimal modification to the standard pretraining pipeline.
  - This differs from WRAP-CoT in a critical structural way: thoughts are maintained in a separate latent space Z rather than mixed into the raw text space X, which ablations show is the primary driver of performance gains rather than merely the quality of the synthetic content.
- To overcome the ceiling imposed by any fixed teacher model, the paper proposes **Bootstrapping Latent Thoughts (BoLT)**, an Expectation-Maximization algorithm where the E-step uses Monte Carlo importance-weighted resampling to select higher-quality latents from K samples, and the M-step trains the next model iteration on those improved latents.
  - The BoLT E-step draws K samples {Z^(k)} from the current posterior q(Z | X; M_t), weights each by w^(k) = p(Z^(k), X; M_t) / q(Z^(k) | X; M_t), and resamples one proportional to those weights — a procedure proven to achieve a tighter importance-weighted ELBO (IWAE) that approaches the true posterior as K → ∞, serving as a "policy improvement operator" without any external reward signal.
  - BoLT is bootstrapped from a small warmstart corpus (240M tokens) with GPT-4o-mini synthetic latents, after which all subsequent iterations are entirely self-generated — analogous structurally to the SFT → RL post-training pipeline but operating on task-agnostic pretraining data.
- At inference time, latent thought models use few-shot CoT prompting where reasoning chains are placed in the latent space Z rather than the raw text space X, enabling the model to reason in the same discrete space it was trained on.

### Results & Capabilities
Training a 1.1B TinyLlama model on GPT-4o-mini synthetic latent thoughts over 480M FineMath tokens achieves 25.4% on MATH, compared to 5.74% for raw data repetition (Raw-Repeat), 11.18% for 8B unique raw tokens (Raw-Fresh), and 19.4% for WRAP-CoT — establishing that the latent space modeling design, not just synthetic data quality, drives the gains.
- On GSM8K, the latent thought model achieves 33.59% vs. 21.08% for WRAP-CoT and 13.27% for Raw-Fresh; on MMLU-STEM, 35.87% vs. 34.51% for WRAP-CoT.
  - Ablations confirm both the latent space separation and the latent-space CoT at evaluation time are independently critical: removing the latent space structure (mixing thoughts into raw text at a 1:1 ratio) degrades to WRAP-CoT-level performance (22.38% MATH), and removing latent-space CoT at eval time drops to 20.34%.
- BoLT bootstraps monotonically across at least three iterations in fixed-corpus experiments (1.92B raw tokens), with consistent improvement on both ELBO likelihood metrics and downstream MATH accuracy, and gains persisting through the fourth iteration in task-specific finetuning evaluations.
  - Using more Monte Carlo samples K monotonically improves both ELBO and MATH accuracy (K=1: ~10.8%, K=2: ~11.1%, K=4: ~11.4%, K=8: ~11.6%), establishing inference compute scaling as an additional axis for improving pretraining data efficiency.

## Key Claims

1. Compute scaling for language model pretraining has outpaced the growth of human-written texts, creating a data bottleneck risk.
2. Web text is a compressed representation of an underlying verbose human thought process, and the latent thoughts contain contextual knowledge and reasoning steps critical for data-efficient learning.
3. Augmenting pretraining data with GPT-4o-mini synthesized latent thoughts achieves 25.38% on MATH, significantly outperforming training on raw data (5.74%) and synthetic Chain-of-Thought paraphrases (1
4. A 1B language model can bootstrap its own performance through BoLT for at least three EM iterations, improving continuously without task-specific data.
5. Increasing inference compute (more Monte Carlo samples in the E-step) monotonically improves the quality of synthesized latent thoughts and thus downstream data efficiency.
6. Embedding synthetic thoughts in a separate latent space Z rather than mixing them into the raw text space is crucial for downstream performance.
7. Performing CoT reasoning in the latent space Z at inference time achieves better downstream performance than reasoning in the observed text space X.
8. The performance ceiling of models trained with synthetic latent thoughts is bounded by the capability of the surrogate posterior used to generate the thoughts, motivating the need for bootstrapping.
9. The BoLT EM algorithm's surrogate posterior achieves an importance-weighted ELBO that is provably tighter than the naive ELBO, improving monotonically with more Monte Carlo samples.
10. Augmenting (rather than replacing) each document chunk X with latent thoughts Z is key to outperforming strong synthetic data generation baselines.

## Capabilities

- LMs augmented with synthetic latent thoughts achieve dramatically improved math reasoning data efficiency: a 1.1B TinyLlama reaches 25.4% on MATH and 33.6% on GSM8K with only 480M raw tokens, versus 5.74% and 5.76% for raw data training on the same corpus
- A 1B LM can self-improve through BoLT (Bootstrapping Latent Thoughts) — an EM algorithm where the model generates its own latent thoughts to augment pretraining data, then retrains on them, iterating at least 3–4 times with monotonically improving performance and no external supervision or verifiabl
- Inference compute scaling (Monte Carlo sampling) applied at pretraining data generation time monotonically improves latent thought quality and downstream model performance: increasing MC samples K from 1 to 8 in the BoLT E-step yields consistent ELBO and MATH accuracy improvements
- Latent thought models can perform chain-of-thought reasoning in a latent space Z (rather than the observed text space X), and this consistently outperforms raw-text-space CoT at inference time on math benchmarks
- Synthetic latent thought augmentation (training jointly on raw text and inferred latent thoughts) outperforms training on an equivalent volume of unique raw tokens, demonstrating that data quality via thought augmentation can substitute for raw data quantity

## Limitations

- The entire experimental validation is confined to a 1.1B parameter model under academic compute budget, making it unclear whether reasoning-to-learn and BoLT generalize to frontier-scale models
- Reasoning-to-learn is only demonstrated on math-heavy reasoning data (FineMath corpus); transfer to general-domain pretraining data is unvalidated and may not yield measurable improvements
- BoLT bootstrapping causes few-shot GSM8K performance to degrade across iterations even as MATH performance improves and finetuned GSM8K improves — indicating that extended optimization on math-heavy corpora degrades general language understanding needed for word problems
- The current latent generation structure is 'myopic': it models latent thoughts autoregressively for each local text chunk, making it unable to capture hierarchical planning processes humans use for long-form content (research papers, novels, codebases)
- Without BoLT, the performance ceiling of a latent thought model is bounded by the quality of the teacher model used for warmstart synthesis; this fundamentally limits the approach's potential to advance frontier capabilities using only weak teachers
- Continual bootstrapping risks catastrophic forgetting during iterative pretraining; the paper required a special warmup-stable-decay (WSD) learning rate schedule and careful initialization to mitigate degradation across iterations
- MMLU-STEM performance across BoLT iterations fell within noise floor (< 28%), revealing that extended math-focused pretraining actively degrades general STEM knowledge; general domain NLL on DCLM corpus also degrades with more FineMath optimization
- Latent generation at scale requires a distributed heterogeneous GPU cluster (H100/200, A100/6000/5000/40, L40, RTX3090) — the synthetic data generation compute cost is substantial and adds a parallel infrastructure requirement on top of pretraining compute
- The approach involves limited exploration of critical design choices: latent generative structure, chunk size, MC sampling strategy, and warmstart model choice are all under-explored, meaning the reported results may not represent the method's true ceiling or floor
- Latent thought quality is sensitive to bootstrapping: amplification of biases in self-generated latents at scale could produce unintended model behaviors, and this risk is not yet systematically characterized

## Bottlenecks

- Pretraining compute scaling has outpaced the growth of high-quality human-written text on the web, creating a data-constrained regime that blocks further capability gains from naive scaling of raw data
- Self-bootstrapping via latent thought synthesis shows performance saturation at small scales (1B model, math domain) after 3–4 iterations, with unclear mechanisms for whether iteration count before saturation scales with model size
