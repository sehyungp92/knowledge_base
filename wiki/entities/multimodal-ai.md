# Multimodal AI

Multimodal AI refers to systems that process and reason across multiple data types simultaneously — text, images, audio, video, and structured data — within a unified architecture. Its significance in the current AI landscape lies less in the underlying technical achievement and more in what it enables strategically: vertical AI companies that integrate multimodal pipelines into domain-specific workflows are emerging as one of the few durable sources of competitive moat in an era of rapid model commoditization.

## Overview

Multimodal AI's primary strategic importance is as the technical foundation for vertical AI's competitive differentiation. According to Part IV: Ten principles for building strong vertical AI businesses, competitive edge in vertical AI "increasingly depends on combining data types and workflow integrations, not proprietary models alone." This reframes multimodality not as a research milestone but as a product architecture decision — the moat comes from *how* multimodal capabilities are integrated into specific workflows, not from model ownership itself.

## The Strategic Role in Vertical AI

The argument for multimodal AI as a durable moat rests on the observation that foundation models are becoming commoditized infrastructure. In this environment, a vertical AI company's defensibility cannot rely on access to superior models — it must come from depth of workflow integration and the diversity of data modalities it ingests. A legal AI system that processes contract text is replicable; one that simultaneously parses document structure, audio from client calls, and signature metadata across a proprietary dataset is far harder to displace.

This dynamic is already visible in market outcomes. Part I: The future of AI is vertical documents that Thomson Reuters acquired CaseText for $650M in 2023, followed by DocuSign's $165M acquisition of Lexion in 2024 — early signals that incumbents are pricing in the structural advantages of vertically integrated AI. LLM-native vertical companies are sustaining ~65% gross margins while growing ~400% year-over-year, and have already reached 80% of the average contract value of traditional vertical SaaS incumbents despite being far younger. The speed of this trajectory suggests multimodal workflow depth is compressing the usual time-to-parity.

The historical analogy from the SaaS wave is instructive but should not be over-read: horizontal SaaS preceded vertical SaaS by a decade (Salesforce went public a full decade before Veeva), but vertical AI is not following the same lag pattern. Traditional vertical software frameworks remain necessary but are explicitly insufficient — Part IV notes that prior frameworks "are only part of" what is needed, leaving open the question of what the genuinely novel principles are.

## Demonstrated Capabilities

Early capability signals are encouraging but remain at early maturity stages. In research settings, multimodal AI diagnostic agents have shown greater robustness in diagnostic accuracy against poor-quality images than human primary care physicians — a notable finding given that image degradation is a common real-world condition, not a controlled-lab artifact (maturity: research only). Separately, multimodal research agents have been demonstrated detecting intent signals from phrasing, tone, and facial cues in real time to dynamically steer interview conversations (maturity: demo).

A more grounded example of production multimodal deployment is Bridge, a medical documentation tool described in State of the Cloud 2024: it records patient conversations, transcribes and summarizes them, and inputs structured data directly into EMR systems. This combines audio processing, NLP, and structured data output in a single workflow — precisely the multimodal integration pattern that generates lock-in through data accumulation and workflow dependency.

## Investor and Market Framing

From the VC perspective, multimodal AI cuts across multiple investment theses. a16z's Anish Acharya on Consumer AI illustrates how the firm has oriented entirely around AI as a cross-cutting concern, maintaining separate funds for AI apps and AI infrastructure precisely because the market dynamics differ. Foundation model providers (the layer where multimodal capabilities are first developed) sit in the infrastructure fund; the application layer — where multimodal capabilities get deployed into vertical workflows — sits in the apps fund. B2B voice AI is already working in two categories: voice agents replacing phone-based customer service, and voice scribes providing accurate transcription for hands-on professionals like clinicians. Both are multimodal in the sense that they combine audio, language, and structured output pipelines.

Three business models are emerging for vertical AI that leverages multimodal capabilities: co-pilots, agents, and AI-enabled services. Each represents a different depth of multimodal integration and a different risk/reward profile for founders and investors.

## Open Questions and Limitations

The central open question is whether multimodal integration creates *durable* moat or merely temporary differentiation as models improve. The claim that competitive edge depends on "combining data types and workflow integrations, not proprietary models" implicitly acknowledges that model-level differentiation is eroding — but does not address whether workflow integration itself becomes a commodity once tooling matures. If multimodal APIs become as accessible as text APIs, the integration advantage may compress.

The research capability findings (diagnostic accuracy, intent detection) are promising but exist only at demo or research-only maturity. The gap between what is demonstrated in controlled evaluations and what is deployed reliably in production workflows remains large and underreported. The Bridge example is a notable exception — a production deployment — but the broader evidence base for robust multimodal production systems at scale is thin in the sources currently indexed.

There is also a conspicuous absence of discussion around the *cost* of multimodal inference at scale. Vision and audio processing are significantly more expensive than text-only inference, which has direct implications for the ~65% gross margin figure cited for LLM-native vertical companies. Whether those margins hold as multimodal pipelines scale is an open empirical question.
