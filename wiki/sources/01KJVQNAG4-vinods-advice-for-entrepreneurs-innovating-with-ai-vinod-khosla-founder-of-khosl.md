# Vinod’s advice for entrepreneurs innovating with AI | Vinod Khosla (Founder of Khosla Ventures)

**Authors:** 
**Published:** 2024-04-22 00:00:00
**Type:** video

## Analysis

Background

- <a id="_Hlk171588124"></a>Had always been interested in what you can do with technology, what you can learn, and how much more you can do with it\. And that still remains interested in possibilities opened up by technology 
- Would describe himself as a techno\-optimism with empathy, and deploys large amounts of capital on projects that could have a large impact\. Most interested in situations where technology can be applied to address to large problems like fusion, plant proteins, public transit etc\.
- <a id="_Hlk171587919"></a>Have changed fields very frequently and believes that has helped to not get caught up in the details of any one area, but to be able to look across areas and synthesise larger things\. For instance AI in itself is interesting, but more so in the context of so many other areas, such as construction or drug discovery
- <a id="_Hlk171588175"></a>Has a goal of providing 7 billion people the level of affluence and prosperity as 700 million enjoy today, which was based on a realisation that 10% of the people on the planet have a rich lifestyle and 100% want it but are constrained by resources \(e\.g\., steel, number of doctors etc\.\)\. Solutions that can multiply resources can only come from technology\.
	- These are exciting but difficult problems, including low carbon steel, low carbon power etc\.

Visionaries vs\. Experts

- There are experts who will try to define what’s possible and what’s not by often just extrapolating based on the past\. However, if you believe radical change won't happen, you won't attempt it\. And so it can be damaging when people listen to expert forecasts\.
- Entrepreneurs, on the other hand, invent the future they want with their vision of technology\. 
	- For instance, when Elon Musk started Tesla, nobody believed electric cars would be important, and Tesla exceed the 25\-year forecast for the number of electric cars from the Department of Energy in 2010 in 5 years\.
- The other thing to keep in mind is that when you attempt large things, you are likely to fail\. However, your willingness to fail allows you to succeed – shouldn’t mind something that has the 90% chance of failing if there's a 10% chance of changing the world\. Believing things are possible and then driving to make them possible is what's important and how society changes

Perspectives on Risk

- <a id="_Hlk171589539"></a>Most people don't like to fail, so they don't venture out into anything new\. If you're not egocentric, when you fail, you say I failed, now let's try the next thing\. Some things are too important not to try, and it doesn't matter what the probability of success or failure is\. 
	- That's why they invested in plant proteins and fusion when nobody was doing it\. 
- If enough people tried and took different shots on goal, you would have success\. On the other hand, if you don't take risks, you can't possibly innovate\.

Perspectives on AI

- <a id="_Hlk171588328"></a>Long believed in the power of AI to make a large impact, which is not how most people think about AI\. With primary care and education there's zero chance, subject to regulations, that technology isn't there\. These are powerful tools that everybody should have\.
	- For instance, technology is going to be available in the next couple of years to have an app that does everything a primary care physician can do when they're not directly in front of the patient\. So why not provide it to everybody on the planet? If you could give free primary care to everybody on the planet for $1\-2, that’s something worth attempting regardless of how long it takes\. 
	- It is also clear that AI\-based tutors would equalise education\. Many of us have parents who could afford to send us to give us personal tutors, but if every student could have that for near free, it's a huge impact and also a fun challenge to take on\.
- It is difficult to imagine an industry that won't be innovated by AI\. Get more excited broader you look\. For instance, you could do structural engineering, chip design, nutrition \(e\.g\., HealthifyMe in India\), build a financial analyst, customer support, therapists, recruiters agents that do tasks for people 
- Funding is not always a good indicator of where the interesting opportunities are\. Once successful, everybody naturally wants to invest in that area\. It’s important not to solely focus where the crowd is going and not follow the crowd\. Think for yourself from first principles, ask where can AI make a large difference and apply your thinking
	- It's very exciting to see how AI could transform media, music, animation, programming, robotics etc\. 
- Picking the right market and the right part of the market, and then within a market, where to get toehold in the market, picking the right approach to a market, building a team, building an MVP, all of this is tricky\. And the right team in the right market can still fail because of tactics and strategy\.
- The primary focus should be solving a problem very well, then can worry about other issues such as dependencies\.

Open Source

- For security reasons, open sourcing the state\-of\-the\-art models may not be optimal\. It is important because whoever wins this race of AI technology over the next 20 years will have strong economic influence and whoever has strong economic influence will have strong political influence – the world that believe in liberal philosophies, e\.g\., freedom of speech, vs\. one that believes in more centralised control\.
- If regulation doesn't intervene to limit the distribution of open source models, there may not be another path to slow down China's speed of innovation\. Restricting the latest Nvidia chips into China may also be a good idea along those lines
	- Similarly, an argument why TikTok should be banned include that it may potentially be an instrument through which future persuasive AI could be used to influence voters in a way that would be detrimental to liberal values

Perspectives 

## Key Claims

1. AI is making deep expertise free by replicating the knowledge of specialists such as oncologists, structural engineers, and climate scientists.
2. LLMs represent only the first inning of a long match in AI development, and many more AI approaches beyond LLMs are possible.
3. It is hard to find any industry that will not be radically innovated by AI.
4. An AI application that performs most primary care physician functions will be available within a few years.
5. AI-based tutoring will equalize education by providing every student with a near-free personal tutor.
6. Neuro-symbolic approaches to AI will be important, representing an alternative to transformer-based models.
7. Probabilistic programming will be an important complementary approach to LLMs.
8. Over the next 10 years, AI will be capable of doing 80% of 80% of all economically valuable jobs, meeting Khosla's definition of AGI.
9. Two-thirds of all jobs by headcount will be capable of being done by AI, subject to regulatory and political constraints.
10. Training next-generation AI models in 2025–2026 will cost $5–10 billion per model.

## Capabilities

- LLMs can use external tools (calculators, database lookups) and choose which tool is appropriate for each step of a task
- AI models can learn to control and navigate software interfaces (EMRs, operating systems) end-to-end without explicit programming
- Multi-modal models trained on vision, language, and code simultaneously improve all three modalities beyond single-modality models
- AI-assisted EMR navigation systems can save 30-40% of physician time by learning complex medical software interfaces

## Limitations

- Current LLMs are static after training and cannot continuously learn from new experiences or adapt to new environments
- LLMs with high temperature/creative sampling can hallucinate plausible-sounding false information
- Unresolved uncertainty about optimal tradeoffs between general models, domain-specific fine-tuning, and mixture-of-experts approaches
- General-purpose LLMs sacrifice domain-specific optimizations that could be obtained through specialized training
- Products dependent on external API-based models face switching costs and lock-in risk if upstream provider changes capabilities or pricing
- Ongoing debate about whether scaling laws will continue yielding improvements or approach fundamental limits

## Bottlenecks

- Training frontier LLMs requires $5-10 billion for next-generation models (2025-2026), creating massive capital barrier for new entrants
- Extreme capital requirements concentrate frontier model development around few well-funded players, limiting architectural diversity
- Regulatory bodies and professional licensing restrict deployment of capable AI systems in medicine, law, and other regulated professions
- Labor unions and collective agreements slow AI adoption by protecting existing workers and business models from disruption
- Current LLM training paradigm is post-hoc static; architecture cannot support continuous learning from deployment experience
- Uncertain whether additional data/compute scaling yields proportional capability improvements or encounters fundamental limits

## Breakthroughs

- Multi-modal training (vision + language + code in same model) improves all modalities simultaneously rather than in isolation
- Large Action Networks learn to control software interfaces end-to-end without explicit APIs or programming
- Tool-use agents delegate computation to external tools rather than reasoning probabilistically, composing multiple specialized tools
- Neurosymbolic and procedural programming approaches are emerging as competitive alternatives to pure transformer scaling
