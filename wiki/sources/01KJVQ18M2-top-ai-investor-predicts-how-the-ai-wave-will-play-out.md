# Top AI Investor Predicts How The AI Wave Will Play Out

Mike Volpi, GP at Index Ventures, offers a veteran investor's framework for navigating the AI wave — drawing on pattern recognition from the internet and mobile shifts to argue that AI is a genuine platform transition, not hype, but that timing remains fundamentally uncertain, startup value capture will be harder than in the internet era, and the capital intensity of frontier model training is creating structural concentration that separates this wave from its predecessors.

**Authors:** Mike Volpi
**Published:** 2024-01-26
**Type:** video

---

## Core Thesis

Volpi's central argument is that AI qualifies as a genuine platform shift — not because its applications are obvious today, but because it enables *categories of activity that were not possible before*, in the same way the browser and web server unlocked internet commerce. The analogy to the 1990s internet is both encouraging and cautionary: direction matters more than timing, which means investors should lean into the hype cycle, but the financial bubble risk is real for those overweighting near-term revenue.

> "Some of the things that people in the late 90s imagined the internet would offer were correct, but they just didn't happen in the time people thought they would."

This temporal uncertainty is not a peripheral risk — it is the central investment problem. See [[themes/ai_market_dynamics|AI Market Dynamics]].

---

## What Makes AI a Real Platform

Volpi's platform test is enabling net-new activities, not just improving existing ones. By this standard, crypto failed (Bitcoin is a currency, not an enabler of new categories) while AI passes. The analogy to the browser moment is precise: LLMs have crossed a deployment threshold where they function as a base layer on which others can build.

> "They end up being platforms where people can take them and realize new ideas off that base."

This breakthrough is notable but not sufficient for predicting which applications will survive. See [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|Startup Formation and GTM]].

## Distinguishing Real AI Products from Wrappers

The most practically useful section of the analysis is Volpi's framework for identifying durable AI applications versus thin API integrations. Three criteria elevate an app above "wrapper" status:

1. **Network effects** — multiple users benefit from shared usage, not just individual productivity
2. **Domain-embedded workflow knowledge** — the application captures sector-specific workflow logic that is not trivially reproduced by swapping the underlying model
3. **Compound model usage** — intelligent combination of multiple capability types (vision, large language, small specialized), not single-model passthrough

> "How much does this particular app embrace details of the use case of what the user is doing, and how much of it requires knowledge of a specific domain?"

The identification problem cuts both ways: most current AI startups may not survive model commoditization, but the ones that do will have built something the model provider cannot easily replicate. This connects directly to the [[themes/startup_and_investment|Startup and Investment]] theme.

## Limitations and Open Questions

These are the most analytically valuable signals from Volpi's framework:

**Capital concentration (severity: blocking)**
The capital intensity of frontier model training creates a structural barrier preventing competitive diversity at the foundation layer. Volpi expects 2–3 players to dominate long-term. Unlike the internet (which required capital for telco infrastructure, not for the application layer), AI's capital moat sits at the model layer itself — directly limiting startup participation.

> "Large models cost a lot of money, having lots of good data costs a lot of money, and so at least in my crystal ball, the model layer will be largely the big guys."

**Open-source enterprise gap (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)**
Open-source LLMs have rough edges that make them insufficiently enterprise-friendly for production deployment. This gap is currently sustaining elevated pricing for proprietary enterprise models, but Volpi expects it to close within 1–2 years.

**Incumbent data moats (severity: significant, trajectory: worsening)**
Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Apple hold data advantages that compound with capital intensity to block startup competition at the foundation layer. Unlike mobile (where incumbents captured most value) or internet (where startups captured 80–90%), AI may tilt further toward incumbents than any prior wave.

**Regulatory vacuum for open-source misuse (severity: significant, trajectory: stable)**
Government regulation cannot keep pace with AI development velocity. Open-source frontier models create an unmitigable misuse vector — capable enough to assist bad actors, impossible to recall once released. No effective regulatory mechanism is apparent.

> "There isn't an obvious regulation that I can think of that would be effective to stop that."

**Timing uncertainty (severity: significant, trajectory: unclear)**
The fundamental problem: even correct directional bets can fail financially if the timing is a decade off. This is not unique to AI — it matches the 1990s internet pattern — but it means current valuations at $500M for $1M ARR companies embed substantial probability of loss.

## Incumbents vs. Startups: The Value Capture Question

Volpi's comparative analysis across platform shifts is the most distinctive contribution here:

- **Internet**: 80–90% of value captured by new entrants (Amazon, Google)
- **Mobile**: Majority captured by Apple and Google (incumbents)
- **AI**: Expected to tilt further toward incumbents than mobile, due to the combination of (1) incumbent AI competency, (2) data moats, (3) capital intensity

However, Volpi remains constructive on VC participation because the relevant VC window is the zero-to-$100B journey, not $100B-to-$1T. Enough value will be created in the application layer and infrastructure that portfolio construction across the wave remains viable — but entry valuations must be disciplined.

## Investment Framework

Volpi's current environment diagnosis is blunt: one sector (AI) is hot, portfolios broadly are struggling, and the prior decade's multi-sector opportunity has collapsed. The key shifts:

- **Judgment over velocity**: The 2010s trained VCs to move fast and win term sheet races. The current environment rewards wisdom over speed.
- **Portfolio construction matters**: Impossible to know which AI bet is right; diversification within the wave is rational given failure rates.
- **Entry valuation discipline**: 10x outcomes statistically require reasonable entry points; $500M pre-revenue bets are structurally unfavorable.
- **Lower interest rates as a catalyst**: 2–3% rates expected to normalize company valuations and restore capital flows.

> "If something feels like an inevitability, our job as venture capitalists is to lean into the hype cycle and not worry too much about valuations — within reason."
