### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- RLVR methods that succeed in structured domains (math, code) fail to transfer to open-ended tasks like document revision, analytical summarization, and scientific report writing, because these tasks lack direct verifiability and thus stable reward signals.
  - Binary outcome rewards (correct/incorrect) do not exist for long-form generation, making reward design the central unsolved problem.
- Plain sequence-level self-certainty aggregation — averaging log-probabilities or probabilities over a long reference answer — produces rewards with weak discriminative power for ranking CoT traces.
  - Analytical and empirical evidence shows that only ~2–3% of tokens in a long-form reference answer are "reasoning-reflective" (vary significantly across rollouts), and when these are averaged with hundreds of uninformative tokens, their signal shrinks at O(1/√t) to O(1/t) under groupwise z-scoring, effectively masking quality differences between good and bad reasoning.
- Converting rubric judgments into fine-grained dense rewards is brittle: LLM-generated rubrics are inconsistent, multi-criterion aggregation compounds noise, per-criterion majority-voting is computationally expensive, and static task-level rubrics cause score saturation and mode collapse during RL training.
- Reward hacking is a persistent vulnerability: token-level dense rewards alone allow the policy to generate fluent but fundamentally non-compliant outputs (e.g., making no revision at all) that still receive well-structured advantage scores because groupwise standardization amplifies relative differences even within a uniformly degenerate rollout group.

---

### Proposed Approach
- DRO (Direct Reasoning Optimization) frames open-ended RL training as a constrained optimization problem built on GRPO, combining a token-level dense reward (R3) for CoT quality with rubric-gating as hard feasibility constraints at the rollout-group level.
  - Unlike prior work that either trains a separate reward model, uses unweighted self-certainty, or converts rubric scores into dense rewards, DRO uses only the reference policy itself for both reward computation and rubric evaluation, eliminating any external judge or learned reward model.
- The Reasoning Reflection Reward (R3) identifies reasoning-reflective tokens — those whose self-certainty (probability π(y_j | q, ĉ_i, y_{<j})) varies most across sampled CoT traces — and up-weights them via softmax weighting proportional to their across-rollout standard deviation σ_j, combined with probability clipping to exclude tokens with consistently extreme probabilities (>0.85 or <0.05).
  - Using raw probability rather than log-probability is a deliberate design choice: log-probability is dominated by extremely low-probability tokens (e.g., −4 to −25 on the log scale) that arise from stylistic mismatches and inject large noise rather than reasoning signal.
  - The softmax weighting with temperature ω ∈ [1, 2] prevents over-concentration on a single token while still amplifying the minority informative subset; setting ω too high (>4) causes collapse onto a single token, magnifying gradient noise.
- Rubric-gating enforces hard accept/reject decisions on entire rollout groups using query-specific yes/no rubrics generated by the reference policy, checked against the reference answer to ensure rubrics are well-posed, and applied via two gates: a Coverage gate (every rubric satisfied by at least µ rollouts) and a Consistency gate (top ρ% of rollouts by R3 score must each satisfy at least ν% of rubrics).
  - This separates feasibility determination from reward ranking: rubrics gate which groups can update the policy at all, while R3 ranks within accepted groups, preventing high dense-reward scores from arising in outputs that violate task fundamentals.
- A variance-based dynamic filtering mechanism rejects rollout groups whose top-10% tokens by σ fall below an adaptive threshold τ_t, updated every N steps as the q-th percentile of recent group variances, discarding low-signal groups that would spuriously amplify advantages under groupwise standardization.
  - In practice, static pre-filtering retaining the top ~10% highest-variance groups (plus ~10% of remaining groups to preserve hard cases) outperforms dynamic on-the-fly filtering at scale, yielding effective retention rates of 12–18% depending on dataset.

### Results & Capabilities
- DRO consistently achieves the best downstream performance across all four evaluated datasets (ParaRev, RaR-Medicine, ContractNLI, FinQA), outperforming the strongest individual baselines by clear margins.
  - On ParaRev (scientific paragraph revision), full DRO achieves a 63.7% win rate vs. the base model, compared to 57.8% for R3 alone, 55.9% for rubric-only, and 49.5% for avg log-probability.
  - On ContractNLI (legal contract review), DRO achieves 84.5 macro-F1 vs. 80.6 for R3 alone and 73.6 for avg log-probability.
  - On FinQA (financial numerical reasoning), DRO (68.4% accuracy) approaches the oracle verifier upper bound (69.0%), demonstrating effectiveness on short-form verifiable tasks as well.
- R3 reaches equivalent downstream performance 2–3× faster than plain average probability aggregation across most datasets.
  - On ParaRev, R3 reaches the same performance level at step 96 vs. step 288 for avg probability; on RaR-Medicine, 128 vs. 256 steps; on ContractNLI, 208 vs. 320 steps.
- Rubric-gating improves rubric constraint satisfaction throughout training even without converting rubric judgments into explicit rewards, as evidenced by a declining cumulative rejection rate curve on ParaRev and RaR-Medicine — fewer rollout groups are rejected as training p

## Capabilities

- Constrained RL training on open-ended long-form tasks (scientific paragraph revision, medical QA, legal contract review, financial reasoning) using token-level dense reward and rubric-gated constraints, without external reward models or verifiers
- Reasoning Reflection Reward (R3): token-level dense reward computed from model self-certainty on reference outcome tokens, weighted by cross-rollout variance to emphasize reasoning-reflective tokens, enabling discriminative RL signal for long-form generation without external judges
- Rubric-gating as hard accept/reject feasibility constraints on RL rollout groups: LLM-generated query-specific rubrics gate rollout groups without converting rubric judgments into dense rewards, enforcing lexical and semantic compliance while avoiding rubric instability
- 2–3× faster sample-efficient RL learning on long-form tasks via R3 compared to plain probability aggregation, attributed to sharper gradient signal-to-noise ratio from reasoning-reflective token emphasis
- Variance-based dynamic filtering of RL training rollout groups: retaining only high-variance rollout groups (top ~10% of corpus) enables more stable and sample-efficient RL convergence, with effective corpus retention rates of 12–18%

## Limitations

- Token-level dense rewards alone are vulnerable to reward hacking — the model can generate uniformly fluent but low-quality rollout groups that still exhibit relative reward differences, causing misguided policy gradient updates and eventual model collapse
- Only ~2–3% of tokens in long-form reference answers are 'reasoning-reflective'; for some corpora this falls below 1%, causing plain sequence-wide aggregation of self-certainty to fail as a discriminative RL reward signal for long-form generation
- Rubric-based dense scoring is brittle at scale: crafting fine-grained criteria is labor-intensive, LLM-generated rubrics are inconsistent when atomic or evolving criteria are needed, and multi-criterion aggregation compounds noise from unreliable per-criterion scores and LLM-assigned weights
- Training on the full unfiltered corpus precipitates model collapse — only the top ~10% of rollout groups by reward variance are informative enough for stable RL; including low-variance groups produces spuriously amplified advantages that destabilize gradient updates
- Autoregressive dependence between token self-certainties reduces the effective number of independently informative training signals: earlier reasoning-reflective tokens constrain later ones, exacerbating the dilution problem in long-form RL rewards
- Rubric-gating is ineffective for structured/JSON-formatted outputs — when answer format is tightly constrained by schema, comprehensive semantic rubric checks are difficult to express and provide limited training improvement
- DRO requires reference answers for all training queries — R3 measures self-certainty against a reference outcome, restricting applicability to tasks where curated reference answers are available and limiting use in truly unsupervised open-ended settings
- Over-sharpening of reasoning-reflective token emphasis (ω > 4) concentrates reward weight onto a single token, magnifying gradient variance and accelerating model collapse
- Plain average log-probability reward fails on long-form generation because extremely low-probability tokens (<0.01) arising from style/formatting misalignments induce large log-scale noise that dominates the reward signal
- Query-agnostic static rubrics lead to score saturation and over-regularization toward superficial features, potentially inducing mode collapse during RL training on open-ended tasks
- R3's gains diminish when most output tokens are already highly predictable — on JSON-formatted outputs or short-form verifiable answers, R3 provides only marginal improvement over plain aggregation
- Late-stage performance collapse occurs when rubric-gating and variance-based filtering are both disabled — R3 alone induces collapse slightly faster than plain aggregation due to comparative weighting amplifying groupwise z-scores when within-group reward variation is low

## Bottlenecks

- Reward signal dilution in long-form RL: extreme sparsity of reasoning-reflective tokens (~2–3% of reference sequence, <1% in some corpora) causes naive self-certainty aggregation to fail as a discriminative reward signal, blocking reliable RL training on long-form open-ended tasks
- Automated rubric generation reliability: LLM-generated rubrics for open-ended RL training are inconsistent, labor-intensive to author manually, and inherently incomplete because new model failure modes continuously emerge during training invalidating existing criteria
- Persistent reward hacking in open-ended RL: even with improved token-level reward designs, models can generate uniformly low-quality rollout groups that exhibit relative reward differences, requiring mandatory additional constraint layers (rubric-gating, variance filtering) to prevent policy collaps
