# No Priors Ep. 72 | With Sarah Guo and Elad Gil

> A wide-ranging investor conversation between Sarah Guo and Elad Gil rebutting Goldman Sachs skeptics on AI ROI, arguing that critics misapply old-school ML frameworks to transformer-era capabilities, and advancing a thesis around AI-driven market creation through three novel mechanisms: incubation, AI-led buyouts of services companies, and the coming wave of enterprise adoption that has not yet materialized.

**Authors:** Sarah Guo, Elad Gil
**Published:** 2024-07-18
**Type:** video

---

## Expert Analysis

### Responding to the Goldman Sachs Skeptics

The episode opens as a direct rebuttal to two prominent Goldman Sachs critics — Daron Acemoglu and Jim Covello — whose arguments had received significant attention at the time.

Acemoglu, an MIT economics professor, argued that AI will impact fewer than 5% of all tasks and that the trillion-dollar capex being deployed on model training is largely wasted on problems AI cannot solve. Covello, head of global equity research at GS, made two separate claims: first, that unlike the internet — which disrupted *expensive* things cheaply from the start — AI begins as a very expensive solution; and second, that any efficiency gains AI delivers will simply be competed away, leaving no net benefit for any company.

Guo and Gil treat both positions as symptomatic of the same deeper error: **applying an old-school ML framework to a fundamentally different technology**. The critics are not wrong about prior ML — they are wrong about transformers. Modern architectures scale with data in ways that produce qualitatively different capabilities, and the pace of improvement makes 10-year pessimistic projections especially suspect when even AI practitioners decline to forecast two years out.

A subtler point is raised about competitive dynamics. Covello's "efficiency gains competed away" argument assumes uniform adoption — as if every company in a sector will capture AI gains simultaneously and equally. History contradicts this. Amazon did not merely share internet gains with Borders; it destroyed Borders. The economic outcome of AI will likewise be **asymmetric and concentrating**, rewarding aggressive adopters and punishing laggards.

### The Three Drivers of New AI Markets

Rather than defending AI's current capabilities defensively, Guo and Gil articulate a structural thesis: AI is opening markets in *unexpected* ways through three distinct forces.

1. **New generalizable capabilities.** Prior ML required bespoke specialized models for each domain. Modern AI trains one general model to do many things — across biology, robotics, language, image generation, and video. This generalizability is itself a capability discontinuity, not just a performance improvement.

2. **API accessibility.** Any organization globally can now access advanced AI with a simple API call, eliminating the need to build MLOps infrastructure or run model training. This removes a major barrier to enterprise experimentation.

3. **Organizational AI mandates.** For the first time in roughly two decades — analogous to the emergence of the internet — companies have an active mandate to consider AI products. Buying behavior has shifted; categories that were previously closed are now open.

These three forces together explain why [[themes/ai_market_dynamics|AI market dynamics]] look different from prior technology cycles.

### Enterprise Adoption: Still in the First Inning

Despite years of hype, enterprise AI adoption remains strikingly early. Most large enterprises are thinking about AI in three sequential stages:

1. What vendor tools can I buy?
2. How do I adapt my internal tools?
3. How do I integrate AI into customer-facing products?

The vast majority are still at stage one or two. Almost nothing has happened yet on the product side — the consumer-facing AI-native companies (ChatGPT, Perplexity) are the visible exceptions, but enterprise product integration is barely beginning. This means **the big wave of enterprise adoption has not yet arrived**, and current AI impact figures dramatically understate the eventual effect.

This framing has direct implications for the [[themes/ai_business_and_economics|economics of AI investment]]: the capex being deployed now is a bet on a future wave, not a harvest of current productivity.

### AI-Led Buyouts and Incubation as Novel Investment Mechanisms

The most distinctive strategic contribution of this episode is the thesis around two investment strategies that AI makes newly viable.

**Incubation** — typically a poor venture strategy because of the bootstrapping difficulty — becomes more attractive when AI can substitute for early headcount and compress time-to-product. A small founding team (6–8 engineers, 1–2 founders, a designer) can do things that previously required far larger teams.

**AI-driven buyouts of services companies** represent an even more novel idea. The traditional tech-enabled buyout playbook — acquire a services company and drive cost structure improvement with software — has always been constrained by the pace of technology adoption and the difficulty of forcing organizational change. AI changes two of these constraints:

- **Adoption can be imposed.** When you own the company, you can restructure operations directly rather than waiting for organic adoption.
- **The cost structure transformation is now dramatic rather than incremental.** The Klarna customer support case is the anchoring example: 700-person headcount reduction via LLMs and custom workflows, while simultaneously achieving 24/7 coverage, ~20 languages, higher NPS than human agents, faster response time, and lower repeat incidents.

The buyout acquires **distribution and customer relationships** — the hard part — while AI handles the cost structure transformation. The key bottleneck is that software engineering teams generally lack the operational and M&A expertise required to identify and integrate services companies. This is a [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC and startup ecosystem]] structural gap.

The broader picture is quantitatively framed: US software spend is approximately $500 billion per year, while headcount cost across services industries is roughly $5 trillion. The software market is well-served; the services market transformation has barely begun.

## Key Claims

1. Acemoglu argues AI will impact fewer than 5% of all tasks; Guo/Gil treat this as a fundamental misreading of transformer-era capabilities.
2. Covello's "expensive from the start" argument misses that AI cost trajectories are rapidly declining.
3. Covello's "efficiency gains competed away" argument assumes uniform adoption, ignoring concentration effects (Amazon vs. Borders analogy).
4. Critics are applying old-school ML frameworks — where data labeling was paramount and models were specialized — to a different regime.
5. Scale and data quality have objectively improved model capability and output quality over multiple years.
6. Predicting 10 years of insignificant improvement is implausible when practitioners won't forecast two years out.
7. Enterprise AI adoption is primarily at vendor-tool evaluation; customer-facing product integration has barely begun.
8. The big wave of enterprise AI adoption has not yet happened; current impact figures are early-stage.
9. AI is opening markets through three forces: generalizable capabilities, API accessibility, and organizational AI mandates.
10. Modern AI trains one general model across many domains; prior norm was many small specialized models.
11. US software spend is ~$500B/year; services industry headcount cost is ~$5T/year — the latter is the larger transformation target.
12. Klarna reduced customer support headcount by 700 people using LLMs and custom workflows while improving all quality metrics.
13. AI-driven buyouts of services companies are a viable new strategy: acquire distribution, transform cost structure with AI.
14. Incubation is newly attractive as AI compresses the cost of building early product.
15. The mismatch between AI technologists and domain experts is a primary bottleneck for high-impact application development.

## Capabilities Referenced

- **Scaling laws** — more data and scale objectively improve model capability and output quality. (maturity: broad_production)
- **Transformer generalizability** — one model trained to perform across biology, robotics, language, image, video — displacing the era of specialized models. (maturity: broad_production)
- **LLM-based customer support automation** — Klarna case demonstrates 87% headcount reduction with superior quality metrics at scale. (maturity: narrow_production)
- **API-accessible AI** — no MLOps or training infrastructure required; global accessibility via simple API call. (maturity: broad_production)
- **Administrative task automation** — "email jobs" (data entry, CRM updates, email responses) automatable to yield ~10x employee leverage. (maturity: narrow_production)
- **On-device small models** — smaller models running locally without cloud infrastructure. (maturity: narrow_production)

## Limitations and Open Questions

- **Models not yet coherent enough for all valuable tasks** — current LLMs still struggle with complex reasoning tasks at production quality. (severity: blocking, trajectory: improving)
- **Coding assistants provide incremental, not transformative, gains** — existing copilots deliver some efficiency but not the 5x threshold needed for large-scale cost structure transformation. (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)
- **Data quality misconceptions persist** — "data is the new oil" overstates data's role; model architecture and application design matter equally. (severity: significant, trajectory: stable)
- **Enterprise adoption is early-stage** — most companies are evaluating vendor tools; AI in customer-facing products is rare. (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)
- **AI/domain expert mismatch** — the people who understand AI capabilities are rarely the people who understand high-value domain problems. (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)
- **Startups too small to automate headcount** — a 6–8 person founding team has no headcount to reduce; AI automation effects are initially irrelevant at this scale. (severity: blocking, trajectory: improving)
- **Large-scale headcount reduction is 2–3 years away** — beyond narrow functions (SDRs, customer support), general workforce impact is not yet here. (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)
- **Operational and M&A skill gap in software teams** — engineering-led firms lack the PE/operational expertise to successfully acquire and transform services companies. (severity: significant, trajectory: stable)
- **Competitive dynamics question remains open** — whether AI gains concentrate (Amazon thesis) or diffuse (Covello thesis) depends on adoption rate asymmetries that are not yet observable at scale.

- **Transformer architecture as paradigm shift** — not an improvement on old ML but a qualitatively different regime: scalable, general-purpose, data-hungry in a productive way. (significance: paradigm_shifting)
