## Briefing

**BVP's investment framework for vertical AI argues that building a defensible vertical AI business requires going beyond traditional SaaS playbooks — particularly on questions of what to automate, how to avoid commoditization, and where durable competitive moats actually come from in a world where foundation models are rapidly becoming interchangeable.** The central thesis is that the technical moat no longer lives in the model itself but in multimodal workflow integration, proprietary data quality, and deep customization for niche buyer requirements — and that novel AI-native business models are unlocking market segments and margins previously inaccessible to vertical software.

### Key Takeaways
1. **Traditional SaaS frameworks are necessary but insufficient** — vertical AI introduces novel challenges around automation design, commoditization, and business model innovation that classic software playbooks don't address.
2. **The moat is not in the model** — competitive edge increasingly depends on combining data types and deep workflow integrations rather than proprietary model ownership, directly implicating the model commoditisation thesis.
3. **Customer automation appetite ≠ technical automation feasibility** — customers have varying (sometimes strict) requirements for which workflows they will and won't automate, and product design must honor this regardless of what's technically possible.
4. **Industry-structural bottlenecks block adoption independent of product quality** — healthcare diagnostics AI is technically viable but sees slower adoption because payment models haven't caught up, illustrating that go-to-market timing depends on non-technical factors.
5. **Administrative workflow AI gets adopted fastest** — healthcare scribing/documentation AI saw swift clinician adoption because clinicians actively want to offload administrative burden, signaling that "pain relief" automation beats "capability augmentation" in early adoption curves.
6. **Data quality compounds; quantity does not** — early success depends on high-quality, relevant domain data, which becomes a self-reinforcing advantage as the business scales.
7. **Novel pricing and delivery models are the economic unlock** — AI automation enables new business model structures (outcome-based pricing, AI-enabled services) that access broader market segments and expand margins beyond what traditional SaaS licensing allows.
8. **Modular infrastructure is a strategic requirement** — locking into any single model is a liability; systems must be architected to swap in better models as the AI landscape evolves, treating foundation models as interchangeable commodities.
9. **Niche, underserved markets are the beachhead** — initial competitive advantage typically comes from overlooked high-ROI areas where incumbents haven't gone, not from competing directly in crowded categories.
10. **Complexity requirements are a moat** — compliance, security, and other nuanced enterprise buyer requirements that competitors find costly to satisfy become defensible barriers when built into the core product.
11. **Superhuman-scale tasks are the highest-value AI wedge** — the most differentiated applications are those where AI operates at speeds or scales genuinely unattainable by humans, not merely automating existing human workflows.
12. **Quantifiable ROI is the adoption flywheel** — demonstrating clear revenue gains or cost reductions, rather than abstract capability, is what drives both initial adoption and long-term customer loyalty.

### What Makes Vertical AI Distinct from Traditional Vertical SaaS

- Traditional vertical software principles still apply to vertical AI, but they are only part of the foundation needed to build a durable AI-native business.
  - Lessons from industry-defining vertical SaaS companies remain relevant for product strategy, go-to-market, and customer development.
  - However, novel considerations around automation design, commoditization dynamics, and AI-native business models require a new layer of thinking that has no direct SaaS precedent.
- **Vertical AI is still in an early chapter**, but a cohort of prescient founders who built before the current AI wave provides initial blueprints.
  - EvenUp (2019), Subtle Medical (2017), Abridge (2018), and Fieldguide (2020) all predated the mainstream AI era.
  - These companies succeeded by identifying domains where AI — once capable enough — could generate ROI that traditional software fundamentally could not, not just do existing things faster.
- The framework is organized around four dimensions: **functional value, economic value, competitive position, and defensibility** — a structure that implicitly treats AI as a product layer, business model layer, and moat layer simultaneously.

### Automation Strategy: Build Where Customers Actually Want AI

- **Technical automability is not sufficient justification** for building an automated workflow — customer desire and readiness must be independently validated.
  - A workflow that is trivial to automate technically may face strong customer resistance or regulatory constraints that make it a poor product bet.
  - The principle: "just because something can be automated doesn't mean it should be or that the conditions are right."
- **Customer automation preferences are granular and must be embedded in product design**, not treated as binary.
  - Example: a dental office may want fully automated procurement below a cost threshold but require human review for larger purchases — an AI procurement tool must support both modes natively.
  - Example: a law firm may fully automate payment workflows but use human-in-the-loop for brief drafting because of client expectations or liability concerns about the final product.

## Key Claims

1. Traditional vertical software frameworks are necessary but not sufficient for building vertical AI businesses
2. Several leading vertical AI companies were founded before the current AI era by identifying where AI could eventually solve problems traditional software could not
3. Vertical AI is in an early stage, but blueprints of success already exist
4. A workflow's technical propensity for automation is not the only consideration when deciding what to build
5. Customers have varying degrees of interest in automation and may have strict specific requirements even for workflows that seem ideal for full automation
6. Healthcare AI solutions addressing administrative workflows like scribing have seen swift adoption because clinicians actively want to automate those tasks
7. Healthcare AI diagnostic solutions have experienced slower adoption because payment models have lagged behind technological innovation
8. Industry-specific bottlenecks such as payment models can block adoption of technically viable AI solutions
9. Technical competitive moat in vertical AI increasingly depends on combining data types and workflow integrations rather than proprietary models alone
10. High-quality, relevant data compounds in value as a vertical AI business scales

## Capabilities

- AI-powered administrative workflow automation in healthcare (e.g., scribing) has achieved swift clinical adoption, driven by strong clinician demand to eliminate administrative burden
- Vertical AI systems can implement configurable human-in-the-loop thresholds within a single workflow — fully automating low-risk sub-tasks while routing high-stakes decisions to humans based on cost, risk, or compliance criteria
- Multimodal AI applications for clinical diagnostics are technically functional and generating practitioner interest, but clinical adoption is constrained by payment model misalignment rather than capability gaps
- AI can automate end-to-end legal payment workflows and generate initial drafts of legal briefs under human oversight, enabling partial workflow automation in a liability-constrained professional domain

## Limitations

- Healthcare AI diagnostic adoption is capped by reimbursement model misalignment — technical capability exists but commercial deployment is blocked because payment structures haven't adapted to AI-generated diagnostic outputs
- Proprietary foundation models alone are insufficient to build a durable competitive moat in vertical AI — model-level differentiation is being rapidly commoditized, forcing defensibility into workflow integration depth and multimodal data combinations
- Technical automation feasibility does not map directly to deployment readiness — industry-specific bottlenecks (regulatory, payment, customer acceptance) create adoption ceilings independent of AI performance levels
- Core high-stakes professional workflows (legal drafting, clinical decision-making) cannot be fully automated even when technically feasible — professional liability, client expectations, and accountability norms enforce persistent human-in-the-loop requirements
- Vertical AI systems require high-quality, domain-specific curated data that is scarce and difficult to acquire early — generic pretraining corpora are insufficient, creating a data cold-start barrier for new entrants
- AI features built directly on foundation model capabilities face rapid commoditization pressure — well-resourced competitors can replicate feature-level functionality quickly, eroding differentiation for builders who don't integrate deeply into workflows
- Healthcare AI adoption is implicitly bifurcated — administrative workflows achieve production adoption while diagnostic and clinical workflows stagnate, revealing a structural ceiling on AI's near-term value capture in healthcare that is almost entirely absent from the text's framing
- Vertical AI technical infrastructure must be rebuilt or significantly re-architected as foundation models evolve — teams that hard-code dependencies on specific model versions or vendors face substantial rework overhead

## Bottlenecks

- Healthcare reimbursement and payment models have not adapted to AI-generated diagnostic outputs, creating a commercial viability ceiling for multimodal diagnostic AI despite demonstrated technical capability
- Industry-specific compliance, security, and regulatory requirements in verticals (healthcare, legal, financial) impose high customisation and certification overhead that slows scaling and raises the capital cost of vertical AI deployment
- Most high-value industry vertical workflows remain unaddressed by specialised AI — the ecosystem is in an early chapter with a large untapped surface area, but absence of domain experts who understand both AI and industry context limits build rate
