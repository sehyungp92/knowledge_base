Importance of Why Now

- When you have a strong why now, it's like a strong current that just pushes the company forward, and then the best founders take great currents and maximise that opportunity. When you don't have enough of a real why now, it almost feels like you're in a boat and you're having to paddle really hard but not make a lot of progress.
	- If the current pulling the company is so strong, it gives the company additional resilience and mistakes almost do not matter.
	- A clear why now doesn't necessarily mean a trend, it can be an opportunity that's catalysed by a new technology like we're seeing now with AI. 

Perspectives on AI

- Everybody talks about AI as something that advantages the incumbents, and when it’s advantages the incumbents instead of the disruptors, i.e., the startups, then it tends to be seen a sustaining technology.
- It's always been a race for the incumbent to get innovation before the startup gets distribution, and in the case of AI it has been easy for incumbents to innovate because for this first wave of AI use cases, it has effectively involved using APIs from the likes of OpenAI
- If AI is going to be for existing employees and their existing workflows, it’s going to be sustaining and it's going to drive tremendous market cap for the incumbents. But that doesn't mean that there aren't disruptive opportunities for startups leveraging AI. The difference is that the mental model we have been trained to think about startups for a very long time has to change. 
- For the last 25 years, we're so used to thinking about application software as a means for productivity improvement. But if you realise that what AI enables a company to actually sell a very different unit of work, namely the work actually done. And so you're almost a software company that looks like a services business that is able to sell the full work product or the outcome, as opposed to selling a software that an employee has to learn to use and gain a productivity boost.
	- This is likely to be very disruptive to incumbents because incumbents are used to thinking about selling per seat and not pricing per seat based on the cost of the headcount.
	- Can almost think about it as unbundling an employee, so what are the different work products an employee has to do.
- For an AI startup, the market for selling work could actually be 10-50x larger than if you were selling software because you’re essentially selling an ~95% productivity improvement instead of a ~10% productivity improvement. Therefore, you're essentially selling against the cost of the headcount as opposed to a productivity improvement for that headcount. That will also be associated with an easier go-to-market because you're not asking an employee to adopt something new, but instead just selling a package of work
- The high valuation or entry price of an investment in AI may be a rational decision given the size of the potential opportunity. And it may be that investors accept the upfront dilution given the capital intensity required to go after a tremendously big market by gaining a competitive advantage and creating a moat, where the capital intensity required to compete almost serves as a moat itself.
- While there is no question that as the foundation models improve, we're going to see the ability to take on more complex tasks, there are certainly use cases in the spectrum that can be automated right now. 
- There is also the question of having a human in the loop, preferably your own employee as the AI software provider, which is the way that you see some companies bridging the gap right now until the models are able to do it on their own.
- If you divide the world between the incumbents and the hungry non-incumbents that want to become the incumbents, more often than not, new technologies are best served by the group of hungry newcomers that want to become the new incumbents. And there’s likely to be a class of companies that realise that if there are early adopters of this technology, they'll be able to come in with a very different pricing structure, do things faster, and have a cost advantage that they pass on to their customer that lets them grow their market share
- There's now a new wave of companies that have internalised what's possible with AI that are coming into the market with a very different distribution of value. They're owning more of the workflow, doing more of the work to create a new experience.

Perspectives on the Application Layer

- From the perspective of who owns the user over time, huge believer that the application layer is where a tremendous amount of value gets ends up being captured and created.
- The goal is to ultimately build a company that can be independent and enduring and imagining what that might look like in the future and pulling forward that future into the present to think about the dynamics of the company and whether or not it has the type of dynamics that lead to those types of outcomes. 
- Given the intense competition, differentiation and separating from other competitors may also be the single hardest question in AI right now, where there seems to be a bit of a land grab.
	- It may be that there’s even greater emphasis placed on a founder who has that competitive energy, high urgency, aggressiveness and ambition that will let them navigate a very competitive ecosystem and emerge as the victor. It's going to be a very competitive next few years
- In order to escape competition you may want to lean heavily into ideas like network effects for instance. In the case of B2B software, economies of scale would be more relevant, where you build a product for a very specific use case, execute on the go-to-market to grow that product into the hands of a lot of users, then add more and more features over time to that product so that you can charge more, giving the company a positive net revenue retention and a go-to-market that can afford to

## Key Claims

1. Frontier AI models will be closed source due to the enormous capital required for training
2. Foundation model training is currently compute and power constrained
3. Each successive frontier model will be more expensive to train than its predecessor
4. The escalating cost of training frontier models points toward an oligopolistic model landscape
5. Each step-function improvement in foundation models costs approximately 10x more than the previous generation to train
6. Inference costs for end customers are continuing to decrease as competition in the model layer intensifies
7. Power consumption is becoming a significant constraint for both AI training and inference
8. Meta's open sourcing of Llama could fundamentally change the AI model landscape by making frontier-class models freely available
9. The application layer will capture the majority of value in the AI ecosystem because it owns the end user relationship
10. First-wave AI startups functioned primarily as wrappers around LLMs, with the underlying model provider capturing most of the product value

## Capabilities

- Automation of specific work tasks including HR operations, recruiting, sales, and other 'unbundled' employee functions
- High-quality instant machine translation with human-level output quality
- Foundation model training by individual companies to develop competitive advantages

## Limitations

- Human-in-the-loop validation and QA still required for AI-automated work; systems cannot reliably complete complex tasks end-to-end without human oversight
- Application-layer AI companies struggle to capture and retain value; depend on improvements to underlying foundation models for core capability
- Economic viability of open-sourcing frontier foundation models remains unproven; business model for monetizing open-source frontier models unclear
- Enterprise adoption of AI applications remains limited; mainstream enterprise customers not yet adopting AI solutions at scale
- Power consumption is becoming a binding constraint on both training and inference at scale
- Successive generations of frontier models require exponentially higher training costs (10x per step function), creating severe barriers to entry

## Bottlenecks

- Compute and power constraints blocking progress on frontier foundation models; scaling frontier models requires massive capital investment in specialized hardware and power infrastructure, leading to oligopoly consolidation
- Unresolved business model for open-source frontier models; inability to monetize massive R&D investment in training creates pressure toward closed-source models for frontier capabilities
- Enterprise integration and operational barriers limiting adoption of AI applications to SMB and mid-market; incumbent sales forces, procurement processes, and organizational inertia slowing enterprise deployment

## Breakthroughs

- Meta's strategy of investing heavily in open-source foundation models (Llama) while maintaining economic viability, potentially resolving the closed-source monopoly on frontier capabilities
