# Shardul Shah: How Index Makes Decisions & Why Benchmarks & Averages in VC are BS | E1202

Shardul Shah (Index Ventures) reflects on the craft of venture investing: why there is no universal playbook, how power law dynamics render average-seeking strategies counterproductive, and what distinguishes genuine conviction from delusion. The conversation is grounded in specific decisions at Index, including multi-stage bets on Datadog and Wiz, and offers a frank postmortem of the firm's most costly mistakes of omission.

**Authors:** Shardul Shah
**Published:** 2024-09-16
**Type:** Video / Podcast

---

## The Absence of a Universal Playbook

One of the central arguments Shah makes is that there is no correct approach to venture. The mistake most new investors make is copying styles that are not authentically theirs. The differentiated edge in [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC]] comes from identifying where you are uniquely great across the three pillars of sourcing, selecting, and servicing, then leaning into that rather than conforming to perceived norms.

This extends to firm culture. Shah describes the spirit at Index as one of belonging rather than assimilation: people must be themselves for the firm to reach its best potential. Assimilation trades individual signal for conformity and sets the firm up for mediocrity.

---

## Power Law as the Dominant Structure

Shah is explicit that the power law dominates venture returns at every stage, including late-stage growth investing and even public market indexes (where, at the time of the interview, seven companies were driving broad index returns). The structural implication is direct:

> The primary source of pain is not making bad investments; it is not being in the $10–100 billion companies.

This renders several conventional risk-management exercises problematic:

- **Pattern recognition** applied to downside protection becomes an exercise in psychological safety rather than a return driver.
- **Benchmarking and averages** are structurally misleading in a power law distribution. Targeting 3–5x over 3–5 years (Index's initial growth fund hypothesis) proved wrong because the actual distribution had very few companies in that band. The correct target is 5x+ even at late-stage, high-price entry points.
- **Price sensitivity** is a value-investing instinct misapplied to venture. If you have conviction in a category-defining company, paying up is rarely the error. Index's investments in Datadog and Wiz were made at high prices across multiple rounds; Shah describes each as decisions he feels confident about.

The asymmetry runs in both directions: sins of omission (not being in generational companies) have been far more costly for Index than sins of commission.

---

## TAM as a Trap

TAM analysis is characterised as one of the most misleading frameworks in [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|startup evaluation]]. The market caps of the largest public companies now exceed what their S-1 TAM analyses projected as the total addressable market. The best founders do not operate within predefined market boundaries; they find and expand market opportunities. Treating TAM as a ceiling pre-selects against the companies most likely to generate fund-returning outcomes.

---

## Building and Stress-Testing Conviction

Shah draws a careful distinction between conviction and delusion. Delusion presents as:

- Laziness: skipping detail work, not redoing analysis
- Being surprised by new information
- Stopping to ask questions

Conviction, by contrast, survives structured challenge. When Index debates an investment and conviction is unchanged after genuine disagreement, that is a signal to go deeper rather than to capitulate.

For follow-on decisions, Index does not simply average down cost basis. The process is a full reconstruction of the investment case: rebuilding financial models, rotating shadow partners to generate a fresh objective view on the management team, redoing customer calls, and reassessing competitive dynamics from scratch. The investment question is not "do we believe in this company" but "can this become a fund returner from here?"

Domain expertise introduces a specific risk: deep knowledge in a concentration area can cause investors to be **dismissively intuitive** toward disruptive ideas within that domain, precisely because fluency creates the illusion of having already considered and rejected novel angles. This is an under-discussed limitation of specialised VC strategies.

---

## Founder Selection Criteria

Shah identifies three characteristics he looks for in founders: imagination, operational excellence, and high-quality decision-making. He notes that this combination is rare in a single individual. The emphasis on decision quality is consistent with the broader framework: at a firm level, the quality of a small number of decisions determines nearly all outcomes.

---

## Capital Intensity as a Structural Risk

One category of investment mistake Shah flags is underestimating capital intensity. Some business models have real capital needs and can fail along two distinct failure modes:

1. Founders are unable to raise sufficient capital at the right time.
2. The distribution model constrains growth to below the level needed to overcome capital requirements.

In these cases, even high conviction on market upside can be wrong if the capital structure does not support the trajectory required.

## Key Claims

| Claim | Implication |
|---|---|
| TAM projections routinely understate the markets of the largest companies | TAM-gating at early stage is a structural error |
| Power law dominates at every stage of venture | Optimising for average returns is the wrong objective function |
| 5x+ is the correct minimum target even at growth-stage, high-price entry | Late-stage VC is not value investing |
| Deep domain expertise raises the risk of dismissing disruptive ideas | Specialisation requires deliberate countermeasures |
| Sins of omission exceed sins of commission at Index | Caution around generational founders is more costly than overpaying |
| Delusion is identifiable by laziness and surprise at new information | Conviction and rigour are not in tension |

## Open Questions

- How do you operationalise the distinction between intuition-as-signal and intuition-as-dismissal, particularly in concentrated domain expertise? Shah acknowledges the risk but does not offer a systematic solution.
- The 5x+ targeting framework at growth stage implies significant concentration and volatility in fund outcomes. What portfolio construction constraints does this require?
- Shah describes rebuilding the investment case from scratch for follow-ons, but gives limited detail on how shadow partner rotation is structured to avoid anchoring on the existing internal narrative.
