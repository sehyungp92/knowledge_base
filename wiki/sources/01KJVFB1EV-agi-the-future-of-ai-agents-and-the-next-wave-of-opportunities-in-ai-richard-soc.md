## Analysis

The Age of AI 

- The AI era may be the Industrial Revolution, Renaissance, and the Enlightenment combined\. 
	- Similar to the Renaissance, there seems to be a new excitement around research\.
	- Similar to enlightenment, there is a new way to gather knowledge, summarise it, categorise it, and increase the amount of knowledge\. 
	- And then it's also a mix of the industrial age in that it's enabling new level of productivity that makes humanity much more interesting overall and changes the human condition entirely\. 
		- 150 years ago, 90%\+ of people worked in agriculture, but now only 5% of people have to work in agriculture, while there is still an abundance of food\. 
- In the current age of AI, similar increases in productivity are true for repetitive intellectual labour\. 
	- Any job that involves doing the same thing over and over again, people should now think about how to hand that off to an AI agent, how to prompt this AI to do that work on your behalf, and how to cover all the corner cases and the tools that should be used to do the repetitive process in my workflow, and then just tell it to the AI to do it\. 
		- That represents a whole new step function of human productivity and hence changes the human condition\. And so, humanity may be entering a new age\. 
- In the beginning of the AI revolution, the bottom half of performers may benefit the most\. 
	- For instance, currently the top programmers do not actually benefit that much from AI, but they're informing AI a lot and they're giving better training data to it\. 
- Over time that will probably shift, and at some point, AI may eventually replace the work of a below average performer in a lot of different intellectual jobs\. And hence, there may be no need for anyone but the top people\. 
	- And then, there's the question of how to train top people because not everyone starts to be one of the top people in the field right away, but there will always be a need for the top people in almost any field because the AI needs to be trained\. 
	- Also, new things happen, so if there's new hardware, new capabilities, then new kinds of code have to be written, new kinds of frameworks have to be written, and human ingenuity will happen\. 
- Ultimately, we're going to all work on much higher levels of abstraction and compositionality\. 
	- Just thinking about programming, you used to have to program in assembly language, and now in C\+\+ and C\# and eventually Java and now Python, and now within a few lines of code, you can spin up an entire web server and it's not that complex anymore\. 
	- With AI, it’s just going to get to another level where you can build even more complex things\. 
- Succeeding in the age of AI will involve understanding the problem at a deep foundational level, and then architecting and orchestrating employees, some of which might be AI agents\. 
- One way to help predict the near\-term future is to look at what the wealthiest and most successful people have in terms of access to goods and services and products, and where can AI technology have a significant impact\. 
	- For instance, AI may become a personal tutor for kids\. Even though it is easy to learn something now online, people may be able to do it in an even more personalised level that only the wealthier people can afford with personal tutors\. 
	- The wealthy may have personal health teams or a personal assistant, which can now be roles that can be played by AI\. For instance, artists on big projects may have many assistants, and now every artist can easily afford a lot of assistance with AI, and can focus on work involving higher levels of abstraction, such as finding interesting ideas\. The same will be true for programmers\. 
- Jevons paradox of LLMs: GPT\-4 and 4o token costs dropped by almost 80% over the course of a year\. One may think that cheaper LLMs may result in a lot of AI cost savings across organisations\. However, Jevons paradox suggests we will just use more of it and automate more processes\.
	- As intelligence gets increasingly cheaper, the jobs that currently require intelligence will not all disappear, but we will just use intelligence in more places\. Overall, the amount of tasks that require decent amount of intelligence will skyrocket\.

	- With a lot of the top models, you can't add multiple orders of magnitude more text to the training set because there isn't that much more text to add as the frontiers companies have already scraped most of what they can legally or generally find on the internet\.

## Key Claims

1. As the marginal cost of intelligence decreases, the number of tasks perceived to require intelligence will skyrocket rather than total demand collapsing (Jevons Paradox applied to AI).
2. Scaling laws will not continue exponentially but will flatten into a logarithmic leveling-off as additional data yields diminishing returns on reasoning ability.
3. Frontier model companies have largely exhausted the legally accessible text data available on the internet.
4. Once Llama 4 is released, open-source models will be sufficient for the majority of AI workflows.
5. Foundational LLM companies may resemble telecom infrastructure: they create enormous value for the world but cannot capture it, as open-source rapidly erodes their competitive moat.
6. The marginal cost of intelligence is actively and continuously decreasing.
7. LLM costs are declining fast enough that spending engineering resources on cost optimization over the past year and a half would have been a strategic mistake.
8. AI agents will surpass humans as the dominant source of web traffic within the next few years.
9. Multi-step agent accuracy degrades multiplicatively: a 95%-accurate agent executing 15 steps succeeds only ~46% of the time.
10. AI agents that write and execute arbitrary code present significant security risks, including unauthorized resource abuse.

## Capabilities

- LLM API costs have declined dramatically; models are becoming commoditized with open-source alternatives achieving frontier-level performance
- Multi-LLM orchestration: systems can route queries dynamically between different language models based on query type, cost, and latency optimization
- Intent-based query routing: systems can classify query intent (factual-with-citations, creative, financial ticker, etc.) and route to appropriate processing pipelines
- Accurate retrieval-augmented generation with verified citations: RAG systems that cite specific sources and mark exact locations in source material
- Multi-turn conversational context tracking: systems can maintain conversation history, reformulate queries based on context, and track referents across turns
- Web-surfing agents: AI agents can autonomously browse the web, decide whether to search, extract information, and execute multi-step web-based tasks
- Code-writing agents: agents can generate code, decide to execute it, and complete programming tasks autonomously
- Enterprise workflow automation: AI agents can automate repetitive workflows within enterprise SaaS platforms (Salesforce, Word, Excel, PowerPoint)
- Consumer subscription-based AI: AI-powered answer/productivity tools can generate sustainable revenue from knowledge workers willing to pay for complex, accurate answers

## Limitations

- Scaling laws will eventually plateau as returns diminish: further increases in data/compute will produce logarithmic rather than exponential capability gains
- High-quality internet text data is nearly exhausted at the frontier: major AI labs have already crawled most legally accessible public internet text
- Long-horizon agentic task accuracy degrades sharply with task length: 95%-accurate agent fails ~50% of the time on 15-step tasks due to error compounding
- Hallucinated citations in retrieval-augmented systems: competitor systems produce false, fabricated citations that appear credible but link to irrelevant sources
- Agentic code execution creates critical security vulnerabilities: agents writing and executing arbitrary code enable credential theft, system compromise, and malicious activities
- Prompt length optimization is critical: adding excess retrieved context to prompts significantly increases latency, cost, and model quality degradation
- Simple query domains lack differentiation: consumer search questions (factual lookups, score checking) show minimal capability gap between LLM and traditional search solutions
- Geographically constrained profitability: some countries cannot profitably run search+LLM stacks due to low advertising purchasing power
- LLM output lacks metacognitive direction: current agents execute next-token prediction robotically without ability to set their own objective functions or self-reflect on task relevance
- Retrieval quality dependency: LLM accuracy is directly constrained by search backend quality; poor retrieval leads to irrecoverable hallucinated facts
- Foundational model value capture declining: open-source model release by competitors erodes proprietary advantage and pricing power, similar to telecom infrastructure
- Hardware acceleration ROI uncertainty: new hardware claims become obsolete before deployment; Nvidia's iterative GPU improvements outpace competing architectures

## Bottlenecks

- Scaling law saturation: further data/compute scaling will produce diminishing returns; no breakthrough can overcome fundamental limits on exponential growth
- Internet-scale text data exhaustion: all legally accessible public internet text has been crawled by frontier labs; continued scaling requires synthetic data or other modalities
- Long-horizon agentic task accuracy degrades multiplicatively: 95% per-step accuracy yields 50% failure rate on 15-step tasks; 50+ steps almost always fail
- Search backend quality criticality: LLM accuracy is bounded by retrieval quality; no post-hoc LLM processing can recover from bad search results
- Agentic code execution security: agents writing and executing arbitrary code cannot be safely deployed without comprehensive sandboxing and oversight mechanisms
- Consumer AI unit economics mismatch: simple factual queries show minimal LLM advantage over traditional search; cannot achieve 10x differentiation needed for consumer willingness to pay
- Foundational model value capture: open-source LLM availability eliminates pricing power for proprietary frontier models; infrastructure economics dominate
- Multi-modal scaling law uncertainty: visual autoregressive models show power-law scaling but dimensionality bounds (speed of light causality, bounded sensor availability) create unknown constraints

## Breakthroughs

- Emergent capabilities from language model scaling: language models achieve entirely new task categories (reasoning, programming, mathematics, multimodal understanding) when scaled beyond threshold sizes
