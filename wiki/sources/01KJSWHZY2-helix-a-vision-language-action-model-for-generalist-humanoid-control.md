## Briefing

**Helix is Figure AI's Vision-Language-Action model that directly bridges internet-scale VLM knowledge into full humanoid upper-body control, achieving zero-shot generalization to thousands of novel objects using only ~500 hours of training data — a fraction of what prior VLA systems require. Its core insight is a decoupled "System 1, System 2" architecture that resolves the longstanding speed-generalization tradeoff in robotics, running the slow semantic reasoner and the fast motor controller in asynchronous parallel on embedded hardware. This matters because it represents the first plausible path to scaling home robotics without the per-task demonstration bottleneck that has made the problem intractable.**

### Key Takeaways
1. **Speed-generalization tradeoff resolved** — A decoupled S1/S2 architecture lets a 7-9 Hz VLM handle semantics while an 80M parameter transformer executes precise 200 Hz motor control, matching single-task policy speed while generalizing zero-shot across thousands of novel objects.
2. **First VLA for full upper-body humanoid control** — Helix outputs 35-DoF continuous control including individual finger flexion and abduction, wrist poses, torso orientation, and head gaze simultaneously at 200 Hz — no prior VLA has done this at real-time rates.
3. **Multi-robot coordination via shared weights** — Two Figure robots running identical Helix weights coordinate collaborative grocery storage tasks with entirely novel objects using only natural language role prompts, with no robot-specific training or explicit role assignments.
4. **Emergent "pick up anything" capability** — Helix handles thousands of novel household items in clutter from a simple "Pick up the [X]" prompt, including bridging abstract language to objects (e.g., "desert item" → toy cactus selection and grasp).
5. **Data efficiency is striking** — ~500 hours of teleoperated data, less than 5% of prior VLA dataset sizes, produces stronger generalization across a higher-dimensional action space, comparable in scale to single-task imitation learning datasets.
6. **Single weight set for all behaviors** — No task-specific fine-tuning, no separate action heads, no multi-stage training; one 7B + 80M model handles picking, placing, drawer operation, refrigerator use, and cross-robot handovers.
7. **Commercial deployment is immediate** — Helix runs entirely onboard dual embedded low-power GPUs on Figure robots — the first VLA to do so — making it deployable without cloud compute or high-power infrastructure.
8. **Hindsight auto-labeling solves the language annotation problem** — Training labels are generated by prompting a VLM with "What instruction would you have given the robot?" on teleoperation video clips, avoiding expensive human annotation of natural language intents.
9. **Temporal offset training closes the sim-to-real gap at inference** — The training process deliberately mirrors the latency difference between S2 and S1 during deployment, so the model learns to cope with the asynchronous timing it will encounter in the real world.
10. **Action tokenization is a dead end for humanoids** — Prior VLA approaches using discrete action tokens have shown limited success even for binarized parallel grippers; Helix's continuous regression output avoids this bottleneck entirely for 35-DoF spaces.
11. **Head and torso control is a historically unsolved feedback problem** — Their coupled effect on both reachability and visibility creates instability that prior systems avoided; Helix is claimed to be the first to solve this in a generalizable VLA setting.

### The Home as Robotics' Unsolved Scaling Problem
- The core argument is that the home is categorically harder than industrial settings — not just quantitatively more objects, but qualitatively different: deformable, varied, semantically complex items in unpredictable clutter.
  - Teaching a robot one new behavior today requires either extensive expert programming or thousands of human demonstrations, both prohibitively expensive when scaled to the full object diversity of a household.
  - The current paradigm "will not scale to the home without a step change" — no incremental improvement in demonstration efficiency closes the gap; a fundamentally different approach is required.
- The proposed step change is leveraging the common-sense knowledge already captured in internet-pretrained VLMs and translating it directly into robot actions.
  - This reframes the core robotics challenge: instead of teaching individual skills, the problem becomes extracting and grounding VLM semantics into generalizable control.
  - If successful, skills that previously required hundreds of demonstrations become obtainable instantly through natural language.

### System 1 / System 2 Architecture: Resolving the Speed-Generalization Tradeoff
- Prior VLA approaches faced a fundamental tradeoff: VLM backbones are general but slow; visuomotor policies are fast but narrow.
  - Helix decouples these concerns into two systems that are **trained end-to-end together but operate at different timescales**.
- **System 2 (S2)** is a 7B-parameter open-source, open-weight VLM pretrained on internet-scale data, running at 7-9 Hz.
  - Processes monocular robot camera images and robot state (wrist pose, finger positions) by projecting them into vision-language embedding space.
  - Combined with natural language task commands, S2 distills all semantic task-relevant information into **a single continuous latent vector** — not tokens, not a distribution, just one dense representation.
  - This latent vector is the only communication channel from S2 to S1.
- **System 1 (S1)** is an 80M parameter cross-attention encoder-decoder transformer running at 200 Hz.

## Key Claims

1. Helix is the first VLA to output high-rate continuous control of the entire humanoid upper body, including wrists, torso, head, and individual fingers.
2. Helix is the first VLA to operate simultaneously on two robots, enabling them to solve a shared, long-horizon manipulation task with novel items.
3. Figure robots equipped with Helix can pick up virtually any small household object, including thousands of items never encountered during training, via natural language prompts.
4. Helix uses a single set of neural network weights for all behaviors without any task-specific fine-tuning.
5. Helix is the first VLA that runs entirely onboard embedded low-power-consumption GPUs, making it commercially deployable.
6. Teaching robots a single new behavior currently requires either hours of PhD-level expert manual programming or thousands of demonstrations.
7. Helix's System 2 is an internet-pretrained VLM operating at 7-9 Hz for scene understanding and language comprehension.
8. Helix's System 1 is a fast reactive visuomotor policy that translates latent semantic representations from S2 into continuous robot actions at 200 Hz.
9. Helix matches the speed of specialized single-task behavioral cloning policies while generalizing zero-shot to thousands of novel test objects.
10. Prior VLA action tokenization schemes face scaling challenges with high-dimensional humanoid control, having shown success only in low-dimensional setups like binarized parallel grippers.

## Capabilities

- High-rate continuous control of the entire humanoid upper body at 200Hz across a 35-DoF action space — individual finger flexion/abduction, wrist poses, torso, and head — while generalising zero-shot across tasks
- Zero-shot pick-and-place of virtually any small household object via natural language prompt, including thousands of items never seen during training
- Multi-robot collaborative manipulation of novel objects using identical model weights, coordinated via natural language — no robot-specific training or explicit role-assignment logic
- Full VLA inference pipeline running onboard dual embedded low-power GPUs, matching the speed of single-task imitation learning policies — no cloud dependency
- Abstract concept-to-action bridging: robots interpret abstract natural-language descriptions (e.g. 'desert item') and map them to novel physical objects for precise dexterous grasping
- Single unified neural network (7B + 80M parameters, no per-task fine-tuning or dedicated action heads) covering picking, placing, drawer/refrigerator operation, and dexterous multi-robot handovers

## Limitations

- Only small household objects are demonstrated; large, heavy, bulky, or structurally complex items are conspicuously absent from results
- System 2 VLM runs at only 7-9 Hz, creating a semantic latency bottleneck for rapidly changing scenes, fast-moving objects, or high-frequency reactive decisions
- No quantitative success rates, failure analysis, or reliability metrics are reported — actual pick success rate and failure mode distribution are unknown
- Lower body control is entirely absent — Helix solves upper-body manipulation only, leaving locomotion, balance during manipulation, and whole-body coordination open problems
- Training still requires ~500 hours of human teleoperation; fully autonomous or self-supervised data collection is not demonstrated
- Multi-robot coordination requires externally scripted natural-language role assignments per step; emergent role negotiation or autonomous task decomposition is not demonstrated
- Monocular camera input only — no stereo depth, LiDAR, or tactile sensing integrated, limiting spatial precision and grasp robustness for edge cases
- Testing conducted in controlled lab/demo settings, not deployed in actual homes — real-world robustness under domestic lighting variation, occlusion, and unpredictable clutter is unproven
- Authors explicitly anticipate needing to scale by '1,000x and beyond', indicating current capability is far below the threshold required for reliable production home deployment
- Deformable object manipulation (clothing, soft items) is cited in the problem motivation but conspicuously absent from demonstrated results
- Human-robot safety — collision avoidance in human proximity, force limiting, and regulatory certification — is entirely absent from the technical description despite 'commercial-ready' claims
- Auto-labeling of training instructions via a VLM introduces label noise — the quality of language supervision depends on the labelling VLM's ability to accurately describe robot actions from video
- S1 visuomotor backbone is initialised from simulation-only pretraining, implying a latent sim-to-real gap that may limit performance on real-world textures, lighting, and physical dynamics

## Bottlenecks

- High-dimensional continuous action spaces for full humanoid control (35+ DoF with finger-level detail) have blocked VLA scaling — tokenisation schemes designed for binary parallel grippers cannot transfer
- Per-behavior data collection for robot training has required prohibitive human effort — either hours of PhD-level programming or thousands of demonstrations per skill
- Integrating VLM semantic generality with real-time low-latency motor control has been an unsolved architectural split — VLM backbones are general but slow; visuomotor policies are fast but narrow

## Breakthroughs

- Helix demonstrates the first VLA achieving zero-shot whole-body humanoid manipulation generalising across thousands of novel objects, running fully onboard at commercial speed — directly transferring internet-scale VLM semantics into high-dimensional motor control without task-specific fine-tuning
- Decoupled System 1/System 2 architecture trained end-to-end resolves the speed-generality tradeoff: a slow VLM at 7-9 Hz drives a fast reactive visuomotor policy at 200 Hz, with temporal offset in training explicitly calibrated to match deployment latency
