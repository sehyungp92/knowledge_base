# Moravec's Paradox

> Moravec's Paradox is the counterintuitive observation that high-level cognitive tasks long considered hallmarks of human intelligence (chess, legal reasoning, mathematical proof) are far easier for AI systems to achieve than low-level sensorimotor skills that any toddler masters effortlessly (clearing a table, navigating a cluttered room, catching a ball). First articulated in the 1980s by Hans Moravec and colleagues, the paradox cuts to the heart of embodied intelligence: the competencies most deeply encoded by hundreds of millions of years of animal evolution are precisely those that remain hardest to replicate in silicon.

## Overview

Moravec's Paradox frames a persistent asymmetry in AI capability: abstract reasoning over symbol systems is tractable; grounded, real-time sensorimotor control is not. The paradox is not merely historical curiosity. It actively shapes the frontier of modern foundation model research, explaining why models that can pass bar exams still fail to reliably pick up objects, navigate physical spaces, or follow indirect instructions in dynamic environments.

The paradox's force comes from an evolutionary argument. Low-level perception and motor control are the product of deep biological optimization across geological timescales; they are implemented in massively parallel, analog neural substrates that no explicit algorithm yet matches. High-level reasoning, by contrast, is evolutionarily recent and relatively "thin" in terms of dedicated neural machinery, which is why it turns out to be amenable to statistical compression over text corpora. Foundation models inherit this inverted competence profile: they are strong where evolution was weak, and weak where evolution was strong.

## Evidence from the Current Frontier

The trajectory of SIMA 2 offers a detailed case study in how the field is actively pressing against the paradox's constraints. The original SIMA 1 agent was a direct illustration of the problem: trained on annotated gameplay, it could only follow short, direct instructions; it could not reason about its own actions, could not respond in language, and displayed significant brittleness when instructions deviated even modestly from its training distribution. Its language module was trained from scratch, which meant its instruction-following was limited entirely to the vocabulary of annotated data it had seen. These are precisely the failure modes Moravec's Paradox predicts: a system optimized on a narrow sensorimotor dataset collapses when asked to generalize.

SIMA 2 attempts to dissolve the paradox rather than simply push past it, by grounding a capable language and reasoning model (Gemini Flash-Lite) directly in an embodied action loop. The architecture is deliberately constrained to the same interface a human would use: RGB video at 720p, keyboard and mouse actions, no privileged game state. This grounds the system in perceptual realism rather than bypassing it. The action space itself encodes the difficulty: 96 keyboard keys, mouse clicks, and discretized relative mouse movements, generated as structured text output. Getting a general-purpose language model to coordinate this action space reliably across diverse 3D environments is a non-trivial form of the sensorimotor grounding problem.

The training pipeline confronts the paradox's data dimension. Human gameplay does not naturally contain reasoning or dialogue, so SIMA 2 relies on "bridge data": synthetic annotations generated by Gemini Pro that inject causally consistent internal reasoning into recorded gameplay. This allows the model to be trained to simultaneously act, reason, and engage in dialogue, abilities that do not co-occur in raw behavioral data. The implication is significant: embodied intelligence at this level requires synthetic scaffolding because natural behavioral data, even in large quantities, does not contain the reasoning trace signal needed to generalize beyond the training distribution.

Maintaining a mixture of gameplay and non-gameplay Gemini pretraining data during finetuning proved essential. Without it, the model loses vision understanding, dialogue, and general reasoning, collapsing back toward a narrow sensorimotor policy. This is a concrete instantiation of the paradox's tension: optimizing for embodied performance tends to erode the abstract competencies that enable generalization, and vice versa.

## Self-Improvement as a Structural Response

One of the more striking responses to the paradox in SIMA 2 is the open-ended self-improvement loop. Rather than relying on hand-crafted reward functions or human annotation at scale, the system uses three foundation models (task setter, agent, reward model) alongside a general world model to autonomously acquire new skills in new environments. Gemini generates tasks and provides rewards; the agent attempts them; the reward model evaluates success. This loop operates without environment-specific engineering, which is critical given how brittle prior embodied systems have been to novel settings.

The RL phase deliberately excludes held-out environments (ASKA, MineDojo), which means the generalization results to those environments are not contaminated by the improvement loop. SIMA 2 does demonstrate robust generalization to previously unseen environments, including photorealistic worlds generated on-the-fly by Genie 3, and substantially closes the gap with human performance across a diverse game portfolio. This is meaningful progress. But "closes the gap" is not "closes the gap entirely," and the benchmark remains 3D games rather than unstructured physical reality, where the paradox bites hardest.

## Open Questions and Limits

Moravec's Paradox remains unresolved as a structural challenge. The SIMA 2 results demonstrate that large pretrained models with internal reasoning capabilities can be adapted to embodied settings with surprising generality, but the transfer is from virtual 3D environments to other virtual 3D environments. The harder question, raised directly in work by figures like Yann LeCun and Sergey Levine, is whether any system trained primarily on language and 2D visual data can ever acquire the grounded world model that low-level sensorimotor competence requires.

LeCun's position is that current architectures (autoregressive language models and their multimodal extensions) are fundamentally insufficient for this task: they lack the persistent world models, energy-based planning, and hierarchical goal structures that embodied intelligence requires. Levine's framing is more optimistic, arguing that robot learning is closer to viability than commonly assumed, but acknowledges that data scarcity and physical generalization remain genuine bottlenecks.

Several open questions structure the current frontier:

- **Simulation-to-reality transfer.** SIMA 2's generalization across virtual environments does not yet answer whether the same approach transfers to physical robotics, where contact dynamics, sensor noise, and irreversibility introduce qualitatively different challenges.
- **Data geometry.** Bridge data and synthetic reasoning annotations are powerful, but they are still approximations of the real grounding problem. How far synthetic scaffolding can carry a system before it hits the ceiling of what language-mediated reasoning can represent about physical action remains unknown.
- **The reasoning-action tradeoff.** Preserving pretraining mixture is necessary to avoid capability collapse, but this means the system is simultaneously optimizing objectives that pull in different directions. Whether a unified architecture can resolve this tension without permanent tradeoffs is an open architectural question.
- **Reward specification in open-ended environments.** Using a foundation model as a reward model is elegant but defers the alignment problem: the reward model's judgments must themselves be correct and generalizable, which is precisely hard in novel physical or quasi-physical settings.

## Significance

Moravec's Paradox is not just a historical framing device. It is an active organizing principle for understanding where the current wave of foundation model capabilities is likely to plateau. The paradox predicts that progress in abstract reasoning will continue to outpace progress in embodied sensorimotor intelligence, and the empirical record broadly confirms this. Systems like SIMA 2 represent the most serious current attempt to close the gap by importing abstract reasoning capabilities into an embodied loop, but the gap itself reflects a deep structural asymmetry between the kinds of tasks that statistical learning over human-generated data can solve and the kinds of tasks that require grounded physical interaction with an unpredictable world.
