## Briefing

**AI is dismantling the two structural moats — high switching costs and data lock-in — that have protected enterprise Systems of Record (ERP, CRM, HCM) for 30 years, enabling AI-native entrants to offer a genuinely 10x superior value proposition for the first time. The shift from "systems of record" to "systems of action" represents a once-in-a-generation platform disruption of a $400B+ software market, driven by agentic workflows that automate the human labor surrounding these systems rather than merely digitizing it.**

### Key Takeaways
1. **Implementation cost moat is collapsing** — AI code-gen agents are reducing the time to build custom SoR integrations by up to 90%, making rip-and-replace migrations dramatically cheaper and faster than ever before.
2. **Data moat is being bypassed passively** — AI tools like Day.ai and Attio accumulate CRM-quality data automatically from emails, Slack, and calls, reaching near-parity with incumbent data stores without requiring manual entry.
3. **The "wedge then replace" strategy is the dominant go-to-market pattern** — successful AI-native entrants live alongside incumbents first, build data parity, then offer customers an elegant offramp rather than forcing a painful cold migration.
4. **Adjacent human labor spend is the real prize** — AI-native SoRs are capturing legal services, system integrator, and professional services budgets, not just software license budgets, making their ROI case fundamentally stronger than prior platform shifts.
5. **Market timing is structurally favorable** — end-of-life deadlines for on-premise SoRs are clustering, contracts are synchronized, and C-suite AI mandates are creating a simultaneous demand pull across enterprise buyers.
6. **Agentic workflows automate entire business processes, not just features** — procurement, cash flow forecasting, and sales operations are being put on "autopilot," representing a qualitative leap over prior cloud-era value propositions.
7. **Domain-specific language (DSL) agents are unlocking walled garden ecosystems** — companies like CurieTech (MuleSoft) and Mando (Workday) write integration scripts autonomously, breaking vendor lock-in from within.
8. **Natural language configuration eliminates the need for system integrators** — ERP companies like Doss allow non-technical users to describe workflows in plain language and have AI translate them into UI and workflow code, collapsing implementation cycles.
9. **Voice AI is an unexpected wedge into emergency response SoR** — 911 call center automation companies are building data moats in computer-aided dispatch systems using voice AI as the initial value delivery mechanism.
10. **The value equation has inverted** — switching pain previously exceeded switching gain; AI-native products have flipped this calculus, with the benefit now clearly outweighing migration cost for the first time in decades.
11. **Downmarket-first is the recommended GTM** — starting with customers incumbents ignored, then moving upmarket as feature parity is achieved, is the preferred trajectory for new entrants.
12. **The $50B+ maintenance ecosystem is a vulnerability** — annual SoR maintenance spend on implementation, configuration, and upskilling is as large as some software markets and is now addressable by AI-enabled services firms.

### The $400B SoR Market and Why It Has Been Untouchable

- Enterprise Systems of Record (ERP, CRM, HCM, supply chain) represent an estimated **$400 billion in annual IT spend** as of 2023, making this one of the largest addressable software markets in existence.
  - Beyond software licensing, an additional **$50+ billion is spent annually** on SoR maintenance: implementation, configuration, integration, troubleshooting, and staff upskilling.
  - The incumbents — SAP, Oracle NetSuite, Salesforce, Workday — have dominated their respective categories for decades with near-impenetrable competitive positions.

- The dominant moats are structural, not merely reputational.
  - **Moat 1: High upfront implementation and customization costs.** Enterprise SoR deployments require long, expensive cycles with specialized system integrators; switching means enduring this pain twice.
  - **Moat 2: Data centralization.** After years of manual data entry, the SoR becomes the single source of truth for business-critical data that is practically impossible to replicate or migrate.
  - These moats compound: data gravity grows over time, and implementation costs discourage even customers who are dissatisfied with their vendor.

- Veeva's **95% gross dollar retention** in life sciences CRM is cited as emblematic of how incumbents maintain retention without needing to invest in outstanding customer experience — the switching cost alone does the work.

- Prior platform shifts (e.g., on-premise to cloud) failed to justify migration because the value-add did not clearly outweigh the pain; customers stayed put even as technology evolved substantially.

### How AI Is Dismantling Both Structural Moats

- **The implementation moat is crumbling under AI code generation.**
  - AI code-gen agents (e.g., Cursor-class tools) can now handle custom integration, configuration, and data transformation work that traditionally required expensive human system integrators.
  - Expert conversations with middleware platforms and large system integrators show **early evidence of 90% reduction** in time required to build custom integrations.
  - The expectation is that the **entire end-to-end process** — from capturing requirements to implementing them in a new SoR — will become highly automated within a few years.
  - Two approaches are emerging in parallel: AI code-gen tools used by developers, and AI-enabled services platforms that deliver the full migration as a managed service.

## Key Claims

1. AI is degrading the traditional moats that have protected incumbent Systems of Record vendors for decades.
2. By 2023, annual IT spend on Systems of Record software (ERP, CRM, HCM, financial and supply chain management) reached an estimated $400 billion.
3. Beyond software licensing, an estimated $50+ billion is spent annually on SoR maintenance, covering implementation, configuration, integration, troubleshooting, and staff upskilling.
4. AI code generation tools can reduce the time required to build custom SoR integrations by approximately 90%.
5. The entire end-to-end SoR migration process — from capturing requirements to implementation — is expected to become highly automated in the coming years.
6. AI enables natural language interfaces for ERP customization, allowing non-technical users to build tailored implementations without system integrators.
7. AI tools that passively collect data from communications (emails, video calls, messages) can reach near-parity with incumbent SoR data stores for recent activities over time.
8. The two deepest moats for incumbent SoR vendors are high upfront implementation costs and housing customers' critical data.
9. Leading SoR incumbents have maintained extremely high customer retention without necessarily investing in outstanding customer experience — e.g., Veeva CRM has 95% gross dollar retention.
10. Many on-premise SoR contracts are approaching end-of-life simultaneously, creating a favorable timing window for new entrants.

## Capabilities

- AI code generation agents can reduce the time required to build custom enterprise system integrations by approximately 90% compared to traditional approaches
- AI agents can write scripts in enterprise vendor domain-specific languages (MuleSoft, Workday, NetSuite, SAP) to automate integration development and unlock data locked in proprietary systems
- Natural language interfaces allow non-technical users to describe desired business workflows and UI in plain language, with AI translating requirements into working workflow and UI code in ERP systems — eliminating the need for system integrators
- AI can passively collect and structure enterprise data from communication flows (emails, video calls, Slack messages, conversations) without manual data entry, building data stores approaching parity with incumbent SoRs for recent activities
- AI can synthesize unstructured communications data (emails, Slack messages, call transcripts, sales signals) into prioritized, actionable daily recommendations for business managers — turning messy data into operational intelligence
- Voice AI agents can automate responses to non-emergency 911 calls, freeing human dispatchers for critical incidents — serving as an AI wedge into the Computer Aided Dispatch market
- AI can automate IP invention harvesting — collecting novel ideas from inventors and generating documentation needed to initiate patent applications — replacing manual expert labour in a traditionally expensive professional services workflow

## Limitations

- AI-native SoR data stores only approach parity with incumbents for recent activity data — years of historical data accumulated through manual entry in legacy systems cannot be retroactively captured by passive AI collection
- Full end-to-end automation of enterprise SoR migration — from requirements capture through implementation — is not yet possible; it is a near-future aspiration, not a current production capability
- The claimed ~90% reduction in integration time is based only on 'early signs' from expert conversations, not validated through systematic measurement at scale in production enterprise deployments
- AI-native SoRs cannot yet compete with incumbents on features for mid-to-large enterprise customers — they must start with underserved downmarket customers and work upmarket only as feature parity is eventually achieved
- AI integration agents for enterprise ecosystems are vendor-specific and non-portable — agents built for MuleSoft cannot work in Workday, requiring separate bespoke solutions per vendor DSL
- No discussion of hallucination risk in AI systems handling mission-critical enterprise data — a conspicuous absence given the severity of errors in financial forecasting, HR decisions, and customer records
- No discussion of data security, privacy, or regulatory risk from AI passively ingesting all employee communications — a critical gap given enterprise GDPR, SOC2, and data governance requirements
- The complete shift from systems of record to systems of action is acknowledged to 'take time' and is framed as a 5–10 year horizon — AI-native disruption of entrenched SoR incumbents will not be rapid
- The 'wedge product' strategy — where AI-native entrants must co-exist with incumbents before gradually replacing them — reveals that AI cannot yet deliver full SoR replacement value from day one

## Bottlenecks

- Legacy SoR incumbent moats — high upfront implementation/customisation costs and critical business data lock-in — are blocking AI-native entrant adoption despite demonstrably superior product experiences
- Enterprise vendor DSL fragmentation — each SoR vendor (MuleSoft, Workday, SAP, NetSuite) uses proprietary domain-specific languages with no universal standard — forces bespoke AI agent development per ecosystem, blocking scalable AI-driven integration
- Historical enterprise data accumulated in legacy SoRs over years of manual entry cannot be passively replicated by AI — creating a data completeness gap that blocks full enterprise migration from incumbents to AI-native alternatives

## Breakthroughs

- AI code generation is reducing enterprise SoR integration and migration time by ~90%, making rip-and-replace migrations economically viable for the first time — degrading the 'implementation moat' that protected incumbent vendors for decades
- AI passive data collection from communication flows is dissolving the 'data moat' of incumbent SoRs — new AI-native entrants can now organically accumulate enterprise data without requiring manual user entry, approaching incumbents' stored data value for recent activities
