# Saam Motamedi: Why Series B Won't Make Money & Why $1M ARR is a BS Milestone for Series A | E1177

Greylock partner Saam Motamedi argues that the current AI investing environment is arguably more irrational than the 2021-22 peak, that the Series B asset class may not generate attractive returns for this vintage, and that $1M ARR is a meaningless milestone for Series A decisions — offering a clear-eyed framework for distinguishing genuinely defensible AI application businesses from those coasting on novelty and abundant capital.

**Authors:** Saam Motamedi
**Published:** 2024-07-15
**Type:** video

---

## Are We in an AI Bubble?

Motamedi is direct: yes, and it may be larger than most realize. Seed rounds for pre-product AI companies routinely reach tens of millions to over $100M post-money. Revenue multiples of 100–200x are common in private markets — while the best public names trade at 15–20x. This dislocation only makes sense if these companies can sustain growth rates far longer than prior SaaS generations, and Motamedi doubts most can.

Two distorting forces are at work:

1. **Corporate venture capital** — players like hyperscalers are no longer purely return-driven. They are incentivized by partnerships and cloud credits, introducing irrational buyers who reduce the importance of price.
2. **Early-stage platforms filling the growth gap** — when SoftBank, Coatue, D1, and Durable retreated from late-stage markets, the expectation was that Series B would get more rational. Instead, early-stage platforms with massive growth funds stepped in, keeping the Series B market as frothy as or frothier than 2021.

> "I really wonder if the Series B asset class as a whole is positioned to make money this vintage."

This matters for [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC & Startup Ecosystem]] broadly: the supply of capital chasing AI deals has not contracted, it has merely reshuffled.

---

## The Retention Problem Underneath the Growth Numbers

First-generation AI applications — writing assistants, early content tools — posted extraordinary growth metrics two to three years ago. Many have since decelerated sharply. Motamedi frames this as the central hidden limitation of the current wave: **revenue today is not necessarily recurring revenue**.

The core limitation he identifies: *ARR is a poor predictor of success for AI applications*. The vast majority of companies reaching $1M ARR never scale to $10M, let alone $50M or $100M+. Using early ARR as a Series A signal is therefore largely noise — what matters is whether the growth is driven by a sticky, defensible workflow or by novelty-fueled churn.

Related limitations:

- **Enterprise add-on skepticism**: When Box or Notion added AI features, customers appreciated them but refused to pay incremental fees. AI perceived as feature enhancement rather than standalone value creation cannot command premium pricing.
- **Unclear monetization timing**: The window in which customers will accept higher pricing for AI-augmented products remains uncertain, making near-term revenue projections unreliable.

## What Actually Differentiates in a Crowded Market

Motamedi's framework for application-layer AI investing is explicitly a return to SaaS fundamentals, not a new theory:

- Best founder and management team
- Point of view on what drives product depth and stickiness
- Unique, self-compounding distribution

The historical analogs are instructive. HubSpot was dismissed as "just a wrapper on a database." The [[themes/startup_and_investment|startup and investment]] thesis that made HubSpot, Figma, and similar companies durable was not the underlying technology — it was deep workflow penetration for a specific end user, followed by reaching distribution before incumbents could copy the innovation. The same logic applies to AI-native companies.

The "wrapper company" critique of AI applications misses this. What matters is not whether the application sits on top of a foundation model, but whether it has built irreplaceable workflow value.

## The OpenAI Threat: Where It Applies and Where It Doesn't

Motamedi draws a practical distinction between **foundational AI capabilities** and **vertical workflow applications**:

**Foundational capabilities (high threat from OpenAI):**
- Content generation, writing, editing
- Pure code generation
- General-purpose chat interfaces

These are analogous to calculator or maps on a smartphone — primitives that platform owners will absorb. Companies built primarily on these vectors face existential competitive pressure.

**Vertical workflow applications (lower threat):**
- Co-pilot for lawyers or physicians
- SRE incident response and debugging tooling
- Domain-specific enterprise workflows

OpenAI is unlikely to need to own these spaces, and the switching costs embedded in deep vertical workflows provide meaningful defensibility. Motamedi is explicit that "focused applications on top of large model capabilities" can be large businesses — the opportunity is not small just because it is vertical.

---

## Foundation Model Layer: One or Two Winners, Unclear Margins

On the model layer itself, Motamedi's view is cautious. There may be one or two players (OpenAI clearly, Anthropic scaling) who can sustain durable leads and generate margin from model-as-a-service. But whether that separation persists — and whether it is sufficient to support margin — is genuinely uncertain as the model landscape commoditizes.

His updated view on focused model companies is more optimistic than 12 months prior: a better focused model (audio generation, voice synthesis) combined with rapid movement up the stack into applications is a viable strategy. [[entities/elevenlabs|ElevenLabs]] is cited as the archetype — strong underlying model, but defensibility is built in the workflow and product layer above it.

## The Disruption of Horizontal SaaS Incumbents

The most structurally significant thesis in the conversation concerns the simultaneous disruption of Salesforce, ServiceNow, Workday, and SAP via three concurrent shifts:

1. **Data model shift** — AI agents can consume raw customer interaction data directly, making the CRM as structured data repository less necessary
2. **Delivery/pricing model shift** — outcome-based or consumption-based pricing displaces per-seat licensing; incumbents cannot adopt this without cannibalizing existing revenue
3. **Interface shift** — within a decade, sales reps may not interact with Salesforce's UI at all; AI agents serve as the primary interface to underlying systems of record

> "I would argue in 10 years sales reps won't even know what the Salesforce UI looks like because the interface will be an agent."

AI-powered pipeline forecasting illustrates the concrete mechanism: accuracy improves because it draws from raw customer interaction data rather than manually entered CRM records. The incumbent advantage (distribution, existing data) is partially offset by the inability to cannibalize seat-based revenue models.

This connects directly to [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|startup formation and GTM]] — the window for disruption is real but time-bounded by incumbent response speed.

## Open Questions and Limitations

Several tensions remain unresolved in Motamedi's framework:

- **Retention as leading signal**: If churn is the hidden problem, what retention thresholds actually predict scale? The framework identifies the problem but does not provide a clear threshold.
- **Incumbent response speed**: The disruption thesis depends on incumbents being slow to adopt AI-native pricing. How long that window lasts is uncertain.
- **Foundation model margin durability**: Whether OpenAI and Anthropic can maintain pricing power as the model landscape commoditizes is explicitly flagged as debatable.
- **Identifying founder quality at seed**: Founders are the primary signal Motamedi trusts, but assessing founder quality before there is meaningful product or market evidence is the hardest problem in early-stage investing — acknowledged but not resolved.
- **CVC distortion persistence**: The irrational buyer dynamic created by hyperscaler corporate venture arms distorts markets in ways that are difficult to model; how long this persists and what unwinds it is unclear.
