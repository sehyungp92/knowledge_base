# knowledge distillation for reasoning

Knowledge distillation for reasoning is a post-training method in which a student LLM is trained via supervised finetuning (SFT) on long chain-of-thought traces generated by a stronger teacher model. Its significance lies in the efficiency with which it transfers sophisticated reasoning behaviours — including extended deliberation, self-correction, and structured problem decomposition — to smaller models that could not develop those patterns through their own pretraining or even through RL on their own.

## Overview

Knowledge distillation for reasoning trains a student LLM on long chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning traces generated by a stronger teacher model, enabling transfer of novel reasoning patterns that lie beyond the student's base capacity. The method is structurally simple — next-token prediction on curated trace data — but its effectiveness depends heavily on the quality and composition of what the student is trained on, not merely the quantity.

Two contemporaneous results bracket the efficiency frontier of this approach. s1: Simple test-time scaling demonstrated that finetuning Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct on just 1,000 carefully selected samples (the s1K dataset) for 26 minutes on 16 H100 GPUs produced a model capable of competitive AIME24 performance and robust test-time scaling via budget forcing. DeepSeek R1 took the opposite route: the distilled R1-32B variant used SFT on 800,000 reasoning samples and achieved 72.6% on AIME24 against s1-32B's 56.7% — a significant margin, but at roughly 800× the data cost and substantially more compute (394 H100 GPU hours versus 7 for s1-32B). Neither result is strictly superior; they reveal a data-quality–data-volume tradeoff that the field has not yet resolved.

---

## Data Selection Is the Core Lever

The s1 experiments make a strong empirical case that data composition dominates data quantity. Training on the full 59K-sample pool (a superset of s1K) produced no substantial gains over the 1K curated subset while requiring 56× more GPU hours (394 vs. 7 H100 GPU hours). Selecting samples solely by trace length, diversity, or random sampling each degraded AIME24 performance by roughly 30% on average. Only the joint criterion — simultaneously optimising for difficulty, diversity, and quality — preserved strong performance. This finding reframes distillation as fundamentally a data curation problem, not a compute scaling problem.

---

## Relationship to RLVR and the Scope Expansion Question

The most consequential open question about knowledge distillation for reasoning is whether it genuinely expands a student model's reasoning scope or merely shifts the distribution of reasoning it surfaces. Research from Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning Capacity in LLMs Beyond the Base Model? complicates the picture by showing that RLVR-trained models, which are often compared against distilled ones, outperform their base models at small k (e.g., pass@1) but are consistently surpassed by base models at large k across all benchmarks and model families. This suggests that RLVR — and by analogy, distillation — may primarily reshuffle existing latent capacity rather than creating genuinely new reasoning capability.

If that interpretation holds for distillation as well, then the strong benchmark numbers produced by distilled models may be an artifact of trace-shaped output distributions rather than evidence of deeper reasoning. The teacher model's reasoning traces guide the student toward high-probability paths through the problem space, which improves greedy/top-1 performance but may suppress the diversity of approaches the student would otherwise explore.

## Test-Time Compute Scaling and Hard Limits

Knowledge distillation interacts directly with [[themes/test_time_compute_scaling|test-time compute scaling]] through mechanisms like budget forcing, which gives perfect controllability over thinking duration and produces the best AIME24 scores in the s1 experiments. However, this scaling is bounded by a hard constraint: the context window of the underlying language model eventually caps further sequential extension of reasoning traces. This is not a soft limit that additional data or architectural tuning can easily relax; it is a structural ceiling that affects all trace-length-dependent test-time strategies equally.

---

## Connections to Frontier Lab Competition

The speed and cost at which distillation can produce competitive reasoning models has reshaped [[themes/frontier_lab_competition|frontier lab competition]]. The DeepSeek R1 release demonstrated that o1-level performance is replicable — and distributable via distilled variants — at a fraction of the cost that had previously seemed necessary. The s1 result pushed this further, showing that a capable research group with modest GPU access could close a significant fraction of that gap using only 1,000 samples and 26 minutes of training. These results have accelerated the commoditisation of reasoning capability, shifting the competitive question from "can you build a reasoning model" to "what proprietary data, evaluation methodology, or architectural insight do you have that others cannot easily replicate."

---

## Limitations and Open Questions

The method carries several underappreciated limitations. First, the ceiling is set by the teacher: distillation cannot transfer reasoning the teacher does not exhibit, and it cannot correct systematic teacher errors that manifest consistently in the traces. Second, the s1K curation criteria (difficulty, diversity, quality jointly) have not been thoroughly validated across domains — it is unclear whether the same criteria generalise from mathematical competition problems to, say, multi-step scientific reasoning or code synthesis. Third, the context window constraint is not merely a practical inconvenience; it means that trace-based scaling strategies converge to an asymptote that RL-based methods (operating through reward rather than imitation) are not similarly bounded by in the same structural way.

The deeper open question is whether the apparent reasoning transferred by distillation represents learned procedure or learned mimicry. A student model producing chains-of-thought structurally similar to its teacher's traces is not necessarily reasoning in the same way — it may be pattern-matching against superficial trace features. Distinguishing these cases requires evaluation methodology that is not yet standardised, and most current benchmarks (including AIME24) cannot cleanly separate them.
