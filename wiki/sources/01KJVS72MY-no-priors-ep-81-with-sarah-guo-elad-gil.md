## Analysis

LLM Market Consolidation

- It does feel like we're moving into a stage of increasingly more consolidation in the core LLM market\. For instance, a number of model companies have recently seen their teams join larger enterprises, e\.g\., Inflection, Character, Adept\.
	- Many of these companies still continue to exist and some of the products are still running and being used in different ways\. 
- At the same time, there's enormous capital moats emerging to get to the biggest scale for foundation models\. These companies are now raising billions of dollars, often from hyperscalers \(e\.g\., Amazon, Microsoft\) or sovereigns because those are the only ones who can actually provide that amount of capital\. 
	- The big hyperscalers have an incentive to fund these companies simply because it also translates over to more cloud utilisation as an industry in general, while the VC industry is too small to actually be able to support the next round for these companies\. 
	- So an interesting question is for all the other players in the market, where are they going to get these ever rising amounts of capital, and who do they partner with, does Apple or Samsung, for instance, end up with a partner? 
- It does seem like it will be increasingly hard for most companies to end up being competitive outside of a fundamental breakthrough in the model architecture, cost of training, running inference on the model or the post\-training side\. 

Competition and Decreasing API Costs

- At the same time, the market has become more competitive, not less over the last year and a half\. There's continuous performance increases and price decreases as well as real open source players\. 
- API costs have dropped ~200x in the last 18\-24 months or something along that order of magnitude, which means that the revenue available is increasing from the perspective of usage, but it's becoming increasingly harder to just go out and compete with the model, at least as an API business\. 
	- This may push players into specialisation, for instance, providing bespoke specialised models with specific types of post\-training for vertical applications\. 

Innovation in LLM Productisation

- In the long term, the goal of the companies with the capital from a business perspective is still AGI, where they believe there will be emergent behaviours and capabilities in the model and it will be obvious how valuable it is\. But in the more immediate 2\-3 year horizon, consumer remains the main business, i\.e\., apps, subscriptions advertising etc\. So it's become much more of a product fight, and players trying to build moats around the capital and research, trying to lock in customers with for instance additional features such as prompt caching or JSON output interfaces, trying to make it less of a commodity\. 
	- Anthropic’s prompt caching is an interesting move in terms the impact on cost, timing and latency\. 
	- Interfaces may also be another place where there might be innovation, even if chat does not go away\. 
- The challenges that are possible in the market that people are trying today involve those that are taking very different reasoning approaches \(e\.g\., self\-play for maths and code\)\.
	- As well as different reasoning approaches and other things like emerging agentic flows, there is also differentiation within the infrastructure around the model, e\.g\., caching, long context window etc\.
- Distillation, finetuning and relevance of small models is another open question of how people are going to really use these models\. 
- Beyond the core LLLM market, there will be lots of other model types that will come over time in physics, biology, material science, image gen etc\. In some cases, these things are going multimodal, but in many cases, there is going to be unique models for each\. 

Comparing the LLM and Social Network Market

- There might be parallels with the development of the social network market where people were quick to anoint a winner but new players always emerged, and people thought it became a commodity market where there was never going to be a long term differentiator\.
	- Everybody thought MySpace was going to win, then Facebook emerged to win the core social piece\. Even you had Instagram, Twitter, Snapchat and eventually TikTok\. 
	- There'll also be certain players who will start to grab parts of the market, just as LinkedIn became the enterprise social identity or Twitter became a core piece dealing with real time information\.
- It looks like it may be general purpose models for certain things and then specialised ones for other application areas\. It comes down to what's the degree of generalisability that you have not only in the model capabilities, but then also in the tooling around it, and then whether or not the tooling need to be vertically integrated with the model\. 
	- For example, if you have a good image gen product with and it may have artistic applications, graphic design applications, or UI design applications\. The question is would that involve finetuning or post training on one model, or involve one big model for one aspect and then a bunch of finetuned or specialised models for other things\. That seems to be a big open question\. 
- There are similar discussions to be had around AGI or more general purpose intelligence\. If you look at the way the brain works, there is a set of reasonably specialised modules for vision processing, different aspects of emotion etc\., and so the question is how specialised or generalised will these models be\. 
	- That's also true for things such as image gen, will there be a different model for graphic design than one used for artistic expression? It's an interesting question and time will tell\.

## Key Claims

1. API costs for LLMs have dropped approximately 200x in the last 18 to 24 months.
2. The Venture Capital industry is too small to support the next funding rounds for foundation model companies.
3. Foundation model companies are raising billions of dollars primarily from hyperscalers (Amazon, Microsoft) or sovereign funds.
4. Teams from Inflection, Character, and Adept have joined larger enterprises.
5. The LM market has become more competitive over the last year and a half, with continual performance increases and price decreases.
6. Competing as a pure LLM API business is becoming increasingly difficult due to commoditization.
7. Anthropic has introduced prompt caching as a feature that reduces cost and latency.
8. Anthropic hired Mikey Kay and OpenAI hired Kevin Weil as product leaders.
9. No major AI company has pursued an advertising-based consumer revenue model.
10. Model sizes are shrinking relative to performance, partly through distillation.

## Capabilities

- Multiple independent companies generating high-fidelity video with competitive quality; companies like Runway, Pika, Luma Labs, and others demonstrating production-ready video generation capabilities
- Dramatic reduction in API inference costs across language models; cost-per-million-tokens has decreased substantially, enabling broader deployment and commoditized pricing
- Successful model distillation and compression achieving competitive performance from smaller models; models shrinking in size while maintaining or improving performance
- Advanced API features enabling model specialization and reduced commoditization: prompt caching, JSON output interfaces, fine-tuning capabilities, and latency optimization
- Specialized hardware designs optimized for Transformer architectures and matrix multiplication; emerging cluster of companies focused on AI-specific chip design
- Image generation competition emerging beyond Midjourney; multiple mid-stage and smaller players (Ideogram, Hotshot) achieving competitive fidelity and feature parity
- Real-time video/image generation during user input emerging as nascent capability; models getting smaller enabling lower-latency inference suitable for real-time applications

## Limitations

- Real-time, interactive generative AI applications are not deployed at production scale despite being a desired capability; latency constraints remain significant
- Fundamental uncertainty about whether image/video/audio generation models should be general-purpose with post-training specialization or require purpose-built specialized models for different applications
- No viable consumer business model for AI applications has been demonstrated at scale; advertising revenue (distinct from subscriptions) remains unproven and unattempted seriously
- Independent startups cannot compete with hyperscalers in language model development and API business; consolidation forces retreat to specialization; API business increasingly commoditized and margin-compressed
- Critical data dependency: video model training at scale appears to require YouTube data; legal/ethical pathways for large-scale video data collection are unclear
- AMD lacks competitive software ecosystem and full-stack data center systems design equivalent to NVIDIA's CUDA + systems integration; market confidence in AMD's ability to compete is low
- Venture capital market too small to fund next generation of foundation model training; only hyperscalers and sovereign funds can provide billions-scale capital required
- Architectural/algorithmic breakthrough requirements unclear for next generation of competitive models; whether fundamental innovation is needed or whether known approaches (distillation, self-play) suffice is uncertain

## Bottlenecks

- Enormous capital requirements ($billions+) for training competitive foundation models; only hyperscalers and sovereigns can provide sufficient funding; venture capital market is structurally too small to support independent model companies
- AMD and alternative hardware vendors lack competitive software ecosystem (CUDA-equivalent), full-stack data center systems design, and systems integration capabilities relative to NVIDIA; closing this gap requires major engineering investment
- Clarity on business model differentiation and monetization for language model providers beyond API consumption and subscriptions; unclear whether consumers will adopt advertising-based or other models; unclear if specialization/vertical integration strategies can generate sufficient returns
- Architectural and algorithmic innovation requirements for next generation competitive models remain unclear; uncertain whether current approaches (distillation, self-play, math/code training) can sustain competitiveness or fundamental breakthrough is required
- Large-scale video training data sourcing is constrained by dependence on YouTube; legal and ethical pathways for video data collection and use remain unresolved

## Breakthroughs

- 200x reduction in API inference costs over 18-24 months; language model inference has transitioned from premium to commodity pricing, enabling broad deployment across use cases
- Model distillation and compression achieving near-parity performance from significantly smaller models; demonstrated across multiple model types that capability gains can be decoupled from model size
- Video generation market transitioned from perceived single-winner (Sora) to multi-vendor competitive landscape; multiple independent companies (Runway, Pika, Luma Labs, Ideogram) demonstrating production-quality video generation
