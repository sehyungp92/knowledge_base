### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- Diagnostic reasoning by LLMs has been studied extensively, but management reasoning — disease progression over time, therapeutic response, medication prescription — remains largely unexplored in conversational AI settings.
  - Prior work, including earlier AMIE, demonstrated diagnostic capability but could not handle longitudinal multi-visit encounters, interpret treatment response, or generate precise, guideline-grounded care plans.
  - Existing LLM management studies relied on static, non-conversational vignette settings, missing the dynamic and contextually specific nature of real clinical management.
- No validated conversational AI system had been rigorously compared to primary care physicians (PCPs) on management reasoning across multiple visits, leaving the feasibility of AI in longitudinal disease management uncharacterised.
  - Management reasoning benchmarks for AI were nearly absent; medication reasoning had no standardised multi-jurisdictional benchmark derived from national formularies.
- LLMs tend to err on the side of caution in high-stakes management settings and fail at tasks requiring integration of multiple guidelines for comorbid patients, particularly when precise, actionable directives (specific drug, dose, route, duration) are required rather than broad suggestions.

---

### Proposed Approach
- AMIE is extended with a dual-agent architecture explicitly inspired by Kahneman's dual-process theory: a fast Dialogue Agent handles real-time empathetic conversation, while a slower Management Reasoning (Mx) Agent performs deep, guideline-grounded clinical planning.
  - The Dialogue Agent is fine-tuned on Gemini 1.5 Flash via supervised fine-tuning on multi-visit simulated dialogues, followed by RLHF/RLAIF using pairwise human and LLM preferences over dialogue quality and management recommendations.
  - The Mx Agent exploits Gemini's long-context window (256k tokens per call) to reason directly over up to six full-text clinical guideline documents simultaneously, avoiding the compounding errors and latency of chunked RAG pipelines.
- The Mx Agent uses structured generation with decoding constraints: a JSON schema derived from Python class definitions constrains output to valid citation IDs and structured plan fields, guaranteeing traceable, citation-annotated management plans within a single model call.
  - Each plan is generated by chaining three structured reasoning steps — Analyze Patient, Set Objectives, Plan and Cite — within one long-context call, reducing latency while enabling rich cross-document reasoning.
  - Four draft management plans are generated in parallel and merged via an ensemble refinement step, allowing diverse care strategies to be explored before a final plan is produced.
- A coarse retrieval step using Gecko 1B embeddings pre-filters a 10.5M-token corpus of 627 NICE and BMJ Best Practice guidelines to fit within the context window, with auto-evaluation used to tune all design parameters (context length, number of drafts, reasoning structure).
- The paper also introduces RxQA, a 600-question multiple-choice benchmark derived from US (OpenFDA) and UK (BNF) national drug formularies, validated and difficulty-rated by board-certified pharmacists, designed to benchmark medication reasoning in both closed-book and open-book settings.

### Results & Capabilities
- In a randomised, blinded, multi-visit remote OSCE study across 100 three-visit scenarios and 21 PCPs spanning five specialties, AMIE's management plans were non-inferior to PCPs across all 15 specialist-evaluated axes and all three visits; no domain showed PCPs outperforming AMIE.
  - AMIE significantly outperformed PCPs on overall appropriateness at visit 1 (88% vs. 74%, p=0.019) and on appropriate follow-up recommendations (100% vs. 98%, p<0.001).
- AMIE was substantially more precise in treatment recommendations across all three visits (94% vs. 67%, p<0.001 at visit 1; 91% vs. 70%, p=0.001 at visit 3), shifting from broad suggestions like "prescribe an antibiotic" to fully specified directives including drug name, dose, route, duration, and monitoring plan.
  - Investigation preciseness was equivalent at visit 1 (91% each) but diverged significantly in follow-up visits (99% vs. 84%, p=0.009 at visit 2; 100% vs. 88%, p<0.001 at visit 3), suggesting AMIE's advantage compounds over longitudinal encounters.
- AMIE significantly outperformed PCPs on guideline alignment for treatment recommendations across all three visits (89% vs. 75%, p=0.007 at visit 1; 93% vs. 81%, p=0.014 at visit 3) and on explicit guideline referencing (98–100% vs. 86–88% across visits).
- On the MXEKF rubric assessing ten empirically-derived management reasoning dimensions, AMIE had a median win rate of 42% (95% CI, 29–50%) versus PCPs' 8% (95% CI, 2–14%), with ~50% of comparisons tied; this held across both specialist physician and patient actor perspectives.
- On RxQA higher-difficulty questions, AMIE outperformed PCPs in both closed-book (50.6% vs. 41.5%, p=0.013) and open-book (57.9% vs. 47.8%, p<0.001) settings; no significant difference was detected on lower-difficulty questions.
  - Both PCPs and AMIE improved by more than 20 percentage points on lower-difficulty questions when given access to medication labels, confirming that open external knowledge materially aids medication reasoning for both humans and AI.
  - Peak accuracy remained below 75% for both AMIE (73.8%) and PCPs (67.4%) even in the easiest open-book setting, indicating substantial headroom for improvement.
- Confabulation rates in AMIE's management plans were reported as comparable to PCPs and substantially lower than rates reported in other LLM clinical studies, attributed to the citation-grounded structured genera

## Key Claims

1. LLM capabilities for effective management reasoning—including disease progression, therapeutic response, and safe medication prescription—remain under-explored
2. AMIE was non-inferior to primary care physicians in management reasoning as assessed by specialist physicians in a randomized blinded OSCE study
3. AMIE scored better than PCPs in preciseness of treatments and investigations and in alignment with clinical guidelines
4. AMIE outperformed primary care physicians on higher difficulty medication reasoning questions in RxQA
5. Both AMIE and PCPs benefited from access to external drug information for medication reasoning
6. Further research would be needed before real-world clinical translation of AMIE
7. Diagnostic reasoning is necessary but not sufficient for clinical care applications; management reasoning requires additional complex synthesis capabilities
8. Management reasoning shows considerable context specificity, meaning two physicians given identical patient information may make different decisions
9. Few rigorous investigations of LLM management reasoning capabilities exist, and existing studies rely on static non-conversational settings
10. AMIE uses a dual-agent architecture inspired by dual-process theory, with a fast Dialogue Agent analogous to System 1 and a slower Mx Agent analogous to System 2

## Capabilities

- Multi-visit conversational disease management at non-inferior quality to board-certified primary care physicians, covering disease progression, therapeutic response, and longitudinal reasoning across three sequential patient visits
- AI generates more precise, actionable clinical treatment recommendations than PCPs — naming specific antibiotics, doses, durations, and contingencies rather than generic guidance (94% vs 67% preciseness at visit 1)
- AI clinical management plans significantly better aligned with and explicitly grounded in clinical guidelines compared to PCPs, with near-perfect reference rates across all three visits
- AI outperforms PCPs on higher-difficulty medication reasoning questions from national drug formularies in both closed-book (50.6% vs 41.5%) and open-book (57.9% vs 47.8%) settings
- Long-context LLM reasoning over 256K tokens of clinical guidelines simultaneously, enabling rich cross-document synthesis and in-generation citation without specialized post-hoc retrieval pipelines
- Dual-process multi-agent clinical architecture — a fast System-1 dialogue agent (seconds latency) orchestrating a slower System-2 management reasoning agent (sub-minute), solving the real-time reasoning-latency tradeoff in clinical conversation
- Structured reasoning with inference-time decoding constraints guarantees valid JSON plan structure, constrains citation values to retrieved document IDs, and chains multi-step reasoning in a single model call to reduce latency
- Persistent agent state tracking patient summary, differential diagnosis, and management plan across multiple clinical visits, enabling longitudinal continuity in AI-driven consultations via asynchronous background reasoning

## Limitations

- AMIE is not ready for real-world clinical deployment — validated only in controlled OSCE settings with constructed scenarios, patient actors, and definitive answers that do not reflect actual clinical care complexity
- AI clinical management evaluated exclusively in text-based interface while real-world telehealth is predominantly audio/video — performance claims cannot be extrapolated to actual clinical workflows
- No EHR or electronic provider order entry integration — AI clinical recommendations are free text, missing structured order workflows, pre-populated formularies, and clinical decision support alerts that characterize real clinical environments
- Hallucination/confabulation risk remains a significant patient safety concern — backbone model Gemini 1.5 Flash exhibits >5% hallucination rate on HHEM leaderboard in a domain where errors can cause direct patient harm
- Medication reasoning accuracy ceiling below 75% even in best conditions (lower-difficulty, open-book) — significant headroom before clinical viability, and performance on real-world formulary complexity likely lower than benchmark suggests
- Full clinical guideline corpus (10.5M tokens across 627 documents) exceeds Gemini's 2M token context window, requiring a coarse retrieval step that reintroduces compounding errors — negating the key architectural advantage of long-context reasoning
- OSCE study compressed visit intervals (1-2 days actual vs weeks/months in scenario narrative) inflated physician performance — longitudinal AI advantage over humans with imperfect memory is likely understated
- No access to patient chart review — cases lack the longitudinal medical record context that characterizes real clinical decision-making, making AI management reasoning evaluations artificially clean
- Pharmacist oversight and clinical practice alerts absent from study — real clinical environments have multi-layer medication safety checks that would both catch errors and elevate AI performance through formulary constraints
- Clinical trial validation for LLM clinical decision support is largely absent — privacy regulations and study complexity have significantly delayed rigorous real-world evidence generation, creating a gap between research performance and deployable evidence
- One-minute response time constraint for management reasoning agent caps reasoning depth — the design explicitly sacrifices deeper multi-step planning for real-time interaction requirements
- Closed-book medication reasoning accuracy poor for both AI (41.5-52.8%) and physicians — without external references, neither achieves clinical adequacy, revealing that medication knowledge cannot be reliably memorized by models or humans
- MXEKF management reasoning rubric psychometric properties not fully established — only pilot validity demonstrated, with reliability, inter-rater agreement, and real-world applicability requiring further validation
- RxQA benchmark skewed toward harder questions by design — filtering for questions requiring medication labels creates a benchmark not representative of typical clinical practice, inflating observed difficulty
- Coarse guideline retrieval using Gecko 1B embeddings on titles/abstracts alone risks missing clinically relevant documents — retrieval failures compound into downstream management plan errors

## Bottlenecks

- Real-world clinical validation gap — privacy regulations, patient safety requirements, and study complexity have created a structural lag between AI clinical capability demonstrations and rigorous evidence from clinical trials, blocking regulatory approval and deployment
- Context window ceiling relative to comprehensive clinical knowledge corpora — even 2M token context windows are insufficient for full-corpus in-context reasoning over complete national guideline libraries (10.5M tokens), requiring lossy retrieval that reintroduces errors
- Latency-reasoning tradeoff in real-time clinical AI — deeper multi-step clinical management reasoning requires more compute time than real-time patient interaction allows, forcing architectural compromises that cap management plan quality

## Breakthroughs

- First demonstration of an AI system achieving non-inferior conversational disease management reasoning to board-certified primary care physicians in a randomized, blinded, multi-visit OSCE study — extending AI clinical capability beyond diagnosis into longitudinal disease management
- Dual-process multi-agent architecture demonstrated as a practical solution to the real-time clinical reasoning latency tradeoff — separating fast empathetic dialogue (System 1) from slow evidence-based management planning (System 2) achieves both goals simultaneously in live clinical consultations
