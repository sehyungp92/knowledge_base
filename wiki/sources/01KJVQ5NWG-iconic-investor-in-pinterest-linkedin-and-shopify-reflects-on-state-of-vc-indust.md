# Iconic Investor in Pinterest, LinkedIn and Shopify Reflects on State of VC Industry

> A candid, experience-grounded reflection on venture capital mechanics by a Bessemer Venture Partners partner, covering investor impact limits, the primacy of price discipline, skill versus luck in VC, and why uncertainty around LLM commoditization is bottlenecking confident capital allocation into AI application companies.

**Authors:** Bessemer Venture Partners (interviewee)
**Published:** 2024-04-19
**Type:** video

---

## What Investors Actually Control

A central and somewhat counterintuitive argument runs through this source: investor impact on portfolio companies is structurally small, and mistaking it for large is both analytically wrong and operationally harmful.

The arithmetic is blunt. A partner managing ten to twenty companies devotes roughly one day per month to each. If a fully dedicated founding team can be meaningfully moved by one day of external attention per month, that team is probably missing something, and a company missing something is not a company you should have invested in. The implication: value creation is 99.99% picking and 0.01% helping. The decision to invest is the job. Everything after is largely spectator participation.

This creates a specific danger for former operators turned investors. Operators are trained to ask "how can I help?" The instinct to fix and improve is deeply conditioned. But in the investment context, the *need* for that help is itself a negative signal. An investor without operating experience sidesteps this trap naturally; a former operator must actively suppress their own strongest professional reflex.

The ideal portfolio company is described as a "doughnut company": you show up once a month for the board meeting, eat the doughnuts, and go home. Their not needing you is the success condition. The moments when an investor *can* be wildly impactful, a handful of critical inflection points where they happen to know exactly the right thing at the right time, are real but rare and should not be the motivating frame for doing the job.

On board seat dynamics: founders should want investors in investor seats and operators in operator seats. When a board seat is sold in exchange for investment capital, it becomes structurally difficult to replace even if the relationship sours. Founders wanting operational input should retain seats they control.

## Price, Uncertainty, and the Math That Cannot Be Ignored

The source pushes back directly on a common VC talking point: that price doesn't matter because outcomes are binary. This is described as "wildly flawed, lazy thinking and dead wrong."

The arithmetic is again straightforward. A fund returning 5x that paid twice the prevailing price on all its deals would have returned 2.5x instead. In a world of genuine uncertainty, where the quality of an investment is not knowable in advance, aggregate price absolutely matters. How to determine the right price is art, and can only be judged correctly in hindsight; but the relevance of price to returns is not ambiguous.

This extends to the question of missed investments. There are cases where the decision not to invest preceded a breakout outcome. But the right frame is not regret: given the risk being priced at the time and the information available, declining may have been the correct decision even when the ex-post outcome was spectacular. Outcome and decision quality are not the same thing, and random factors heavily influence end results.

## Skill, Luck, and the Longevity Test

A recurring skepticism runs through this source about reputational halos in venture. Many investors widely regarded as great, the argument goes, had a single key insight early in their careers and subsequently reverted toward mean performance. One successful period or one successful bet does not establish skill.

The genuine test is repeated success across multiple market cycles and multiple periods of time. That is the evidence that distinguishes actual skill from the lucky intersection of an idea and a moment. The venture job further complicates self-assessment: early success transforms the job itself. Where a new investor must seek out opportunities, a successful investor becomes sought out, which changes the information environment, the deal flow, and the social dynamics around decision-making in ways that are hard to disentangle from genuine edge.

Related to this: the most enjoyable moments in the job are precisely the contrarian-at-the-time insights that are proven right years later. The feedback loop is long, the uncertainty is high, and tolerating both requires actually loving the work.

## Firm Structure and Distributed Decision-Making

Bessemer Venture Partners at the time of this conversation is approximately 200 people with a COO handling all firm functions except investing. Investment decisions are highly distributed among partners rather than concentrated in a small leadership group. Non-investment decisions follow the opposite pattern: authority is more concentrated and is earned based on demonstrated track record within the firm.

The source notes with some self-deprecation that Bessemer was nearly 200 people before it hired a head of HR, illustrating a common partnership failure: applying advice to portfolio companies that they do not apply to themselves. Once talented professionals are placed in charge of firm functions (HR, finance, legal) rather than investors doing these things part-time, the difference is significant.

A telling observation about partnership dynamics: websites and office space decisions are disproportionately contentious because they are the only things outside investments where everyone feels qualified to have an opinion. Investment decisions get deference; everything visible and low-stakes generates maximum debate.

## AI and the Current Investment Bottleneck

The source offers a brief but pointed perspective on the LLM moment. The observation attributed to another commentator, that LLMs are "the fastest appreciating asset in human history," is noted as having stuck. This represents mainstream financial validation of the technology wave at the highest levels of the investment community. See AI Capabilities and Progress.

But validation of the technology does not resolve the investment question. The bottleneck is framework clarity: it remains genuinely hard to determine how value will distribute across the LLM stack, which applications will succeed versus be commoditized, and where the durable moats actually sit. The expectation is that collective understanding will sharpen over one to two years. Until then, confident early-stage capital allocation into AI application companies is constrained by genuine epistemic uncertainty rather than lack of interest.

**Open question:** As LLM infrastructure costs continue falling and model performance continues rising, which application-layer bets are investable now versus which require waiting for the commoditization dynamics to clarify? The source offers no resolution, only the honest acknowledgment that a good framework does not yet exist.
