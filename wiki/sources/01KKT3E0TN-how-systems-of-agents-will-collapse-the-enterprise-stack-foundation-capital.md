## Briefing

**Enterprise software has been built in dysfunctional layers for four decades — Systems of Record, Engagement, and Intelligence — each solving one problem while creating new ones. Systems of Agents don't add another layer; they collapse all three into a single cohesive system by capturing, understanding, and acting on business communication at its source, eliminating the need for human data entry entirely. This represents not a new software category but the dismantling of the entire enterprise software industry, threatening SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, and every company whose moat depends on switching costs and structured data.**

### Key Takeaways
1. **The three-layer enterprise stack is architecturally broken** — Systems of Record, Engagement, and Intelligence each failed in succession because they were designed around how computers receive data, not how humans actually work.
2. **"Garbage in, garbage out" is a structural flaw, not a fixable bug** — Systems of Intelligence like Outreach and Clari depend on human data entry that never happens reliably, making their analytics fundamentally unreliable regardless of model sophistication.
3. **Systems of Agents capture data where it's born, not where it's recorded** — by ingesting emails, calls, video, and documents directly, agents bypass the manual translation step that has crippled every previous intelligence layer.
4. **The interface itself becomes irrelevant** — forms, dashboards, and buttons are artifacts of how software constrained human work; agent systems make the UI fade as workflows become organic and automatic.
5. **"Service-as-Software" is the new economic model** — agents don't sell software licenses to humans who do work; they perform the work directly, compressing headcount (e.g., Regie: 3x prospect touches with one-third the SDRs).
6. **Systems of Record will be commoditized to pure storage** — when agents can construct structured records from natural communication, the core value proposition of CRMs and ERPs evaporates, and buyers will stop paying premium prices for glorified databases.
7. **The disruption sequence is ordered: Intelligence → Engagement → Record** — Systems of Intelligence fall first (weakest moats), engagement platforms second, and legacy records systems last due to deep embedding and switching costs.
8. **Multi-agent coordination enables full workflow ownership** — the distinguishing feature of a true System of Agents is not task automation but end-to-end orchestration across what were previously siloed software categories.
9. **Unstructured data is now the primary business signal** — text, audio, video, and voice carry more business value than structured database fields, and any system that can't process multimodal communication is structurally blind.
10. **Seven years without a billion-dollar Systems of Intelligence company is diagnostic** — the absence of major revenue winners in the intelligence layer is treated as empirical evidence the approach is fundamentally wrong, not just underperforming.
11. **Copilot/Autopilot modes define the human-in-the-loop spectrum** — high-intent prospects trigger human review (Copilot); low-intent accounts run fully autonomously (Autopilot), illustrating a practical framework for graduated AI autonomy.
12. **Incumbent moats dry up rapidly when agents bypass data entry** — switching costs and network effects that protected legacy vendors collapse once agents can source equivalent or superior data without touching the legacy system.

### The Forty-Year Failure of Layered Enterprise Software

- Enterprise software evolved through three successive layers, each born from the failures of the previous, creating a Jenga-like stack that added complexity without solving root problems.
  - **Systems of Record (1980s onward)**: designed purely for data storage and processing, focused on three domains — Human Capital Management (employee data), CRM (customer relationships), and ERP (company assets).
    - Built for utility over usability, with clunky interfaces from primitive client apps through early web browsers.
    - Spawned category-defining giants: SAP, Oracle, Workday, Salesforce — all built on the same underlying paradigm of structured data fields.
  - **Systems of Engagement**: emerged to solve usability problems, offering intuitive interfaces layered on top of Systems of Record.
    - Examples: Slack, Microsoft Teams, Zoom — communication platforms that made collaboration easier.
    - **Created a new problem**: their user-friendliness generated vast amounts of unstructured, multimodal data that traditional databases couldn't capture or process.
  - **Systems of Intelligence (coined 2017 by Greylock's Jerry Chen)**: proposed to bridge Record and Engagement by analyzing data to surface insights and drive actions.
    - Attracted significant investor and founder attention as the "key to unlocking value from enterprise data."
    - **Failed structurally on three dimensions**: misalignment with human work, dependency on human data entry, and inability to handle unstructured data.

- The cumulative result is digital dysfunction: enterprises have been patching flawed foundations with more layers rather than replacing the architecture.

## Capabilities

- AI sales agent systems can autonomously orchestrate full prospecting workflows — synthesizing intent signals, generating personalized outreach, tracking engagement, and managing follow-up — achieving 3x more prospect touches and 2.5x more meetings with one-third of prior SDR headcount
- AI agents can automatically capture and structure CRM data from real-time business communications (Zoom calls, emails, documents) without any manual data entry
- AI agents trained on millions of medical records can process patient intake information from faxes, emails, calls, and forms instantly — bypassing manual EHR data entry entirely
- AI recruitment agents can handle the full hiring communication flow — from initial candidate outreach through phone screens, texts, and follow-up emails — replacing multiple recruiter software tools
- AI talent platforms can predict employee career trajectories, suggest learning and development paths, and enable data-driven decisions about hiring, developing, and retaining talent
- AI agents operating in configurable autonomy tiers — 'Copilot' mode (human review of drafts) for high-value interactions vs. full 'Autopilot' for lower-stakes, high-volume accounts

## Limitations

- AI analytics systems that depend on human-entered CRM data are fundamentally constrained by data completeness — employees routinely omit critical information, making downstream intelligence unreliable regardless of model sophistication
- Legacy AI analytics tools (e.g. Outreach, Clari) struggle with sparse historical data — insufficient past examples to generate reliable predictions in typical enterprise contexts
- Traditional AI enterprise systems are architecturally incapable of processing unstructured multimodal business data (audio, video, voice conversations) — the primary channels where modern work actually happens
- AI-assisted email personalization tools (e.g. Outreach) still require humans to write email templates, with content refreshed only a few times per year — preventing true real-time personalization at scale
- AI revenue intelligence platforms (e.g. Clari) fail to adapt to structural business changes — territory redesigns, rep turnover, macroeconomic shifts — making their pipeline predictions brittle in dynamic real-world conditions
- After seven years of heavy investment, no Systems of Intelligence company has achieved $1B+ revenue — implying the architectural approach is fundamentally limited rather than execution-constrained
- AI agent systems in enterprise contexts still require human intervention for high-stakes decisions — full autonomy is only applied to lower-intent, lower-value interactions; humans remain in the loop for critical actions
- Systems of Record (SAP, Oracle, Salesforce) are deeply embedded in enterprises due to switching costs and network effects — AI-native alternatives face a structural moat even when technically superior
- No discussion of security, compliance, audit trails, or data governance for autonomous agent systems handling sensitive enterprise data — a conspicuous absence given regulated industries like healthcare and HR are highlighted
- All agent system examples are narrow vertical deployments (sales, healthcare intake, recruitment) — there is no evidence of general-purpose enterprise agent deployment across heterogeneous workflows

## Bottlenecks

- Enterprise data is fragmented across siloed Systems of Record, Engagement, and Intelligence — AI agents cannot access a unified view of business context, forcing them to either integrate with multiple legacy APIs or start from scratch
- Human-dependent data entry creates poisoned training and inference foundations — AI analytics built on incomplete CRM/ERP data produces unreliable outputs regardless of model quality
- Incumbent SaaS platforms (SAP, Oracle, Workday, Salesforce) hold entrenched positions via switching costs and network effects — AI-native agent systems must either integrate with or displace these systems, both paths facing significant friction

## Breakthroughs

- Narrow-vertical AI agent systems demonstrated as viable full-stack replacements for enterprise software — not an additive analytics layer but a unified system that captures data, drives engagement, and generates intelligence autonomously
