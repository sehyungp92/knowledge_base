# Eric Vishria: Where is the Value in AI - Chips, Models or Apps? | E1206

> A venture capitalist's framework for navigating the current AI platform shift, covering where value accretes across the stack (chips, foundation models, applications), why foundational models are the fastest depreciating assets in history, how the monetization model for LLMs remains unsettled, and what criteria distinguish enduring AI companies from those that will be crushed by incumbents or commoditization.

**Authors:** Eric Vishria (Benchmark Capital)
**Published:** 2024-09-25
**Type:** video

---

## Expert Analysis

[Source](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dKQLq4NxMro)

Vishria opens with a meta-point about investing in platform shifts: the right quality to look for in founders is not a finished answer but a demonstrated capacity for continuous learning and hypothesis revision. Conditions change, new information arrives, and the correct path must be renegotiated in real time. False precision at the early stage is specifically harmful because the upside is unknowable while the risks are identifiable. This cognitive disposition matters more in AI than in prior cycles precisely because the shift may be larger than any previous technology transition.

On the question of where value accretes, Vishria is clear that foundation models occupy a structurally precarious position. He characterises them as "the fastest depreciating asset in human history," meaning that each successive generation of models commoditizes the prior one at an accelerating rate. Benchmark holds no foundational model investments, a deliberate stance. The implication is that the foundation model layer benefits the ecosystem broadly (cheaper inference, better capabilities) while destroying durable value for any individual model company. The economics of training are so extreme that even oil-state-level capital may not be sufficient to sustain them long-term.

At the infrastructure layer, Vishria acknowledges Nvidia's current dominance but does not expect it to persist as the sole player. He treats GPU infrastructure as strategically important but ultimately not the primary site of durable value creation for venture-stage investing.

The more interesting territory is applications, but here he identifies a structural problem: the monetisation model has not been figured out. The analogy he reaches for is early web search, which generated obvious user value for years before paid search unlocked the commercial model in 2000-2001. He argues that a $20/month subscription is obviously wrong for LLMs, that seat-based pricing is insufficient, and that the right model will be considerably more sophisticated, likely outcome- or value-tied. What is unambiguous is that demand exists: customers describe AI products as "magic" and adopt them quickly. The uncertainty is entirely on the revenue capture side.

He frames the AI coding market as the clearest case study. A software engineer costs roughly $200,000 per year fully loaded, with approximately $10,000 attributable to tooling. Hundreds of billions of market cap have already been created on that $10,000 of tool spend. If AI can genuinely capture a meaningful fraction of the $200,000 of human labour value rather than the $10,000 of tool spend, the addressable market expands by roughly 20x. That prize logic applies across every vertical.

On competition dynamics, Vishria is candid about the distribution threat. Incumbents like Microsoft, which can bundle AI features into existing enterprise relationships, could plausibly crush superior point solutions purely through packaging. This creates a structural headwind for vertical AI startups: product quality and technical superiority may be necessary but insufficient, and distribution moats may dominate. He stresses that in a hypercompetitive market, the bar for believing in a specific entrepreneur's execution ability and insight must be correspondingly higher.

He also surfaces the innovator's dilemma as a live concern for incumbent software companies. Adopting AI often requires dismantling existing business models in the short term, which creates organizational inertia even among companies that fully understand the imperative. This dynamic can create windows for startups, particularly at the early stage where the transformation cost is lower and the upside less knowable (making false precision less damaging).

One notable absence: the conversation is conducted entirely in business-model and market-structure terms. Technical performance constraints, hallucination rates, inference costs, accuracy limitations, and training requirements are not discussed. This reflects investor discourse norms but means the analysis implicitly assumes that underlying technical limitations will continue to resolve, which is itself an unchecked assumption.

## Key Claims

1. Foundational AI models are the fastest depreciating asset in human history. — *"foundational models are the fastest depreciating asset in human history"*
2. Nvidia will not remain the only major player in AI infrastructure. — *"I don't believe Nvidia is going to be the only game in town on the infrastructure layer"*
3. The current AI shift could be bigger than all previous technology shifts combined. — *"we have a major major shift in AI which could be bigger than any of these other shifts maybe combined"*
4. Career investors are better investors than operators who transition to investing, because they accumulate 30-35 companies at depth and hundreds of pitches over 15 years, enabling longitudinal pattern matching. — *"if you're a career investor over 15 years you will have seen 30 or 35 companies at some level of depth"*
5. Career investors are not necessarily better board members, advisors, or empathetic partners, because they lack hands-on operating experience and tend to treat startup outcomes as more deterministic than they are. — *"not necessarily better board members not necessarily better advisors"*
6. Poor distribution was the primary reason Rockmelt failed, despite a good team and interesting product. — *"distribution for a browser brutal and so it just didn't work"*
7. Startup success is not deterministic: even a perfect evaluation of team, timing, and product at inception cannot guarantee an outcome.
8. A software engineer's fully loaded cost is approximately $200,000/year, with roughly $10,000 attributable to software tooling. — *"let's say your fully loaded software person is 200 grand for argument's sake and if you look at the tool spend it's maybe $10,000"*
9. Hundreds of billions of dollars of market cap have been created on approximately $10,000/year of software tool spend per engineer. — *"on $10,000 worth of spend for each of these software engineers"*
10. If AI can capture a substantial portion of the $200,000 human labour value (not just the $10,000 tool spend), value creation in AI coding could be ~20x larger than current software tooling. — *"if you can get AI good enough to eat much more of the $200,000 of value attributed to your software engineer"*
11. The monetisation model for LLMs has not yet been established, analogous to web search engines before paid search was invented. — *"I don't know that we figured out the monetisation model"*
12. Search engines did not figure out monetisation until approximately 2001, five to six years after widespread adoption.
13. A $20/month subscription is not the right monetisation model for LLMs. — *"it's not going to be a $20 a month subscription, that's not the right way"*
14. [[entities/benchmark-capital|Benchmark Capital]] holds no foundational model investments. — *"we have no foundational model investments thus far anyway"*

## Landscape Contributions

### Capabilities

- **AI medical scribes** can automatically generate clinical documentation from doctor-patient interactions (maturity: narrow_production)
  > *"there's a bunch of these AI medical scribes and you look at the proposition... it's a better way of doing this so it is not difficult to imagine a market will be created"*

- **AI coding assistants** can generate code and augment software engineering workflows at production scale (maturity: narrow_production)
  > *"we've seen a tremendous amount of capital go into AI coding and copilot and AI software engineers"*

- **Foundation models/LLMs** can perform diverse tasks across domains and are perceived as transformative by customers (maturity: broad_production)
  > *"customers perceive a lot of these products as magic, which they are basically magic, and they want them"*

### Limitations

- **Foundation model commoditization:** models are rapidly losing differentiation, making them the fastest depreciating assets in the history of technology (severity: significant, trajectory: worsening)
  > *"foundational models are the fastest depreciating asset in human history"*

- **Unsettled monetisation:** no clear commercial model exists for foundation models and LLM applications despite widespread deployment and strong demand signals (severity: significant, trajectory: improving)
  > *"I don't know that we figured out the monetisation model but I think what we can say very clearly is that customers perceive a lot of these products as magic"*

- **Survival uncertainty for foundation model companies:** commoditisation combined with extreme capital requirements creates a structurally precarious position for model companies (severity: blocking, trajectory: unclear)
  > *"don't know how those companies are going to survive... a hundred billion even for the oil-state companies of technology"*

- **Incumbent distribution moats:** large incumbents can bundle AI capabilities into existing enterprise relationships and crush superior point solutions regardless of product quality (severity: significant, trajectory: worsening)
  > *"Microsoft has such large distribution, could just crush everybody... you're a 10% better product... you have to factor that in"*

- **Innovator's dilemma for incumbents:** adopting AI often requires dismantling existing revenue models in the short term, creating organizational inertia even among well-resourced companies (severity: significant, trajectory: stable)
  > *"some variant of the innovator's dilemma, which is like you have to make the platform shift and in order to make the platform shift for a company like..."*

- **Technical limitations absent from discourse:** the entire business-model analysis proceeds without engaging training costs, inference latency, hallucination rates, or accuracy constraints, leaving a critical blind spot in the investment framework (severity: moderate, trajectory: unclear)
  > *The entire interview discusses business model, market dynamics, and competition without mentioning training costs, inference latency, accuracy issues, or compute scaling limits.*

### Bottlenecks

- **Unsettled monetisation models** are blocking sustainable commercial deployment and durable value capture for LLM applications (blocking: sustainable business models and long-term viability of AI companies, horizon: 1-2 years)
  > *"it's not going to be a price-per-seat model... it's not going to be just bundled API... there's going to be much more sophisticated ways"*

- **Foundation model commoditization and defensibility** create a structural survival problem for companies making massive training investments with no durable moat (blocking: long-term viability of foundation model companies, horizon: possibly_fundamental)
  > *"foundational models are the fastest depreciating asset in human history... the foundational model war benefits us all in a way"*

- **Incumbent distribution power** constrains vertical AI applications from establishing independent market positions, regardless of technical differentiation (blocking: vertical AI startups from defending market position against bundled incumbents, horizon: 5+ years)
  > *"Microsoft has such large distribution, could just crush everybody... you're a 10% better product"*
