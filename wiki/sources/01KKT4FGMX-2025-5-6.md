# Multimodal AMIE: Conversational Diagnostic AI with Visual Artifact Integration

> Extends the text-only AMIE diagnostic dialogue system with a state-aware multimodal reasoning framework that dynamically integrates skin photos, ECG tracings, and clinical documents into live consultations — demonstrating broadly superior performance to board-certified primary care physicians in a randomized OSCE study across diagnostic accuracy, empathy, and artifact reasoning.

**Authors:** Google AMIE Team
**Published:** 2025-05-06
**Type:** paper

---

## Motivation

Prior LLM-based diagnostic systems like the original AMIE operated exclusively in text-only settings — a fundamental mismatch with real-world telehealth, where patients routinely share photos of skin conditions, ECG printouts, and clinical documents. Patients often cannot articulate precise lab values or visual findings in text, and the absence of visual processing creates diagnostic blind spots while potentially exacerbating health disparities for those with lower digital literacy.

Existing [[themes/multimodal_models|multimodal]] medical AI (LLaVa-Med, Med-Flamingo, SkinGPT-4) addressed visual question answering or single-modality tasks — not the integrated flow of a complete diagnostic consultation. No work had systematically studied uncertainty-driven acquisition of multimodal artifacts mid-dialogue, or validated LLM diagnostic systems against clinicians in multimodal settings.

---

## Approach

### State-Aware Dialogue Framework

Multimodal AMIE introduces a phase-transition architecture built on Gemini 2.0 Flash that structures consultations into three explicit phases:

1. **History Taking** — symptom elicitation, dynamic differential diagnosis (DDx) building, knowledge-gap identification
2. **Diagnosis & Management** — plan generation from converged internal state
3. **Follow-up** — clarification and patient communication

Phase transitions are triggered by internal state evaluation — whether the system's uncertainty has been resolved — rather than fixed turn counts. This emulates the adaptive pacing of experienced clinicians.

A critical design choice distinguishes this from prior work: **artifact acquisition is uncertainty-driven**. The system requests images or documents specifically when its evolving DDx has unresolved uncertainty that the artifact would reduce — not passively or universally. Upon receipt, it prompts for structured interpretation (lesion morphology, ECG waveform features) before integrating findings into ongoing reasoning.

### Evaluation Infrastructure

The paper contributes a three-component simulation environment:
- Synthetic patient scenario generation from real medical image datasets (SCIN for dermatology, PTB-XL for ECG), augmented with clinically plausible metadata
- Turn-by-turn multimodal dialogue simulation between doctor and patient agents
- An automated rater (Gemini 2.0 Flash) assessing DDx accuracy, information gathering, management quality, and hallucination rate

A dedicated **Multimodal Understanding & Handling (MUH) rubric** was developed for OSCE evaluation, covering artifact engagement, isolated interpretation, proactive artifact requests, artifact-grounded reasoning, and communication of visual findings to patients.

## Results

In a randomized, double-blind OSCE with **25 patient actors**, **19 board-certified PCPs**, and **18 specialist evaluators** across **105 multimodal scenarios**:

- AMIE outperformed PCPs on **7 of 9 MUH rubric axes** and **29 of 32 non-multimodal axes** (p < 0.001 for overall diagnostic accuracy)
- AMIE's top-k DDx accuracy exceeded PCPs for all k from 1–10; its broader differential was more comprehensive and more likely to include the ground truth
- Patient actors rated AMIE non-inferior or superior across all patient-centric dimensions including empathy, listening, and trustworthiness (p < 0.01 for all axes)
- AMIE demonstrated significantly greater robustness to **low image quality** — when specialist-rated artifact quality was poor, AMIE's top-3 DDx accuracy dropped substantially less than PCPs'

A critical finding: **dialogue history is not supplementary — it is necessary**. The "images-only" condition yielded markedly lower accuracy, confirming that image and conversational evidence must be cross-referenced rather than treated as independent inputs.

Ablations confirm that the state-aware framework consistently improves performance across all metrics and all tested datasets compared to vanilla baselines.

The inference-time approach is notable: all gains are achieved through prompting and structured reasoning over a base model, with no task-specific supervised fine-tuning. This avoids catastrophic forgetting while delivering consistent improvements — a finding with implications beyond medicine for any domain requiring structured conversational reasoning.

## Limitations & Open Questions

### Evaluation Validity

The most significant concern is the gap between controlled evaluation and real-world deployment. All results come from **trained patient actors in OSCE-style scenarios**, not real patients. Scenarios were constructed post-hoc from image metadata — there is no guarantee they reflect genuine case histories. The auto-rater was calibrated against a prior text-only OSCE study, and its reliability for multimodal dialogue quality has not been independently validated.

Comparative evaluation also risks **unblinding**: systematic stylistic differences between AI and PCP responses make true blinding difficult to achieve, potentially confounding human evaluator ratings.

### Modality-Specific Gaps

ECG interpretation remains a hard bottleneck. AMIE achieves only **0.28 top-1 accuracy on PTB-XL** vs. **0.98 on clinical documents** and **0.84 on dermatology** — reflecting fundamental challenges in extracting quantitative waveform features from static images. Statistical superiority over PCPs is **not significant for ECG or skin photo scenarios** individually; only clinical documents show significant improvement. The study covers only three modality types — radiology, ophthalmology, wound care, and pathology are untested.

### System Architecture Constraints

- The state-aware framework has **rigid phase structure**: new critical information (allergies, contraindications) emerging after a management plan has been conveyed is not handled gracefully
- The multi-call inference architecture (state tracking, DDx generation, continuation decisions) implies **high per-consultation compute cost**, not quantified in the paper
- The system is **not open-sourced** and depends on internal Google infrastructure, blocking independent replication
- **Real-time video consultation** — the most common telemedicine modality — is entirely out of scope; only static image upload via text chat is addressed

### Fine-Tuning Counterproductivity

Supervised fine-tuning on small domain-specific medical datasets **degrades** conversational quality and management appropriateness, particularly for dermatology and ECG. SFT improved ECG diagnostic accuracy in isolation but harmed other consultation dimensions — suggesting that domain specialization via fine-tuning is currently counterproductive for integrated consultation agents. This points to a deeper tension between task-specific optimization and holistic consultation quality.

### Clinical Readiness

AMIE cannot perform clinical actions — it cannot issue referrals, order tests, or prescribe — making it a triage/information tool rather than a full clinical agent. Robustness, equity validation, and real-world generalizability across diverse patient populations, rare conditions, and underrepresented demographics remain unaddressed. The authors explicitly state AMIE is not intended for clinical use.

## Bottlenecks Addressed & Remaining

This work makes progress on the **multimodal integration bottleneck** in conversational diagnostic AI, but surfaces and clarifies several unresolved ones:

- **ECG temporal signal interpretation** remains unsolved for VLM-based approaches — 28% top-1 accuracy indicates this is not merely a scale problem
- **Clinical validation gap**: simulated OSCE results are insufficient to establish clinical readiness; prospective real-patient studies are required before regulatory consideration (horizon: 3–5 years)
- **Standardized evaluation metrics** for multimodal diagnostic dialogue do not exist — this paper contributes the MUH rubric as a starting point, but field-wide adoption is needed
- **Catastrophic forgetting from domain fine-tuning** blocks the creation of specialized medical models that excel at targeted tasks without degrading holistic consultation quality (horizon: 1–2 years)
- **Optimal multimodal fusion strategy** in interactive clinical dialogue — managing heterogeneous data types, cross-modal reasoning mid-conversation — remains an open research problem (horizon: 3–5 years)

---

## Breakthroughs

Two findings in this paper are significant relative to the prior state of the field:

1. **AI surpassing board-certified physicians in multimodal diagnostic conversation** — including dimensions like empathy and artifact-grounded communication previously assumed to require human judgment. This is not merely a narrow benchmark improvement but a broad evaluation across specialist-rated axes.

2. **Inference-time reasoning beating fine-tuned models** — the state-aware framework achieves consistent gains across modalities without any task-specific training, reinforcing a pattern visible across multiple domains: structured inference-time computation can substitute for or exceed training-time specialization.
