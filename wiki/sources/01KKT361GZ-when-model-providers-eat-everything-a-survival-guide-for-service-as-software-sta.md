## Briefing

**Foundation Capital argues that the existential threat to app-layer AI startups — model providers moving up the stack and cannibalizing successful use cases — can be neutralized only by building proprietary feedback loops that compound through real-world interaction data, something no frontier lab can replicate from the outside. The five defensive strategies (high-exception workflows, ambiguous-domain feedback, execution-layer ownership, domain expertise embedding, and speed-with-depth) all point to a single meta-strategy: own the learning loop, not just the product.**

### Key Takeaways
1. **Model providers have panoramic API visibility** — Because labs see which domains drive heavy usage and which features perform best across thousands of apps, successful startups inadvertently signal exactly where providers should build competing products.
2. **The "tailwind principle" is necessary but insufficient** — The canonical advice to architect so that model improvements help rather than replace you is correct but hard to achieve in practice; most startups haven't solved the "how."
3. **RL transforms the competitive dynamic in startups' favor** — Unlike historical data stockpiles (an incumbent advantage), RL-driven feedback loops favor whoever is embedded in live workflows — a position startups can occupy that frontier labs cannot.
4. **The real proprietary asset is the interaction data stream** — Clicks, decisions, and outcomes generated by agents working alongside users compound into a learning advantage; **whoever captures that loop builds a moat the model itself cannot replicate.**
5. **Custom training environments are a frontier lab priority** — OpenAI and Anthropic are actively training agents inside simulated versions of the top 150 websites and major systems of record, raising the competitive bar for generic workflow automation.
6. **High-exception, industry-specific workflows are the best moat** — Rules that vary by policy type, jurisdiction, and edge case are too costly for generalist providers to master per-industry; focused startups turn that complexity into training data.
7. **Evaluators and reward functions are where domain knowledge crystallizes** — Defining what "success" means in a fuzzy domain (e.g., "conversions per rep per channel" rather than "emails sent") is itself a defensible capability that generic models cannot replicate.
8. **The execution layer — legacy systems, shadow processes, undocumented APIs — is structurally underserved by frontier labs** — Startups willing to own the "duct-tape layer" of enterprise software inherit a moat by default, since labs won't invest there.
9. **Domain experts on the founding team are a strategic asset, not just a hiring preference** — Teams with former practitioners (e.g., Regie's sales-engagement execs) can define reward functions and edge-case logic that purely technical teams cannot.
10. **Speed compounds asymmetrically in the agentic era** — Every day earlier a startup ships means more interaction data, faster feedback loops, and model improvements that widen the gap against later entrants, including the labs themselves if they build competing products.
11. **Windsurf-style "special treatment to acquisition target" dynamics are a structural risk** — Early preferential API terms can mask a provider's intention to acquire or out-compete, making independence and data moats more urgent from day one.
12. **Credit assignment across long-horizon tasks is an unsolved infrastructure problem** — Feedback that arrives hours or days after the causal action requires replay buffers, asynchronous rollouts, and advanced attribution techniques that most startups aren't yet building.

### The Three Waves of App-Layer AI: A Compression of Competitive History

- **Wave 1 (2022) — The Wrapper Era:** Startups built thin products: a clever prompt behind a clean UI sitting on a foundation model API.
  - These were fast to ship but equally fast to copy, and as models improved, their value propositions were absorbed directly into the base model or became trivially replicable.
  - The era established the core risk pattern: building on a platform whose improving capabilities undercut the startup's differentiation.

- **Wave 2 (2023–2024) — The AI-Native Era:** Teams designed from first principles around LLM capabilities, built custom data pipelines, and embedded AI tightly into user workflows.
  - This was a meaningful step up in defensibility, but model providers responded by moving up the stack — launching their own apps, changing API terms, and building competing products.
  - Many Wave 2 startups were caught between the platform they depended on and the competing product that platform then released.

- **Wave 3 (2025) — The Agentic Era:** New reasoning models and post-training techniques blur the line between infrastructure and end-user application.
  - OpenAI's Agent Mode and Anthropic's Claude Code are direct examples of providers shipping agentic products to end users, competing with startups in their own product categories.
  - **The bar for "AI-native" has risen: leveraging a powerful model is no longer sufficient; startups must be building learning systems that adapt and improve over time.**

## Capabilities

- RL-driven agents can learn long-horizon workflow patterns from real user interactions — e.g., inferring optimal follow-up timing, stakeholder routing, and channel selection across a full sales cycle without explicit programming
- A universal verifier model can grade another model's outputs step-by-step, cross-checking facts and logic against sources — enabling automated scoring of subjective tasks like creative writing and advanced math proofs
- AI achieves gold medal performance at the International Mathematical Olympiad
- Major labs are training agents inside simulated versions of the top 150 websites and every major system of record — enabling agents that understand real-world software workflows from deployment
- Coding agents can reason across codebases, tickets, telemetry, and architectural graphs — learning from patterns of failure and resolution to continuously improve incident response
- RL gyms and RL-as-a-service tooling are available as infrastructure for AI labs to train agents in domain-specific simulated environments
- AI agents can complete end-to-end contract workflows (DocuSign) from a short natural language prompt by owning the full execution surface including data records, templates, fields, and signatories
- Foundation model providers have built panoramic visibility into which domains drive heavy API usage, which prompts/features produce best results, and which new apps are gaining traction — enabling rapid product replication

## Limitations

- Long-horizon tasks produce sparse and delayed feedback — rewards arrive minutes, hours, or days after the actions that caused them, making credit assignment intractable with standard RL approaches
- The classic credit assignment problem: in complex multi-step tasks involving hundreds of actions, agents cannot determine which intermediate actions were responsible for eventual success or failure
- Off-the-shelf foundation models cannot handle the full variability of industry-specific edge cases, exceptions, and constantly changing rules — requiring domain-specific fine-tuning or human-in-the-loop architectures
- Frontier labs lack visibility into inter-product workflows — the decisions, hand-offs, and multi-tool processes that occur outside any single application's UI — limiting their ability to train agents on real end-to-end work patterns
- Static historical data stockpiles — previously considered a decisive moat for incumbents — are becoming less decisive as AI capabilities advance and shift toward requiring real-world interaction feedback
- Building long-horizon interactive intelligence for agents requires fundamentally new approaches in both AI research and engineering — current methods are not sufficient
- In domains where ground truth is fuzzy or subjective, frontier labs struggle to construct effective RL training signals — they lack the domain-specific rubrics, evaluators, and reward functions needed
- Training agents on real-world long-horizon tasks requires specialized RL infrastructure (replay buffers, asynchronous rollouts, advanced credit assignment) that is not yet standardized or widely accessible
- Simple 'wrapper' AI apps (Wave 1, 2022) face an inherent obsolescence cliff: any capability they deliver via prompt engineering gets subsumed into base model improvements, making their differentiation ephemeral
- App-layer AI startups built on API access face structural dependency risk: the model provider that powers them has full visibility into their usage patterns and can replicate or acquire them at any time
- AI agents operating in shadow enterprise processes (undocumented SOPs, desktop procedures, unofficial workarounds) cannot be trained without first mapping and formalizing those processes — which requires deep customer embedding

## Bottlenecks

- Credit assignment in long-horizon RL: agents cannot determine which of hundreds of intermediate actions caused eventual success or failure when outcomes are separated from actions by extended time and many steps
- Absence of standardized ground-truth evaluators for subjective and ambiguous domains prevents effective RL training outside of narrow, verifiable task categories
- Proprietary cross-product interaction data is structurally inaccessible to frontier labs — real end-to-end workflow data spanning multiple tools and systems can only be captured by embedded application-layer agents
- Sparse and delayed reward infrastructure: off-the-shelf ML training infrastructure cannot support the online, asynchronous, temporally-extended rollouts needed to train agents on real-world long-horizon tasks
- Defensible product moat construction: the principle that model improvements should be tailwinds not threats is conceptually clear but practically very difficult to architect without owning proprietary feedback loops

## Breakthroughs

- Reinforcement learning has emerged as the core training paradigm for the agentic era — enabling AI systems that continuously learn from real-world interactions rather than from static datasets, fundamentally changing competitive dynamics in AI
- A universal verifier architecture — using one AI model to grade another's outputs step-by-step against sources — enables automated quality scoring for tasks previously considered unscorable by AI, including creative work and advanced mathematics
- AI achieves gold medal performance at the International Mathematical Olympiad — the first time AI has reached the top tier of competitive human mathematical reasoning
