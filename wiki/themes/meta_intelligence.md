# Intelligence & Learning

> Intelligence & Learning sits at the conceptual core of the AI field — encompassing the mechanisms by which systems acquire, represent, and apply knowledge. As of early 2026, the theme is defined by a productive tension: frontier models exhibit striking emergent reasoning and generalisation capabilities, yet the underlying mechanisms remain poorly understood, and fundamental questions about sample efficiency, compositional generalisation, and robust out-of-distribution performance remain open. Momentum is building around test-time compute scaling, hybrid neuro-symbolic approaches, and reinforcement learning from human and AI feedback, but the field has not converged on a unified theory of machine learning — and whether one is needed or possible is itself contested.

## Current State

The study of how machines learn and exhibit intelligent behaviour has undergone a structural shift over the past several years. The dominant paradigm — large-scale pretraining on diverse corpora followed by alignment — has produced systems with broad, transferable competence that no earlier learning framework predicted. The question has moved from *can neural networks learn anything useful at scale* to *what are the precise limits of what scaled learning achieves, and what falls irreducibly outside it*.

Reasoning and planning have become the primary battleground. Benchmark saturation on static evaluations has pushed the field toward dynamic, multi-step, and adversarially constructed tasks where brittleness is more visible. Test-time scaling — investing more compute at inference through chain-of-thought, search, and self-consistency — has emerged as a complementary axis to training-time scaling, partially decoupling capability from parameter count.

Reinforcement learning has re-entered the spotlight as a mechanism for sharpening reasoning post-pretraining. RLHF and its successors (RLAIF, GRPO, process-reward models) are now standard components of frontier training pipelines, with active research into when RL fine-tuning generalises versus overfits to reward hacking.

The multimodal frontier has expanded from vision-language to include audio, video, and structured data, raising new questions about how representations learned in one modality transfer to another — a proxy for deeper questions about compositionality and abstraction.
