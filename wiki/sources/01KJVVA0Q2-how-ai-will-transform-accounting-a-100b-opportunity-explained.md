# How AI Will Transform Accounting: A $100B Opportunity Explained

**Authors:** 
**Published:** 2024-12-17 00:00:00
**Type:** video

## Analysis

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OPRJI8Djfq8

Market context 

- The accounting profession is at risk of a significant decline in the number of professionals.
	- 75% of CPAs are set to retire in the next decade and the new rate of CPA graduates is nowhere near going to match that. Even between 2019 to 2022, there was a 16% drop in the number of accountants.
- There are 2 notable market trends that are creating an in interesting why now moment:
	- Although, there is a decline in the number of people who want to become accountants or be in the accounting field in general, e.g., bookkeepers and people in corporate accounting roles, the amount of accounting work and the complexity of tax has only increased over time. So the demand for these professionals from consumers and businesses is increasing.
		- As a result, firms are struggling to meet this demand, which is putting even more pressure on the professionals.
	- Even with the decline in the number of professionals, it is still a huge market. There are ~1.5 million accountants and auditors, and when broadened to include accounting-related positions, whether it's in corporate finance departments, bookkeeping, payroll, etc., there are over 3 million individuals, which is probably well over $100 billion of spending and wages. 
		- At the end of the day, this is a professional service business, and so to the extent that the overall workload is remaining the same or increasing, but the number of staff that are hired and retained to perform that work is either remaining flat or declining, it becomes harder for each individual to provide the same level of service to their customer, which translates into pressure.
		- With the demographic trends, there is great urgency to fill the labour gaps, not just with additional humans, but with software, which makes this one perhaps more compelling relative to other professional services verticals.
- To assess whether professional services verticals are ripe for AI, people tend to look whether there are there a lot of highly paid people who are doing in many cases repetitive work that probably could benefit from smarter automation. Accounting seems to fit this description well. 
- However, unlike law, which is primarily a text-dominated business, which is well aligned with the current capabilities of LLMs, i.e., very capable at handling a lot of the querying and research and inputs and outputs that the legal profession requires, accounting on the other hand is more quantitative in nature, dealing with numbers all the time, which may be why AI has been less prominent in the industry.
	- So, there is not an equivalent of a company like Harvey in accounting just yet, which could also be a function of other vertical-specific factors.

Implementation challenges

- Accounting, like some other professional services, is very high-stakes work that has very limited tolerance for incorrectness. So whereas the more creative fields might benefit from the hallucinations of LLMs, the contents of your tax return and your financial statement audits just have to be 100% accurate. 
	- So, while firms are enthusiastic and eager to buy things and be forward leaning, they are very conservative about what will get rolled out and how quickly it will get rolled out just to make sure that they are delivering a gold standard that they always have.
- Also, historically there may have been a potential incentive misalignment or business model hurdle, as the accounting industry, in general, has tended to run on the business model of billable hours. And so if as a result of a software, the accountants can perform their job in 20% of the time it used to take them, that should, in theory, reduce their billable hours. 
	- This may be a short-term hurdle, and the market may trend over time towards a fixed-fee engagement model and the competitive pressures of supply and demand will meant that if you can deliver the same work product at a fraction of the cost, then you actually should be billing less. 
	- This has huge implications about who you're trying to sell the software to. If you're trying to sell this AI software to an individual contributor, they might be a little bit sceptical or threatened. Whereas if you're selling it to the firm leadership, and they see the clear ROI opportunity in the business, it may be an easier sell.
- Most companies that had success in the accounting ecosystem sold through accounting firms, which meant getting these large firms and small firms to be your allies and distribute your product. 
	- This still may be an effective way of distributing products, and this may be the first time that a software company can build a very large business just selling to accounting firms as well. 

Applying LLM strengths to accounting

- The canonical examples of LLMs excel at are data collection and ingestion. And in the context of accounting, this means automating extraction and reconciliation
	- LLMs will be very good at making sense of unstructured data, such as general ledgers, bank statements, receipts, PDFs of various vendor contracts, and all sorts of things that exist in different formats and different systems, and translating that into an accounting report. 
		- This is where AI has gained most early traction. 
- LLMs again excel at text in, text out, so they will also be good for research purposes. 
	- For instance, if you want an opinion on how you should treat a certain transaction or you want to look something up in generally accepted accounting principles, using LLMs could provide a very robust opinion with citations based on precedent cases or different ways to think about it.
- Apart from these 2 jobs that represent the majority of the activity, there is increasingly more being done on the report generation and filing, which is a bit more tax specific and involves rebuilding the tax engine using AI. ## Key Claims

1. Approximately 75% of CPAs are set to retire in the next decade.
2. The number of accountants dropped approximately 16% between 2019 and 2022.
3. The new rate of CPA graduates is not sufficient to replace retiring CPAs.
4. There are approximately 1.5 million accountants and auditors in the US.
5. Accounting-related positions including corporate finance, bookkeeping, and payroll total over 3 million individuals.
6. The accounting profession represents well over $100 billion in wages.
7. Accounting software in use today is largely legacy software developed during the Reagan Era.
8. Accounting has been slower to adopt AI than legal services due to its quantitative nature, which is less naturally suited to text-in/text-out LLMs.
9. There is no breakout AI company in accounting comparable to Harvey in legal services.
10. Accounting work requires 100% accuracy, creating a high barrier for AI adoption due to low tolerance for hallucinations.

## Capabilities

- LLMs can ingest and process unstructured accounting data in various formats (bank statements, receipts, PDFs of contracts) and convert them into structured journal entries and accounting reports
- LLMs can provide research assistance and generate opinions on accounting treatments and GAAP compliance, retrieving relevant precedents and alternative interpretations
- LLMs can partially automate repetitive manual work in accounting, reducing task volume for highly paid professionals in professional services
- LLMs can replicate accounting domain expertise in software development, reducing dependency on large teams of domain expert accountants collaborating with engineers

## Limitations

- LLMs hallucinate and generate inaccurate outputs that are unacceptable for tax returns, financial statements, and audits which require 100% accuracy
- LLMs perform significantly worse on quantitative and numerical reasoning tasks compared to text-based tasks, limiting their effectiveness in accounting which is fundamentally quantitative
- LLMs only excel at 2 of 4 key accounting job-to-be-done tasks (data ingestion and research); report generation and client advisory services remain in very early stages
- Accounting firms cannot rapidly deploy and scale AI due to requirement for 100% accuracy; conservative rollout posture limits deployment scope and speed
- If AI software reduces accounting work by 80%, firms billing by the hour face revenue pressure; 20% of time to deliver same output should theoretically reduce billable hours and firm revenue
- Accounting software ecosystem dominated by decades-old Reagan-era legacy systems that have barely evolved; personal tax software relies on outdated paradigms (even CD-ROMs still in use 10 years ago)
- Individual accountants may resist AI software adoption fearing obsolescence, reducing bottom-up adoption momentum even when their firms deploy solutions

## Bottlenecks

- LLMs optimized for text processing but accounting requires quantitative and numerical reasoning; absence of accounting-specialized models blocks emergence of breakthrough AI-first accounting software companies
- Accounting industry's billable-hours business model is fundamentally incompatible with AI-driven productivity gains; transition to fixed-fee pricing necessary but not yet occurring at scale
- 100% accuracy requirement in tax and audit work creates conservative adoption posture; firms cannot rapidly deploy AI and must maintain gold-standard quality assurance, limiting deployment pace and scope

## Breakthroughs

- LLMs now enable small teams and solo founders to build large accounting software businesses without the 80+ person teams of domain experts previously required
