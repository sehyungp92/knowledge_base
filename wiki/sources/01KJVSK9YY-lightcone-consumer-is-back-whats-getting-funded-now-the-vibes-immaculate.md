# Lightcone: Consumer is back, What's getting funded now, The vibes immaculate

A YC partner retrospective on the Winter 2024 batch, analyzing structural shifts in what's getting funded: the dominance of AI (70% of companies), the return of consumer, the surge in developer tools, and a broader platform reset in which AI has reopened the entire SaaS market to new entrants. Grounded in batch statistics, it traces how founder demographics, pivot behavior, geographic distribution, and startup category mix have all shifted together in response to the AI moment.

**Authors:** YC Partners
**Published:** 2024-04-25
**Type:** video

---

## The AI Supercycle

AI dominates the Winter 2024 batch in a way without historical precedent. Roughly 70% of funded companies (~170) are AI-focused, compared to just 8% in [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|W20]]. This isn't simply more AI investment — it reflects a structural platform shift. As the discussion frames it, AI has reset the opportunity landscape the way the early web did: "there's a platform shift and this is the moment where every single SaaS dollar in the world is up for grabs again."

The batch's aggregate ARR grew from $6M to $20M over three months — a 3x increase — despite over 80% of companies entering with no revenue and no launched product. That early-stage funding profile is itself notable: in W20, ~62% had no revenue on entry; W24 is even earlier, with more companies building from scratch inside the batch.

---

## Consumer Is Back

Consumer startup formation, largely dormant for over a decade, is returning. Founders who previously would have pivoted toward enterprise B2B SaaS are now pivoting into consumer ideas. The analogy to 2005–2012 is explicit: that era saw prolific consumer startup formation, before Facebook and the incumbents consumed all available attention and the only non-tarpit ideas left were B2B.

The argument for why now is different: AI expands the surface area of human-computer interaction so broadly that no single incumbent can occupy all the new spaces. [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|Consumer founders]] thus have genuine white space again — not the tarpit ideas (travel planning, bill splitting, roommate matching) that look easy but attract no one, but genuinely new interaction paradigms enabled by capable AI.

The caveat is real: consumer pivots during a batch can signal intellectual laziness. B2B pivots historically required founders to develop genuine domain expertise. Consumer ideas, especially consumer social, are harder to validate during a batch because success is measured in growth metrics, not revenue — and growth takes time that a three-month program doesn't provide.

## Developer Tools Surge

30% more developer tool companies were funded in W24 than four years ago, making it one of the largest dev tool batches in YC history. The underlying dynamic follows historical infrastructure cycles: tooling proliferates first, application layers follow.

The core limitation driving this is straightforward. Building AI applications today requires enormous amounts of custom engineering:

> "Right now for building an AI app there's so much plumbing you need to do and customize it."

RAG, fine-tuning, indexing, and query optimization patterns exist, but are not yet standardized — every team is reimplementing the same primitives independently. This is the bottleneck: **lack of standardized, reusable AI application tooling** is slowing the entire category.

Developer tools occupy a structural middle ground between consumer and B2B. The addressable market is ~20M developers rather than billions of consumers, but the adoption patterns resemble consumer: early traction is about usage and community signal, not revenue. Open source tools in particular have long monetization runways — the Docker and MongoDB precedent took years before anyone believed open source could be a business.

**Open source strategy:** The playbook for capturing developer tool markets is now well-established — identify any dominant closed-source tool or platform and build an open source version. Examples cited: Slack → Mattermost, GitHub → GitLab, Amplitude → PostHog. In W20, there were 5 open source dev tool companies; W24 has 22. Hacker News launches are treated as a meaningful signal by investors; Supabase's blowout round was attributed directly to its HN reception.

## The Technical Founder Reset

W24 is described as "the most technical batch ever." 99% of companies have a technical founder, up from 88% during the pandemic. Median founder age dropped from ~30 to ~26.

This reverses a specific dynamic from the COVID-era ZIRP period. The "software eats the world" thesis expanded venture appetite to tech-enabled non-software businesses — logistics (Flexport), delivery (DoorDash, Instacart), real estate. That shift tilted the founder profile toward domain experts with operational expertise, often non-technical. Capital was abundant and looking for places to deploy; AI wasn't the obvious destination.

AI reverses all of this:

- Cutting-edge technical knowledge is now a prerequisite, not a differentiator. "If you want to work on a good AI idea you need to be at the cutting edge of actual AI technology and tooling."
- Deep incumbent domain knowledge can be a liability when the technology paradigm is new enough that no one has baggage.
- Younger founders, less anchored to prior platform assumptions, may move faster.

## Geographic and Category Shifts

Two structural reversals worth noting:

**Bay Area concentration:** ~29% of W24 applicants were Bay Area-based when they applied — matching pre-pandemic levels (~29%) and roughly double the pandemic low (~14%). San Francisco is back as the primary startup gravity well.

**International decline:** International companies dropped from ~45% of W20 to ~20% of W24, the most US-centric batch in years. The probable driver is visa uncertainty and the practical difficulty of building cutting-edge AI companies outside the primary talent and infrastructure clusters.

**Marketplaces collapse:** Marketplace companies in W24 are approximately 4x fewer than in W20. The category that dominated the 2010s has been displaced.

**Crypto absence:** Despite Bitcoin hitting all-time highs during the batch period, there was no corresponding surge in crypto applications — a pattern inversion from every prior bull cycle. The explanation is twofold: engineer mindshare has completely shifted to AI, and US regulatory enforcement has created a chilling effect that makes founders unwilling to build in the space.

## Landscape Signals

### Capabilities Demonstrated

- **Autonomous software engineering:** One demo showed an AI agent implementing dark mode on a website from a natural language prompt, completing a full development workflow autonomously. (maturity: demo)
- **Voice AI passing Turing-level evaluation:** A voice agent demo involved a live call that "pretty much passed the Turing test" in natural dialogue quality. (maturity: demo)
- **AI-native CRM/sales automation:** Companies like Octane are rebuilding Salesforce-category products around AI agents. (maturity: narrow production)
- **RAG and fine-tuning for grounded AI outputs:** Emerging but not yet standardized. (maturity: demo)

### Limitations and Open Questions

- **Tooling fragmentation:** Every AI application team is independently solving the same infrastructure problems. No standard stack exists yet. ([[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|Severity: significant, trajectory: improving]])
- **Model obsolescence risk:** Products built on current model capabilities may need architectural rebuilds within months as GPT-5 and next-generation models arrive. This is the sharpest open question for any company building on top of foundation models. ([[themes/ai_business_and_economics|Severity: significant, trajectory: worsening]])
- **Monetization lag for dev tools:** Infrastructure products have long adoption cycles and high switching costs. Early traction metrics (GitHub stars, HN reception, production deployments) matter more than early revenue — but investors still need to believe monetization is coming.
- **Pre-revenue entry:** 80%+ of companies entered W24 with zero revenue and no launched product. The 3x ARR growth over three months is impressive, but the starting baseline was extremely low.

### Bottlenecks Identified

| Bottleneck | Blocking | Horizon |
|---|---|---|
| Lack of standardized AI application tooling | Rapid commercialization of AI apps | Months |
| Rapid model evolution forcing architectural rework | Long-term product stability and planning | Unknown |
| Long dev tool adoption cycles | Monetization of infrastructure products | 1–2 years |

## Platform Reset Thesis

The broader interpretive frame: AI is not just a new product category. It represents a platform reset analogous to the early web — a moment where the opportunity surface expands faster than incumbents can occupy it.

The historical sequence: 2006–07 was pure software. Then "software eats the world" expanded to logistics, food, real estate — culminating in WeWork, where "there was no software at all." AI resets the pendulum back toward software as the primary value driver, but with a larger capability surface than any prior software era.

The practical implication for [[themes/startup_and_investment|startups]]: every existing SaaS category is now contestable. The companies that built dominant positions under prior technical constraints — where AI wasn't a viable tool — are vulnerable to reimplementation. This is what "every single SaaS dollar in the world is up for grabs" means concretely.

What doesn't change: "what ultimately matters is making something people actually want and are willing to pay for." Platform shifts create opportunity; they don't substitute for product-market fit.
