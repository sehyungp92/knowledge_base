# Vertical SaaS

> Vertical SaaS refers to software-as-a-service products built for a specific industry rather than sold horizontally across all sectors. As AI capabilities mature, vertical SaaS is undergoing a structural transformation — AI-native entrants are not merely improving on legacy software but displacing entire categories of third-party services spend, suggesting the total addressable market for vertical AI may dramatically exceed that of the SaaS generation it replaces.

## Overview

Vertical SaaS describes industry-specific software sold to a defined buyer — legal, healthcare, automotive, finance — rather than generic horizontal tooling. The category is well-established: companies like Veeva (pharma), Procore (construction), and Applied Intuition (automotive) demonstrated that deep workflow integration in a single vertical could command premium pricing and high retention. What is now in question is whether the AI wave will merely improve these incumbents or replace them with a fundamentally different type of company operating under a different business model and capturing a larger slice of industry spend.

## The Three Emerging Business Models

According to analysis in Vertical AI shows potential to dwarf legacy SaaS | State of the Cloud 2024, three distinct business models are crystallising among vertical AI entrants:

1. **Co-pilots** — AI tools that augment existing workers, slotting alongside legacy software without displacing it.
2. **Agents** — autonomous systems that replace human workers in defined workflows, shifting the economic logic from seat-based SaaS to outcome-based pricing.
3. **AI-enabled services** — productized delivery of work previously outsourced to third-party service firms (legal document generation, medical scribing, financial analysis), converting services spend into software spend.

The third model is the most structurally disruptive. Vertical AI companies in this mode are not competing for the software budget line — they are competing for the services budget line, which is typically an order of magnitude larger. This reframes the TAM calculation entirely: rather than displacing a $10B SaaS incumbent, a vertical AI company may be addressing a $200B services market in the same vertical.

## Pricing Power as an Early Signal

Despite being earlier-stage than the legacy vertical SaaS companies they sit alongside, vertical AI entrants are already commanding pricing power at approximately 80% of the ACV of existing vertical software, per the State of the Cloud 2024 analysis. The EvenUp case is illustrative: personal injury law firms are paying nearly as much for EvenUp's AI-generated demand letters as they pay for Litify, their core system-of-record workflow software. That Litify is the more foundational piece of infrastructure makes this pricing parity particularly striking — it implies that AI-generated output is valued comparably to the orchestration layer itself, not as an add-on.

Similarly, Abridge in healthcare automates the full medical documentation loop — recording patient conversations, summarizing and transcribing them, and pushing structured data into EMR systems — replacing work previously done manually by physicians and scribes. The value proposition is not incremental productivity improvement; it is workflow elimination.

## The Platform Capture Risk

A recurring concern in the No Priors discussion with Sarah Guo and Elad Gil is the historical pattern of platform consolidation eating adjacent software. Microsoft Office is the canonical case: Excel, PowerPoint, and Word were once independent third-party companies (Lotus, others) before being subsumed into Microsoft's bundled platform. The implication for vertical AI is that today's independent vertical AI entrant may be building toward acquisition or displacement by whichever foundation model lab or hyperscaler achieves sufficient distribution in that vertical.

This risk is asymmetric. Horizontal players (Microsoft Copilot, Google Workspace AI) will compete on distribution; vertical AI startups must compete on depth of workflow integration and proprietary data flywheels specific to their industry. The window of defensibility exists but is time-bounded.

## White Space and Clustering

Elad Gil notes that AI startups are anomalously clustered around a small number of opportunities — legal, coding, healthcare documentation — leaving large amounts of white space unaddressed across other verticals. This clustering is itself a signal: the verticals with early traction share properties (high documentation burden, clear workflow steps, existing services spend, regulatory tolerance for AI assistance) that make them tractable. Verticals lacking these properties remain underserved not because they are less valuable but because the workflow integration problem is harder.

## Open Questions and Limitations

Several structural questions remain unresolved:

- **Defensibility at scale.** If the AI layer is a thin wrapper over foundation models, incumbents with distribution can replicate the product once the market is proven. Vertical AI companies need proprietary data or workflow lock-in before this happens.
- **Regulatory tolerance.** Healthcare and legal are regulated environments. Abridge and EvenUp are operating in spaces where AI-generated output carries liability implications. Regulatory posture will determine how fast agents can replace rather than augment human judgment in these domains.
- **Budget reallocation mechanics.** The services-to-software budget shift requires a buyer who controls both lines — not always the same person. Sales cycles for vertical AI may be longer and more complex than for traditional SaaS because the economic case crosses departmental budget boundaries.
- **Foundation model dependency.** Vertical AI companies built on top of rapidly improving foundation models benefit from capability improvements for free but also face the risk that model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) build directly into their verticals, as foundation models expand context windows (Google Gemini 1.5's 1M token window is cited as a capability inflection) and multimodal reasoning.

The most important open question is whether vertical AI creates net new value sufficient to justify its pricing premium over time, or whether the current pricing power reflects novelty and early-adopter willingness to pay that will compress as the category matures and competition intensifies.
