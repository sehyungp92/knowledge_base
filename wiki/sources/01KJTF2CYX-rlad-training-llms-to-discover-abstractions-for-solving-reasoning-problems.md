### Motivation & Prior Limitations
Standard RL post-training on long chains of thought optimizes for "depth" rather than "breadth," producing increasingly long traces that build on a single reasoning path rather than exploring diverse solution strategies.
- This depth bias causes models to commit to a narrow set of approaches and degenerate into frequent logic switches and verbose exploration — a failure mode referred to in prior literature as "underthinking."
  - Parallel sampling approaches address breadth but without guidance, while sequential search approaches perform worse on harder problems because they get trapped in locally-optimal but globally-suboptimal strategies.
- Existing methods using textual artifacts (RAG, prompt optimization, scratchpads) either rely on static human-written corpora or input-agnostic prompt learning and do not train models to generate their own problem-specific procedural knowledge on demand.
  - Prior RL training does not explicitly decouple the learning signals for "what strategy to pursue" from "how to execute a strategy," making it hard for models to consistently capture and reuse procedures across diverse problems.

---

### Proposed Approach
RLAD introduces "reasoning abstractions" — concise natural language descriptions of procedural and factual knowledge generated by the model itself — and a two-player RL paradigm that jointly trains an abstraction generator and an abstraction-conditioned solution generator.
- The abstraction generator is rewarded based on the improvement in solution accuracy that results from conditioning the solution generator on its proposed abstractions, while the solution generator is separately rewarded for correctness when using those abstractions — decoupling the two learning signals.
  - This structure enables structured exploration: abstractions serve as high-level subgoals, skills, or priors that guide the solution policy, allowing the solution generator to compose procedural information rather than search over it.
  - The abstraction generator is warm-started via supervised fine-tuning on synthetic problem-abstraction pairs, where multiple solution traces are collected with Qwen3 models and a stronger model (o4-mini) distills useful procedural patterns from them; a post-hoc check verifies abstractions do not leak the answer (zero accuracy when sampling from the base model conditioned only on the abstraction).
- Unlike concurrent work that distills interleaved parallel/sequential sampling into a model without RL optimization, RLAD explicitly trains both generators end-to-end via RL, and unlike hand-designed scaffolds, the abstraction interface is learned automatically.

---

### Results & Capabilities
RLAD achieves an average 44% improvement over the state-of-the-art long chain-of-thought RL baseline DAPO on AIME 2025, and shows consistent gains on DeepScaleR Hard and AMC 2023.
- Generating diverse abstractions at test time outperforms sampling more solutions at large test-time compute budgets, demonstrating that abstractions guide meaningful exploration rather than redundant sampling.
- On the ARC-AGI benchmark (90 puzzles drawn evenly from ARC-AGI 1, ARC-AGI 2, and BARC), conditioning Qwen3-4B on a proposed abstraction improves both pass@k accuracy and coverage (unit tests passed) consistently across k=1 through k=16, with max@k coverage rising from 35.0% to 42.9% at k=16.
- The pass@k metric — which rewards both accuracy and diversity — improves with abstractions, confirming that abstractions increase the variety of successful solution strategies the model explores rather than just boosting raw accuracy on a single attempt.

---

### Implications
Abstractions reframe the test-time compute scaling question: rather than asking how many solutions to sample, one should ask how to allocate compute between strategy generation (abstractions) and strategy execution (solutions), with the former becoming increasingly valuable at large budgets.
- The two-player cooperative RL paradigm — where one agent generates guidance and another executes — suggests a modular architecture for reasoning that may generalize beyond math to any domain where procedural and factual sub-knowledge can be summarized from solution trajectories.
- The result that models can learn to generate their own input-specific procedural knowledge — without retrieval from an external corpus and without guidance from a larger model at deployment — points toward self-improving reasoning systems that accumulate and reuse their own discovered strategies.
- The observation that useful abstractions must be long and detailed (short ones degrade performance) implies that the quality of intermediate reasoning representations, not just their presence, is a critical variable in test-time compute scaling research.

---

### Remaining Limitations & Next Steps
The benefits of abstractions are highly conditional on both the quality of the abstraction generator and the capability of the solution model, and most configurations tested do not yield gains.

## Capabilities

- Two-player cooperative RL (RLAD) jointly training an abstraction generator and solution generator achieves an average 44% improvement over DAPO on AIME 2025 math benchmark
- LLMs can be trained via cooperative RL to self-propose input-dependent reasoning abstractions (concise procedural hints) that improve downstream solution accuracy without any external knowledge source at inference time
- Allocating more test-time compute to generating diverse reasoning abstractions yields greater performance gains than generating additional solution samples at large test budgets
- Conditioning Qwen3-4B on model-generated reasoning abstractions improves both pass@k and coverage on ARC-AGI benchmark consistently across k=1 through k=16

## Limitations

- Standard RL post-training on long chains of thought degenerates into verbose depth — sequential verification rather than diverse strategy exploration — causing 'underthinking' with frequent logic switches that fail to escape suboptimal solution paths
- Reasoning abstractions only improve performance when the solution model is sufficiently capable — benefits collapse for small models (Qwen3-0.6B) that cannot parse and act on abstract procedural descriptions
- Useful reasoning abstractions require generation by strong frontier models (o4-mini); weaker models (Qwen-0.6B through 4B) consistently fail to produce abstractions that improve solution accuracy — most abstraction configurations yield neutral or negative gains
- Neither the ability to generate useful reasoning abstractions nor the ability to leverage them in solutions arises naturally in LLMs — both capabilities require explicit RL training to emerge and cannot be obtained through standard pretraining or prompting
- The RLAD warmstart pipeline requires a stronger oracle model (o4-mini) to generate initial abstraction training data via distillation, creating a dependency on proprietary frontier models and blocking fully self-sufficient training
- Generated abstractions require post-hoc verification to ensure they do not inadvertently encode the solution path — an engineering bottleneck that may not scale cleanly to large abstraction generation pipelines
- Sequential test-time compute scaling degrades sharply on hard problems because single-trace iteration gets trapped in locally-optimal but globally-suboptimal strategies, requiring parallel exploration to escape
- RLAD is restricted to verifiable domains — the 0/1 ground-truth reward requirement confines the approach to math and puzzle benchmarks; generalization to open-ended reasoning domains without automatic verifiers is not demonstrated

## Bottlenecks

- Standard single-agent RL post-training for reasoning cannot produce breadth of strategy exploration — depth optimization dominates, producing degenerate long traces that fail to generalize to harder problems requiring novel procedures
- Bootstrapping reasoning abstraction capability without a strong frontier oracle model is unsolved — smaller models cannot generate or leverage abstractions effectively, preventing self-sufficient training pipelines

## Breakthroughs

- RLAD introduces a two-player cooperative RL framework decoupling abstraction proposal from solution generation, enabling structured exploration of reasoning strategies through model-self-proposed procedural hints — achieving 44% gain over DAPO on AIME 2025
