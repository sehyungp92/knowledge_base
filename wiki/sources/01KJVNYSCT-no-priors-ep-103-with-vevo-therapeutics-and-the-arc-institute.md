# No Priors Ep. 103 | With Vevo Therapeutics and the Arc Institute

A conversation exploring the creation of Tahoe-100M, the world's largest single-cell RNA sequencing dataset, and its implications for virtual cell modeling and AI-driven drug discovery. The episode situates this dataset release as a potential inflection point for cellular biology AI analogous to ImageNet's role in computer vision, while honestly addressing how far current virtual cell models still are from practical utility.

**Authors:** Vevo Therapeutics, Arc Institute
**Published:** 2025-02-25
**Type:** video

## Core Contribution

This source documents the release of Tahoe-100M and the Arc Virtual Cell Atlas, framing them as foundational infrastructure for [[themes/medical_and_biology_ai|medical and biology AI]]. The central argument is that cellular biology AI has been bottlenecked not by model architecture but by data quality and quantity — and that this release begins to address that gap. The episode is candid about the current state: virtual cell models are at a GPT-1/2 level of maturity, with ~10% predictive accuracy on differentially expressed genes, and the field lacks even a shared benchmark to measure progress.

---

## The Data Gap Problem

For roughly a decade, single-cell RNA sequencing data accumulated slowly and in fragmented form, produced by individual academic groups or isolated industry efforts. This produced numerous small, disparate datasets with serious batch effects (where the same cell types could appear different across experiments due to varying reagent chemistries). More critically, almost all of this data had two structural limitations:

- **It came from healthy tissue.** Very little of the accumulated public data captures diseased cells, which is precisely what drug discovery requires.
- **It was observational, not perturbational.** Observational data is a snapshot: you take cells from a liver sample and sequence them. Perturbational data asks "what happens to these cells when you apply this drug or edit this gene?" Only perturbational data can answer causal questions about intervention.

Before Tahoe-100M, the entire world's perturbational single-cell dataset totaled roughly 1-2 million data points. The observational corpus was larger: approximately 45-50 million curated human cells before the Arc Virtual Cell Atlas expanded this to 200-230 million. But the perturbational gap was the critical bottleneck.

> "When you put all of the perturbational data sets in the world together, if you're generous it's like one to two million single data points."

---

## Tahoe-100M: What It Is

Tahoe-100M is a perturbational single-cell RNA sequencing dataset of 100 million data points, representing a 50-fold increase over all prior public perturbational data combined. Its key properties:

- **Scale:** 100 million single-cell data points
- **Coverage:** 50 different cancer models from diverse patients
- **Depth:** 1,200 different drug treatments; 60,000 drug-cell line interactions
- **Quality:** Designed to eliminate batch effects through unified experimental conditions
- **Disease focus:** Generated from cancer models, not healthy tissue

The dataset was generated by four people from Vevo Therapeutics over approximately three days, enabled by the Mosaic platform (see below). This speed is itself significant: it signals that the bottleneck was not experimental throughput but the absence of a coordinated effort to generate data at this scale and quality.

The historical parallel offered in the episode is ImageNet (2009), which catalyzed non-linear progress in computer vision not because it improved model architectures but because it provided the data substrate on which meaningful learning could occur. The conjecture is that Tahoe-100M could play the same role for cellular biology.

## The Arc Virtual Cell Atlas

The Arc Virtual Cell Atlas combines Tahoe-100M with a second component: scBasecamp, a harmonized collection of approximately 200-230 million observational single-cell data points assembled from publicly available sources. An AI agent was used to mine, organize, and process this public data, with significant effort to reduce batch effects.

The design philosophy treats perturbational and observational data as complementary rather than redundant:

- **Observational data** captures the diversity of cellular states across tissues, species, and disease conditions; it is broad but causally inert.
- **Perturbational data** captures dynamic cellular responses to interventions; it is causally rich but was historically scarce.

The intended training paradigm is to pre-train models on observational patterns for breadth, then incorporate perturbational data for causal depth. Whether this combination will produce qualitatively different model behavior remains to be demonstrated.

## The Mosaic Platform

The Mosaic platform is the experimental infrastructure that made Tahoe-100M feasible. It enables parallel drug screening across cells from many different cancer patients, pooled into a single "mosaic tumor." This design allows simultaneous measurement of how hundreds of drugs interact with multiple patient-derived cancer models, dramatically compressing the time and cost of generating perturbational data at scale.

The platform is currently at a narrow production maturity: it functions for specific cancer research applications but has not been generalized across all disease areas.

## Virtual Cell Models: Current State

The virtual cell model concept is straightforward in framing: treat the cell's DNA as ROM (read-only memory encoding the genetic program) and RNA as RAM (working memory reflecting the cell's current state in response to its environment). A virtual cell model would function as a "notional CPU" for the cell, predicting how the RNA expression profile (the transcriptome) changes in response to inputs such as drug application or gene editing.

The inverse problem is where the drug discovery application lives: given a diseased cell's transcriptomic profile, what perturbation (drug or gene edit) would move it back toward a healthy state?

**Current performance is poor.** The best models achieve approximately 10% accuracy predicting differentially expressed genes after perturbation. The episode attributes this primarily to data quality rather than architectural limitations: there are rich ML architectures available, but the models haven't had adequate data to learn from. The conjecture is that Tahoe-100M directly addresses this.

The maturity comparison offered: protein language models are past GPT-3 in terms of predictive capability; virtual cell models are at GPT-1 to GPT-2. Reaching "GPT-4" for cell state models might enable practical drug discovery applications, but even then, clinical validation will require 10+ years of additional development.

## Comparing Biology's AI Subfields

The episode draws a useful contrast between the data constraints facing different biological AI domains:

| Domain | Data constraint | Compute constraint |
|---|---|---|
| DNA language models | Low (decades of genome sequencing) | High (context window limits for long sequences) |
| Protein language models | Moderate (structures + functions accumulated over 20 years) | Moderate |
| Cell state / virtual cell models | Severe (perturbational data was near-zero until now) | Lower relative priority |

DNA language models (the Evo series is mentioned) have demonstrated that models trained on raw DNA sequences without explicit biological supervision learn meaningful features: ribosome binding sites, codon degeneracy, pathogenic variant classification. This is encouraging as a proof-of-concept that biological language models can extract genuine knowledge from sequence data. But these models face a hard architectural constraint: context windows limit how much of a DNA sequence (let alone a whole genome) can be consumed at once.

## Open Questions and Limitations

Several significant uncertainties remain unresolved and are acknowledged explicitly:

**Scaling laws are unknown for cell models.** Whether the scaling laws observed in language models transfer to biological systems is empirically open. No one has generated enough data to map the relationship between dataset size and model performance for cell state prediction. Tahoe-100M is the first dataset large enough to begin probing this question.

**Token counting is unclear.** In language models, "tokens" are well-defined. For cellular biology, it is not obvious how to count informative units: not all gene expression measurements are equally informative, and cells are not sentences. How much of the measurable variation constitutes genuine signal versus noise is unresolved.

**No benchmarks exist.** There is currently no accepted benchmark for evaluating virtual cell model quality. The field cannot yet rigorously compare models or measure progress. This is itself a bottleneck: without a shared metric, research efforts cannot coordinate efficiently.

**The clinical gap is long.** Even if virtual cell models reach GPT-4-level capability, drug candidates generated computationally still face 10+ year clinical development cycles with high failure rates (90% of drugs fail in clinical trials). The computational hypothesis is not yet validated, and validation will take a long time.

**Biological time constraints.** Some experimental validation, particularly for aging research, requires multi-year timescales that fundamentally limit iteration speed regardless of computational advances.
