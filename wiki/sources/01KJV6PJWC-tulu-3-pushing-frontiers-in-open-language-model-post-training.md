# Tulu 3: Pushing Frontiers in Open Language Model Post-Training

Tulu 3 presents the first fully open, reproducible four-stage post-training pipeline (data curation, SFT, DPO, and a novel RLVR stage) applied to Llama 3.1 base models at 8B, 70B, and 405B scale. It closes a meaningful portion of the capability gap between open and proprietary post-trained models, releases all training data and code, and contributes a standardized evaluation toolkit (OLMES) with contamination detection. The paper's most significant contribution is Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), which applies RL using only programmatically verified outcomes rather than a trained reward model, achieving measurable gains on math and instruction following at all scales.

## Motivation

Open post-training recipes lagged significantly behind proprietary ones in November 2024, with the most critical components (training data and recipes) being precisely the least transparent parts of frontier development. As a direct measure of this opacity: no model in the top 50 of LMSYS ChatBotArena had released its post-training data. Prior open models like Tulu 2 and Zephyr-β underperformed on core capability benchmarks including MATH, IFEval, and GSM8K, and no open recipe incorporated RL-based post-training comparable to what proprietary labs used internally.

Compounding this, the absence of standardized, decontaminated evaluation frameworks made principled iteration difficult. Contamination of public datasets against popular benchmarks was found to be non-trivial, undermining the validity of claimed improvements across the field.

## The Four-Stage Pipeline

### 1. Data Curation

Sources include WildChat, Open Assistant, No Robots, FLAN v2, and UltraFeedback, with synthetic persona-driven data generated to fill gaps in math, code, and precise instruction following. Real-user queries from WildChat were found important for general chat capability; targeted synthetic prompts drove gains on verifiable skill domains. All data underwent decontamination against the evaluation suite using a purpose-built toolkit.

> "We find targeted prompts to be influential to improve core skills, while real-world queries, e.g., WildChat, are important for improving general chat capabilities."

### 2. Supervised Finetuning (SFT)

Data mixture was determined through iterative ablation, guided by skill-specialized upper bounds (e.g., math-only models). The goal was to pull the general model toward those bounds without degrading other skills. The process is notable for its transparency: ablation methodology and mixture ratios are released.

### 3. Preference Tuning via DPO

On-policy preference data was generated by sampling completions from Tulu 3-SFT and comparing them pairwise against other models, producing 354,192 preference pairs. Length-normalized DPO was selected over standard DPO and PPO for its balance of simplicity and empirical performance.

> "We experiment with several preference tuning algorithms and observe improved performance in using length-normalized Direct Preference Optimization."

### 4. Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR)

RLVR is the most novel stage. Rather than training a separate reward model, it applies a constant binary reward only when a completion is programmatically verified as correct against a ground-truth outcome. This eliminates reward hacking from a learned model by construction. Targets are math (GSM8K, MATH) and instruction following (IFEval). Infrastructure uses an asynchronous setup with vLLM running concurrently with gradient updates, enabling training at 70B and 405B scale.

> "We refer to this algorithm as Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR); it obtains a constant reward value if a completion is successful."

## Results

| Model | Avg | GSM8K | MATH | IFEval | HumanEval |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tulu 3 8B | 65.1 | 87.6 | 43.7 | 82.4 | 79.2 |
| Llama 3.1 8B Instruct | 62.9 | 83.4 | — | 80.6 | — |
| Tulu 3 70B | 76.2 | — | 63.0 | — | 88.0 |
| GPT-4o-mini | 69.6 | — | — | — | — |
| Claude 3.5 Haiku | 75.3 | — | — | — | — |
| Tulu 3 405B | 80.0 | 95.5 | 67.3 | — | 95.9 |
| GPT-4o | 80.5 | — | — | — | — |

RLVR contributions are measurable and consistent across scales. At 405B, it pushes MATH from 59.9 (post-DPO) to 67.3 and GSM8K from 94.2 to 95.5. Each stage provides incremental gains: SFT establishes the foundation, DPO improves chat and instruction following, RLVR delivers final gains on verifiable domains.

Safety performance (85.5 at 8B, 88.3 at 70B) is strong, though early SFT checkpoints score higher (94.4) and trade off modestly during DPO and RLVR for other capabilities.

## Capabilities Introduced

**[[themes/post_training_methods|Post-Training Methods]]**

- **RLVR** (demo maturity): post-training RL stage that rewards only programmatically verified completions, scaling to 405B without a learned reward model. Demonstrated gains on math and IFEval across all three model sizes.
- **Fully open SFT + DPO + RLVR pipeline** (demo maturity): the first complete open post-training recipe achieving parity or better with proprietary models at 70B (surpassing GPT-4o-mini, approaching Claude 3.5 Haiku) and competitive at 405B with GPT-4o and DeepSeek V3.
- **Length-normalized DPO** (demo maturity): drop-in improvement over standard DPO with no additional complexity.

**[[themes/synthetic_data_generation|Synthetic Data Generation]]**

- **Persona-driven synthetic instruction data** (demo maturity): targeted generation for math, code, and precise instruction following; released as permissively licensed datasets.

**[[themes/evaluation_and_benchmarks|Evaluation and Benchmarks]]**

- **OLMES evaluation toolkit** (broad production maturity): unified framework with separate development and held-out benchmark splits, plus automated decontamination tooling. Held-out split scores were never examined during development, enabling honest overfitting detection.
- **On-policy preference dataset pipeline** (demo maturity): SFT model generates completions, preference labels obtained via pairwise comparison, yielding 354K instances.

## Limitations and Open Questions

### Structural Constraints of RLVR

RLVR is restricted to domains with programmatically verifiable outcomes. It cannot be applied to open-ended generative tasks (creative writing, multi-turn dialogue, agentic reasoning), which constitute the majority of real-world language use. This is not a gap that engineering effort alone can close; it requires either new verifier paradigms or proxy reward signals with reliable alignment to actual quality. See [[themes/rl_for_llm_reasoning|RL for LLM Reasoning]] for related discussion of this bottleneck.

> "We select tasks with verifiable outcomes, such as mathematical problem-solving, and only provide rewards when the model's generations are verified to be correct."

The authors also document RLVR overoptimization: training on IFEval rewards causes regressions on non-targeted skills (Appendix B.4). Reward shaping for any one verifiable domain pulls the model's distribution in ways that degrade adjacent capabilities.

### Safety Evaluation Gap

Safety benchmarks appear only in the development split; there is no held-out safety evaluation. This means all safety scores are subject to potential overfitting during training. Given that safety and capability appear to trade off during DPO and RLVR, this is a meaningful blind spot in the evaluation methodology.

> "Both splits cover all identified skills, except we have no unseen safety evaluation."

### Benchmark Contamination as a Field-Wide Problem

Non-trivial contamination was found in several widely-used public post-training datasets against standard evaluation benchmarks. This affects not just Tulu 3 but improvement claims across the open post-training literature. The OLMES decontamination toolkit addresses this for future work, but prior comparisons remain suspect. See [[themes/benchmark_design|Benchmark Design]] for broader context.

### Performance Gaps vs. Frontier Models

Despite strong aggregate results, Tulu 3 8B lags Qwen 2.5 7B on MATH by approximately 26 points (43.7 vs. 69.9) and on MMLU by 8 points (68.2 vs. 76.6). These gaps likely reflect differences in pretraining data quality and quantity rather than post-training recipe, suggesting the ceiling for post-training is partly determined upstream. This remains an open question: how much of frontier model performance is recoverable through post-training on a weaker base?

### What Drives Post-Training Gains Remains Unclear

The paper concludes with an analysis of techniques that failed to reliably improve performance across settings:

> "We conclude with analysis and discussion of training methods that did not reliably improve performance."

This is an underappreciated contribution: the field lacks principled understanding of what drives post-training gains. Many promising techniques are empirically inconsistent. Tulu 3's ablations are unusually transparent about failures, but the fundamental explanatory gap remains.

### Evaluation of Closed Models is Fragile

Some closed-model scores required statistical imputation (MICE) due to formatting errors or unavailability in technical reports. Cross-model comparisons on standard benchmarks are less reliable than they appear.

## Bottlenecks Addressed and Created

**Addressed:**
- **Post-training opacity** in the open research community: Tulu 3 releases full data, code, and methodology, partially resolving the bottleneck blocking independent validation and extension of post-training techniques. This is a months-horizon bottleneck where Tulu 3 provides meaningful relief. Related to [[themes/finetuning_and_distillation|Finetuning and Distillation]].
- **Benchmark contamination**: the OLMES decontamination toolkit provides tooling to detect and prevent contamination in future work, though it cannot retroactively fix the existing literature.

**Reinforced or Created:**
- **RLVR applicability boundary**: by demonstrating RLVR's power and publishing its infrastructure, the paper makes its structural limitation more visible. Scaling RL-based improvement to general language tasks is now a more clearly defined bottleneck, with a 1-2 year resolution horizon at minimum.
- **Verifier availability**: the entire RLVR paradigm depends on reliable, scalable verifiers. For math and code, these exist. For most tasks, they do not. This dependency is now a more explicit constraint on the paradigm's reach.

---

## Breakthroughs

**First fully open competitive post-training pipeline**: Tulu 3 70B surpassing GPT-4o-mini and approaching Claude 3.5 Haiku with fully released data, code, and methodology is a notable inflection. Prior open models were not competitive at this level; this closes a gap that had been widening.

**RLVR as an open, scalable technique**: demonstrating RLVR at 8B, 70B, and 405B with full infrastructure release moves the technique from a proprietary capability to a reproducible method the community can build on. The asynchronous vLLM training setup is itself a contribution for large-scale RL training.
