# Elad Gil | Acting on Conviction, Future of AI, Biotech, Ambition, Speed, Non-Obvious Startup Advice

Elad Gil — prolific angel investor and founder — offers a practitioner's lens on the AI investment landscape, arguing that most enterprise AI adoption still lies ahead, that startups cluster irrationally around a handful of opportunities while vast white space goes unaddressed, and that the API-first abstraction of AI has fundamentally lowered the barrier to building. The conversation weaves together investment philosophy (conviction in unpopular ideas, product-market fit as the organizing principle), observations on AI capability trajectories, a pointed critique of biotech's failure to produce new large-scale companies, and non-obvious startup advice about speed and business model disruption as the two true advantages of early-stage companies.

## AI Adoption: Underhyped and Unevenly Distributed

Gil's central contention is that AI is **underhyped**, not overhyped. [[themes/ai_market_dynamics|AI Market Dynamics]] are being measured against the wrong baseline. [[entities/chatgpt|ChatGPT]]'s November 2022 launch was "the starting gun for this new era" — but enterprise planning cycles run 12–24 months, meaning that the wave of real organizational deployment has barely begun. The internal prototyping phase is winding down; the scale adoption phase is just beginning.

> "The future is already here — it's just not evenly distributed."

This creates a structural dynamic: the most visible AI activity (consumer launches, developer tools, VC-backed startups) precedes by 1–2 years the mass enterprise adoption that will define the market. Gil sees an **acceleration** coming, not a plateau.

### Capability Trajectory

Gil identifies four near-term model-space trends:

1. **Reasoning and deduction** — incremental but meaningful capability jumps with each new frontier model. The [[entities/harvey|Harvey]] legal AI case is illustrative: the product was not viable on GPT-3.5 but became viable on GPT-4. Capability steps matter discretely, not just directionally.
2. **Multimodality** — across modalities, not just text-to-image.
3. **Agentic workflows** — stronger reasoning combined with action chaining, enabling models to execute sophisticated multi-step tasks autonomously.
4. **Long context windows** — [[entities/gemini-15|Gemini 1.5]]'s extended context is flagged as a significant capability step, particularly relevant for domains like biology where document corpora are dense and long.

### The API Abstraction Breakthrough

Possibly underappreciated in public discourse: the shift from requiring ML operations infrastructure to simply calling an API is itself a structural breakthrough. This eliminates the prior requirement for specialised ML expertise and custom training pipelines, producing an explosion of new application possibilities.

> "You can do anything through an API now. You just ping an API and instead of dealing with MLOps, setup and training..."

This is classified as a **major breakthrough** in accessibility — not a capability advance in the models themselves, but a distribution and deployment advance that radically expands who can build with AI.

## Limitations and Open Questions

### Enterprise Lag

The 12–24 month enterprise planning cycle is not just a temporary friction — it is a **structural bottleneck** on AI revenue materialisation. See [[themes/ai_market_dynamics|AI Market Dynamics]]. Even organizations that are strategically convinced of AI's importance cannot move faster than their budget and procurement cycles allow.

### Adoption Velocity Constraints in Slow Industries

A sharper limitation: for industries that are slow technology adopters, *building a purpose-built AI startup to sell into that industry may be the wrong model entirely*. If adoption velocity is too low, a startup will run out of runway before the market is ready.

> "If that industry is a slow adopter of technology, actually building a company to sell into that industry may not actually be useful."

The alternative Gil proposes: buy existing assets in that industry, use AI to restructure the cost basis, expand footprint aggressively, and capture market share as an operator — not as a vendor. This is a significant reframing of the standard VC-backed vertical AI playbook.

### Ecosystem Clustering and White Space

One of Gil's most pointed observations concerns [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC and the Startup Ecosystem]]: AI startups cluster irrationally. Seven companies attack the same identical opportunity while adjacent white space — often larger opportunities — goes unaddressed.

> "There's just white space and so many opportunities that no one is working on."

This is partly a function of how VC incentives work (follow the consensus, de-risk the fund) and partly a failure of imagination. Gil views it as an opportunity: the unpopular, uncrowded bet is where the real returns are.

## Investment Philosophy: Conviction in Unpopular Ideas

Gil frames effective investing around the capacity to act on conviction when the thesis is broadly contested. Each of his cited examples required a *different type* of conviction:

- **Anduril** — conviction in the *importance of a market* (defence tech), formed after Google cancelled Project Maven due to internal employee backlash. If big tech was abandoning defence, startups had an opening.
- **Stripe** (later-stage) — conviction in a *company and its momentum*, not just the category.
- **Early generative AI** — conviction in a *technology wave*, before broad consensus had formed. Gil led the first round in [[entities/perplexity|Perplexity]] before generative AI investment was fashionable.

The common thread is a process of *questioning assumptions others take for granted* and revisiting what is always important but currently forgotten. In Anduril's case: defence has always been important. The assumption being questioned was whether tech could and should serve it.

> "The best companies are always non-obvious at the time — because if they're obvious, everybody would be doing them."

### Listening and Communicating as Competitive Advantages

Gil invokes Steve Jobs as an exemplar of a distinct cognitive skill: **listening more clearly than others and communicating more crisply and concisely**. This same skill marks the strongest founders — they have re-checked reality more carefully than everyone else, and can articulate what's important and why with unusual clarity.

## Product-Market Fit as the Organizing Principle

See [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|Startup Formation and GTM]]. Gil's framework for what causes companies to win reduces to product-market fit — but he unpacks it carefully:

- Is this the *right product* for this *specific market*?
- Is there *natural momentum* — does the market want to move?
- Is there a clear *why now* — something that makes this possible today that wasn't possible before?

> "Amazing teams get crushed by terrible markets, and pretty bad teams do very well in extremely good ones."

The implication for investors: market selection dominates team quality in determining outcomes, though the combination of exceptional team and exceptional market produces the category-defining companies (Meta, Google, Stripe, Databricks).

### Early-Stage Startup Advantages

Against incumbents, Gil argues there are exactly two structural advantages available to an early-stage startup:

1. **Speed** — the ability to move faster than a large organisation can respond.
2. **Business model disruption** — the ability to change the economic model of a category in ways an incumbent cannot without cannibalising itself.

Everything else (capital, distribution, brand, talent pipelines) favours the incumbent.

## Biotech: A Structural Puzzle

An extended section addresses why biotech has failed to produce new large-scale biopharma companies since the 1980s wave. The data point: the most recent high-market-cap biotechs — Moderna (~$40B, attributable substantially to COVID), Vertex (~$100B), Regeneron (~$100B) — were all founded 30–40 years ago.

> "It's been 30 to 40 years since the last wave of very big biotech companies were started."

Gil's framing is diagnostic rather than conclusive. The possible explanations include industry structure, regulatory dynamics, and founder models — but he treats this as an open question, not a resolved one. The contrast with technology (where new multi-hundred-billion-dollar companies emerge on decade timescales) is striking.

This is relevant to [[themes/frontier_lab_competition|Frontier Lab Competition]] and adjacent AI-bio investment theses: if long context windows are important for biology models (as Gil suggests), and if biotech's structural constraints are addressable via AI, the investment opportunity could be significant — but the adoption velocity constraint applies here too.
