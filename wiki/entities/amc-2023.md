# AMC 2023

> The American Mathematics Competition 2023 (AMC 2023) is a standardized math problem set widely used as a reasoning benchmark for evaluating large language models. Its structured, verifiable problems make it a natural testbed for comparing inference strategies, training paradigms, and compute scaling approaches in mathematical reasoning research.

## Overview

AMC 2023 consists of problems from the American Mathematics Competition 2023, serving as one of several challenging math reasoning benchmarks used to evaluate LLM reasoning approaches. Within recent literature it appears primarily as part of evaluation suites for methods that question or refine the dominant paradigm of extended chain-of-thought reasoning — alongside harder benchmarks like AIME 2025 and ARC-AGI. Its role is to probe whether accuracy gains from explicit thinking processes hold across a range of mathematical difficulty levels and token budget constraints.

## Significance as a Benchmark

AMC 2023 occupies a useful middle ground in reasoning evaluations: harder than trivial arithmetic but more tractable than olympiad-level problems. This makes it sensitive to differences between inference strategies that might converge on harder tasks. Several distinct research threads have used it to stress-test assumptions about how reasoning models should operate.

The "Reasoning Models Can Be Effective Without Thinking" paper is the most direct user of AMC 2023 as a benchmark in this cluster. Its central finding — that **NoThinking**, a prompting method that bypasses explicit chain-of-thought by prefilling a dummy thinking block, outperforms standard Thinking-mode models when controlling for token count — was validated across seven datasets including AMC 2023. This challenges the assumption that the long search-and-backtracking process reasoning models generate is uniformly beneficial. The advantage of NoThinking is especially pronounced at low token budgets and grows as the number of parallel samples increases (pass@k with k > 1), suggesting that the token savings from skipping explicit reasoning are better spent on diversity through parallel sampling rather than depth through sequential thinking.

## Training Paradigms Evaluated Against It

AMC 2023 also appears in the evaluation of RLAD, a two-player reinforcement learning framework that jointly trains an abstraction generator and a solution generator. The abstraction generator is rewarded when the abstractions it produces improve the solution generator's accuracy — creating a cooperative RL loop warmstarted via SFT on synthetic problem-abstraction pairs generated by o4-mini. While RLAD's headline results are on AIME 2025 (44% improvement over DAPO), AMC 2023 provides a complementary signal across difficulty levels. The abstraction mechanism also showed benefits on ARC-AGI in pass@k and coverage metrics, reinforcing that structured intermediate representations generalize beyond pure math.

The "Beyond the 80/20 Rule" paper contributes another training-side finding: using only the top 20% highest-entropy (forking) tokens in the RLVR policy gradient loss achieves performance comparable to full-gradient updates on Qwen3-8B. This implies that most tokens in a reasoning trace carry little gradient signal — a structurally important result for efficient RL training of reasoning models.

ParaThinker introduces a parallel reasoning paradigm, training models via SFT on 6.2K problem-solution pairs with up to six distinct reasoning paths per problem. The 12.3% accuracy improvement for 1.5B models with 8 parallel paths (and 7.5% for 7B models) suggests that native parallelism in the thinking process — rather than sequential depth — is a productive axis for test-time compute scaling.

## Open Questions and Limitations

A recurring tension across these works is unresolved: **when does explicit chain-of-thought help, and when is it wasteful?** NoThinking's strong token-efficiency results hold on AMC 2023 and similar benchmarks, but the same paper notes that for formal theorem proving with perfect verifiers, NoThinking with parallel scaling achieves 7x lower latency and 4x fewer tokens than Thinking — suggesting the verifier structure, not just the problem difficulty, mediates the tradeoff.

It remains unclear how AMC 2023's difficulty tier interacts with these findings. Results on harder benchmarks like AIME 2025 do not always transfer cleanly to AMC-level problems, and the reverse may also be true. The benchmark's accessibility also means it risks ceiling effects as models improve, limiting its future discriminative power for frontier systems.

The cluster of findings here also raises a deeper open question: whether reinforcement learning on verifiable math problems — the paradigm shared by RLAD, DAPO, and RLVR approaches — produces reasoning capabilities that genuinely generalize, or whether benchmark-specific patterns are being reinforced. AMC 2023, as a fixed and well-known problem set, is not immune to this concern.
