# No Priors Podcast

No Priors is a podcast co-hosted by Sarah Guo and Elad Gil that covers AI and technology investing. It serves as a recurring public forum where two of the most active early-stage AI investors share their evolving read on the market — making it a useful artifact for tracking how sophisticated investor thinking about AI has developed from 2022 onward.

## Overview

No Priors is co-hosted by Sarah Guo (founder of Conviction) and Elad Gil, a prolific angel investor and author. The podcast sits at the intersection of AI capability tracking and investment thesis formation — episodes typically blend technical observations about model progress with structural thinking about how AI reshapes markets and startup formation.

## Key Findings

### The Scaling Inflection Narrative

A recurring thread across No Priors episodes is a first-hand account of how investor conviction in AI formed through a series of discrete capability step-functions. GPT-3 was the first major signal — described as "such a big step up from GPT-2" that it began attracting serious investor attention. GPT-3.5 was then characterised as an even larger step-function, directly triggering a wave of new AI company funding and researcher outreach. ChatGPT's public launch in November 2022 — just one month after Conviction's founding in October 2022 — was the moment that validated the thesis at consumer scale, though notably even close observers did not anticipate the speed or intensity of the consumer reaction.

This narrative matters because it illustrates how [[themes/scaling_laws|scaling laws]] translated into investment behaviour: each capability jump was legible enough to informed observers to shift capital allocation, but not so legible that broader market timing was obvious in advance.

### Platform Displacement and the SaaS Disruption Thesis

Episodes draw heavily on historical platform analogies to frame AI's impact on software markets. The observation that Microsoft Office applications — Excel, PowerPoint, Word — were originally independent third-party companies (e.g., Lotus) before being subsumed into Microsoft's platform is used as a lens for thinking about [[themes/vertical_ai_and_saas_disruption|vertical AI and SaaS disruption]]. The implication is that current AI-native point solutions may face a similar fate: viable as standalone businesses until a platform layer consolidates them.

Similarly, Instagram's acquisition at just 12 employees is cited as a reference point for the small-team leverage that AI-native companies can achieve — relevant to how [[themes/startup_formation_and_gtm|startup formation and GTM]] logic changes when AI handles work previously requiring headcount.

### White Space and Clustering

A striking structural observation from Elad Gil: AI startups are anomalously clustered around a small number of opportunities, leaving large amounts of white space unaddressed — even as most AI adoption is judged to still be ahead of us, making AI underhyped rather than overhyped by conventional metrics. This is an implicit critique of herd dynamics in [[themes/vc_and_startup_ecosystem|VC and the startup ecosystem]], and a signal that the podcast hosts are actively tracking where founder and investor attention is *not* going.

### Frontier Model Tracking

Episodes also function as a loose public log of frontier model developments. The launch of Google Gemini 1.5 with a 1 million token context window and Apple's release of relatively small models on Hugging Face are both noted as significant market events, situating the podcast as a real-time observer of [[themes/frontier_lab_competition|frontier lab competition]].

## Open Questions and Limitations

The podcast format introduces selection bias: the perspectives are those of investors with strong incentives to maintain a bullish AI narrative. The claim that "most AI adoption is ahead of us" is a thesis, not a measurement. The historical analogies (Microsoft Office, Instagram) are illustrative but not structurally predictive — the degree to which AI consolidation will mirror prior platform cycles remains genuinely contested.

Episodes also lack the technical granularity needed to evaluate capability claims at depth. Observations about model step-functions are phenomenological rather than mechanistic, which limits their use as evidence for specific claims about [[themes/pretraining_and_scaling|pretraining and scaling]] trajectories.
