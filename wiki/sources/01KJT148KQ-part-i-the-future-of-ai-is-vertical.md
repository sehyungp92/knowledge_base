# Part I: The future of AI is vertical

## Briefing

**Vertical AI — LLM-native applications purpose-built for specific industries — represents a structural leap beyond legacy vertical SaaS by unlocking language-intensive markets (legal, healthcare, finance) that software alone could never economically serve. Bessemer argues this is not an incremental improvement but a category redefinition: because AI attacks the services economy (13% of US GDP) rather than just software workflows (1% of GDP), Vertical AI's eventual market cap will be at least 10x that of legacy vertical SaaS. The window for building is now, with early portfolio data already showing ~400% YoY growth and healthy unit economics.**

### Key Takeaways
1. **Vertical AI targets the services economy, not just the software economy** — Software spend is ~1% of US GDP; Business and Professional Services dominated by repetitive language tasks is ~13%, making the addressable opportunity for AI fundamentally larger than prior SaaS waves.
2. **Early LLM-native companies already rival SaaS contract values** — LLM-native companies (founded 2019–present) are achieving 80% of the average contract value of traditional vertical SaaS while growing ~400% YoY and sustaining ~65% gross margins.
3. **The 10x market cap thesis** — Bessemer predicts Vertical AI market cap will be at least 10x legacy vertical SaaS as AI absorbs tasks previously performed by human labor in the services sector, not just software-replaceable workflows.
4. **TAM expansion is the core unlock** — AI increases the scope of value delivered enough to make previously-too-small markets viable, meaning companies don't need to target the largest existing TAMs; they can create new ones.
5. **Supporting workflows may be better early beachheads than core workflows** — Because supporting work is ancillary to a professional's identity (e.g., a doctor's paperwork vs. patient care), AI adoption faces less resistance and can be delegated more readily.
6. **Incumbents are actively seeking AI tools** — In healthcare and legal — historically the slowest-adopting industries — large players are now proactively adopting Vertical AI out of competitive fear, compressing adoption timelines Bessemer expected from the SaaS analogy.
7. **M&A is already validating the category** — Thomson Reuters acquired CaseText for $650M (2023) and DocuSign acquired Lexion for $165M (2024), signaling that incumbents are buying rather than waiting to build.
8. **Vertical AI is mostly complementary to legacy SaaS, not displacement** — The majority of Vertical AI value is additive to existing software stacks, reducing competitive friction and accelerating adoption cycles vs. prior platform transitions.
9. **The "wrapper" criticism is real but overapplied** — Some AI apps are shallow API wrappers, but robust moats exist around proprietary data, product depth, and economic value delivered; defensibility is nuanced and evolving rapidly.
10. **Five $100M+ ARR companies and the first IPO within 3 years** — Bessemer's projection based on growth rates of more mature startups in the portfolio, suggesting the category is approaching escape velocity.
11. **Horizontal SaaS timing analogy breaks down for AI** — Salesforce IPO'd a full decade before Veeva, but Bessemer explicitly warns that SaaS frameworks and timelines do not map cleanly onto AI development cycles.
12. **Automation propensity does not equal good use case** — Customer willingness to automate a given workflow is independent of technical feasibility; investment bankers will use AI for slide decks but not AI-delivered client presentations, because relationships are the product.

---

### Why Vertical AI Is a Category-Level Shift, Not an Incremental Upgrade

- Legacy vertical SaaS transformed industries by digitizing processes, but left large portions of the economy behind because **software ROI alone was insufficient to overcome implementation costs, process change, and technophobic buyers**.
  - Industries like construction and hospitality came online, but high-touch, language-heavy sectors — legal, healthcare, finance — remained largely software-resistant.
  - The upfront infrastructure, training, and process costs made the math on software adoption not work for many SMB-heavy verticals.
- **Vertical AI changes the equation by automating the actual work, not just organizing it**, making ROI immediate and obvious rather than deferred to operational efficiency gains.
  - AI solutions that take over tasks entirely (e.g., clinical note generation, demand letter drafting) deliver value without requiring customers to change their workflows significantly.
  - This is why historically slow-moving industries are now actively seeking AI tools — the value proposition is qualitatively different from SaaS.
- The Horizontal → Vertical SaaS analogy is instructive but imperfect: Salesforce (horizontal) preceded Veeva (vertical) by a decade, but **the AI transition may be faster and more simultaneous** across horizontal and vertical layers.
  - Bessemer explicitly flags that their SaaS mental models and timelines should not be assumed to hold for AI.

---

### The Economics: TAM Expansion as the Core Opportunity

- **Software spend represents ~1% of US GDP; Business and Professional Services (dominated by repetitive language tasks) represents ~13%.** This statistic anchors the 10x market cap prediction.
  - The implication: Vertical AI is not competing for the software budget — it is competing for the services labor budget, which is an order of magnitude larger.
- Vertical AI companies can build sustainable businesses in niches that would have been too small for legacy SaaS because **AI dramatically increases the scope of value delivered per customer**.
  - EvenUp (demand letter automation for personal injury attorneys) has exceeded the traditional SaaS TAM for workflow management by allowin

## Key Claims

1. The top 20 public vertical SaaS companies in the US have a combined market capitalization of approximately $300 billion.
2. Vertical AI applications can target high-cost repetitive language-based tasks in legal, healthcare, and finance — markets largely out of bounds for legacy vertical software.
3. LLM-native companies founded since 2019 have achieved 80% of the average contract value of traditional vertical SaaS systems.
4. LLM-native vertical AI companies are growing approximately 400% year-over-year.
5. LLM-native vertical AI companies maintain approximately 65% gross margins.
6. At least five Vertical AI companies will reach $100M+ ARR within the next two to three years.
7. The first Vertical AI IPO is anticipated within the next three years.
8. Thomson Reuters acquired CaseText for $650M in 2023, signaling market acceptance of vertical AI.
9. DocuSign acquired Lexion for $165M in 2024.
10. Vertical AI's market capitalization will be at least 10x the size of legacy Vertical SaaS as it expands into the services economy.

## Capabilities

- AI can transcribe and structure patient-doctor conversations into clinical notes in real-time production deployments, reducing physician administrative burden
- AI can automatically review hundreds of hours of video footage to surface legally relevant content for public defenders during criminal discovery
- AI can analyze multi-source industrial data (IoT telemetry, field failures, production logs, supplier data) at scale to help manufacturers identify product quality issues
- AI can automate complex audit and compliance workflows (SOC 2, financial audits, PCI DSS, internal audits) with measurable auditor productivity gains
- LLM-native vertical AI startups are achieving ~80% of legacy SaaS average contract values at ~400% YoY growth and ~65% gross margins — commercial velocity not seen in prior software waves
- Law firms that historically resisted even basic CRM adoption are now deploying AI co-pilot tools for contracting, demand summary generation, and case intake
- Multimodal AI is extending vertical AI applications beyond text workflows to voice, video, and image processing in domain-specific production contexts

## Limitations

- AI automation is strongly concentrated in text- and language-heavy industries; manual-labor-intensive verticals (construction, home services, manufacturing) remain largely out of reach for meaningful workflow automation
- Relationship-intensive client-facing professional work resists AI adoption even when the underlying task is technically automatable — social norms and client expectations block deployment
- Many vertical AI applications are 'wrapper' products built on third-party model APIs with insufficient proprietary value, making them easily replicable by competitors or rendered obsolete by model provider feature additions
- Vertical AI defensibility through specialization comes at the cost of addressable market size — deeper vertical focus creates stronger moats but smaller TAMs, requiring multi-product 'layer cake' expansion to build large businesses
- Healthcare AI adoption faces structurally long enterprise deal cycles that persist even when ROI is demonstrably clear — the sector's compliance exposure and multi-stakeholder procurement impose delays
- Horizontal AI incumbents (Intercom, Zapier, Canva) are rapidly incorporating AI into existing platforms, threatening to neutralize vertical AI differentiation in supporting workflows
- Technophobic decision-makers and high upfront implementation costs continue to block AI adoption in many legacy industries even when economic ROI is substantial
- Vertical AI is primarily capturing back-office and supporting workflows; core revenue-generating professional functions (financial modeling, client advising) face higher adoption resistance and quality bar requirements
- The defensibility landscape for vertical AI applications is fundamentally unsettled — there are no established frameworks for what constitutes a durable moat in AI-native application businesses

## Bottlenecks

- Relationship-intensive professional workflows (client-facing presentations, high-trust advising) are structurally resistant to AI automation due to social and professional norms, blocking full-stack automation of the highest-value professional services revenue generation
- Vertical AI specialization tradeoff: deeper vertical focus produces stronger defensibility but constrains TAM, requiring multi-product 'layer cake' expansion to build large businesses — increasing execution risk and capital requirements for vertical AI founders
- Wrapper risk and absent proprietary data moats: early vertical AI companies cannot yet demonstrate sustainable differentiation beyond UX and prompt engineering, making them vulnerable to undercutting by foundation model providers or horizontal incumbents

## Breakthroughs

- LLM-native vertical AI has demonstrated that language-based service economy workflows — historically immune to software automation — are now commercially addressable at scale, potentially expanding the total software TAM by 10x
