# Figma

Figma is a browser-based collaborative design platform that has become one of the most-cited case studies in enterprise software strategy discussions, particularly as a model for understanding how AI tools might propagate through organizations. Its significance in AI business discourse lies less in the product itself and more in what it reveals about go-to-market dynamics: Figma demonstrated that powerful software can capture an enterprise by winning individual contributors first, then expanding outward — a pattern investors and strategists now invoke when reasoning about how AI-native tools will displace incumbents.

## Overview

Figma's defining strategic insight — as articulated in Miles Grimshaw: The 5 Pillars of Venture Capital & Why Co-Pilot is an Incumbent Strategy — is that it was never merely design software. The product functions as a **collaboration nexus for digital products**, with design as the core stakeholder but with copywriters, marketers, product managers, and engineers all orbiting the design artefact. Grimshaw notes this reframing came to him only in retrospect: what he had initially categorised as a design tool should have been understood as the gravitational centre of digital product collaboration.

This distinction matters enormously for how the company was valued, how it spread, and why it became defensible. Traditional enterprise software sells top-down — procurement, IT, legal — but Figma entered through individual designers and then became too deeply embedded in cross-functional workflows to displace. The product earned adoption before it earned contracts.

## As a GTM Case Study for AI

The Figma playbook is invoked repeatedly in AI investment discussions as a template for how AI-native vertical tools might win:

- **Bottom-up IC adoption**: A single designer installs Figma; within months, the entire product org is living inside it. The same dynamic is hypothesised for AI coding assistants, AI design tools, and agent-based workflows — individual practitioners adopt first, and enterprise contracts follow.
- **Collaboration network effects**: Once multiple roles depend on the same artefact, switching cost becomes organisational rather than individual. AI tools that position themselves as collaboration surfaces — rather than solo productivity tools — inherit this structural advantage.
- **Horizontal reach from a vertical entry point**: Figma entered through designers but became essential to PMs, engineers, and marketers. This "vertical entry, horizontal expansion" model is now a reference pattern for AI startup GTM strategy, particularly in the context of whether AI tools will displace SaaS incumbents or replicate their adoption curves.

## Limitations and Open Questions

The Figma analogy, while instructive, has important limits that the sources do not fully resolve:

- **The nexus role may not generalise**: Figma's expansion was enabled by the fact that design artefacts are inherently shared objects — everyone needs to comment on, reference, or hand off designs. It is unclear which AI tools have analogous structural properties versus which are fundamentally solo-use productivity aids.
- **Timing and category maturity**: Figma displaced Adobe in a market where the incumbent had weak network effects and a desktop-first product. Many AI incumbent targets (e.g., enterprise CRM, ERP) have far stickier integrations and procurement relationships. The substitution dynamics may be slower and more contested.
- **The missing counter-evidence**: The sources that invoke Figma do so approvingly, as validation of a thesis. No source in this corpus seriously examines cases where the bottom-up IC playbook failed — which makes it difficult to identify the conditions under which it applies versus when top-down enterprise sales remains necessary.
