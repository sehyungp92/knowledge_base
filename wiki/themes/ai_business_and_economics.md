## Current State

The landscape data for this theme is minimal — one limitation, no capabilities, bottlenecks, or breakthroughs on record — but that sparsity is itself a signal worth reading carefully.

The theme appears to have entered the knowledge base at a moment of market ambiguity: likely mid-transition, when AI-native application companies (the so-called "zero-layer" or vertical AI stacks) had demonstrated early traction but had not yet forced a reckoning with legacy SaaS pricing power or retention moats. The single extracted limitation — whether AI will displace SaaS incumbents or merely complement them — is not a gap in the literature but a gap in the *outcome*. Even the most optimistic VC framing hedges the competitive result. This structural uncertainty defined 2024–2025 and remains unresolved in early 2026.

No capabilities have been formally extracted, which likely reflects the nature of the theme more than an ingestion failure. Business and economic dynamics manifest as emergent market outcomes rather than discrete technical capabilities, making them harder to score against a maturity scale. What is likely true given the broader trajectory: AI cost curves have continued falling sharply — inference economics shifted dramatically in 2024–2025 with open-weight model proliferation — enterprise willingness-to-pay has been tested and partially validated, and the "picks and shovels" layer (infrastructure, APIs, tooling) has proven more durable than application-layer bets in the near term.

Two cross-theme signals amplify this picture in important directions. First, the barrier to building personal AI infrastructure has compressed to hours of work — a functional WhatsApp-to-Claude relay was reportedly built in one hour. This implies vertical AI products may increasingly be assembled by individual developers rather than funded teams, disrupting traditional SaaS formation economics from the supply side. Second, the safety requirements for autonomous agents — spending caps, multi-factor authentication, human-in-the-loop oversight, incremental trust gating — become significant operational cost factors. Safety is not merely a technical constraint here; it is a business model constraint that shapes unit economics and deployment feasibility.

Momentum in this theme will be visible in sources not yet ingested. Watch for: resolution signals on the SaaS displacement question (churn data, pricing pressure in specific verticals), emergence of AI-native revenue models that diverge from seat-based SaaS, and any evidence that AI agents are beginning to act as economic agents — buying, contracting, or substituting for human procurement decisions. These would shift the theme from "AI changes how software is sold" to "AI changes who the buyer is."

## Contradictions

*No formal contradictions extracted. ## Research Opportunities

- **SaaS displacement evidence base** — Systematic collection of churn data, pricing pressure signals, and net revenue retention trends in AI-adjacent SaaS verticals would directly address the primary open limitation.
- **AI-native revenue model taxonomy** — A structured comparison of emerging pricing models (outcome-based, consumption-based, agent-seat hybrids) against the seat-based SaaS baseline would clarify where genuine model divergence is occurring vs. rebranding.
- **Safety costs as business model variable** — Empirical work on how much autonomous agent deployment costs increase under different safety regimes (MFA, spending caps, human review) and how this varies by use case and organization size.
- **Agent-as-buyer dynamics** — Early-stage monitoring of cases where AI systems participate in procurement or contracting decisions, which would mark a qualitative shift in the competitive landscape.
