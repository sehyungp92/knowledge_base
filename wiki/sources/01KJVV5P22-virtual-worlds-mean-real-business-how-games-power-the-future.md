# Virtual Worlds Mean Real Business: How Games Power the Future

**Authors:** 
**Published:** 2024-12-25 00:00:00
**Type:** video

## Analysis

- Games are essentially virtual simulations\. And for the last couple of decades, those virtual simulations have been designed for fun\. But they will increasingly be used in the real world for all kinds of use cases, whether it's learning and development or training grounds for robotics and other autonomous systems or visualisation to allow people to see things in real time 3D\. 
	- 1990s was about text on the internet, 2000s was about images, 2010s was about video, and 2020s is going to be about interactive 3D and gaming technology being used in the enterprise\. 
- Potential uses cases of real time 3D include: 
	- Visualisation for architects to be able to walk through their design before it's constructed, and see if there are errors or other imperfections that they wish they had known when they were designing\.
	- For automotive manufacturers, they use real time 3D for the design, but also virtual test driving\. And now the heads up display in Rivian is powered by Unreal, BMW’s is powered by Unity\. 
	- There's also virtual training, whether it's for heavy machinery operations or other tasks\. 
- But some of the bottlenecks for these use cases are the same constraints that the game developers have faced, which are starting to get alleviated\.
- On the content creation side, within a game studio, more than half of the spend goes towards creation of the assets and the art and the content that goes into these virtual simulations\. And the same is true for these non\-gaming use cases, except they don't have 3D artists on staff to build those\. 
	- Now with AI for asset generation, whether it's images, audio or now 3D assets, that issue is significantly alleviated\. 
- The second bottleneck is for 3D capture techniques\. For a lot of these non\-gaming use cases, they want to capture the physical world as it's seen\. There been technologies in the past that have allowed this, such as photogrammetry or Matterport, which is just a 360 degree image which does not allow you to interact with the environment the same way you can with a video game\. 
	- Now with newer technologies like NeRF, other Radiance Fields technologies like Gaussian Splatting or transformer based approaches which allow consumers to capture photorealistic 3D in a much more efficient manner\. 
- Also for some of these non\-gaming use cases, this is where there may be a prevalence of XR and being able to put on a headset, e\.g\., see how the BIM model overlays on the construction site or for medical surgery simulation\. 
	- As we get better, lighter headsets with eye tracking and other capabilities, that may provide the necessary unlock for some of these use cases
- In terms of the economics there, for some of the use cases, especially the non\-gaming, photorealism is everything, which is the focus of 3D engines like Unreal that has unlocked various use cases\. But for other use cases it is less relevant, and just have to be up to par with users’ expectations for their use cases, which turns means costs could drop dramatically\. 
- Perhaps more importantly, particularly for virtual training use cases, if you wanted to train the workforce on maintenance and repairs for a robot, you would build this experience and develop this virtual simulation\. But then, if the team wanted to update it or add content to it, previously they would have to go back to the outsourced agency who built them the original digital twin\. 
	- Now they will be empowered to do that themselves internally\. So it's not just how the economics of creating one thing is changing, but also how it's integrated into the entire system\. 
- More specifically about applications, autonomy is deeply rooted with these virtual simulations\. 
	- Interestingly Anduril’s first acquisition was a game studio as they were interested in acquiring it for the game engine that the studio had developed, which they used for strategy simulation and other workflows\. 
	- For Applied Intuition, the scale of training data that you need to capture this in the real world was impractical\. And so when you have these virtual simulations, not only can you scale the amount of data, but also take into account the fringe and edge cases that you would never be able to experience or capture in the real world, whether it's extreme weather or other 1 in 1,000 situations\. 
- While in the past, we had the ability to use these virtual simulations for mostly either physics simulations or hard skills, now with AI NPCs in the gaming context, rather than just being scripted, these agents can take on a life of their own, observe the environment, can reason and plan, and then act\. 
	- When you have a multi\-agent simulation, e\.g\., next pandemic response or immigration policies, we may be able to test these in a virtual environment with these agents who can interact with each other\.
- Amongst many, one consumer application is the ability to plan out the space, e\.g\., a new apartment, where you can drag and drop furniture and see how it fits in a 3D environment\. All the technology exists today to have that experience in an intuitive way, but a product does not exist\. 
	- We should be able to scan our space and develop a digital twin of the 3D environment, show design inspiration from Pinterest, have it find the pieces of furniture, the artwork, that closest matches my inspiration, fill the scene, and then either be able to walk through it in a virtual world or use augmented reality and see how it fits into the space\. 
- There is also some really interesting research being done in the Human\-Machine Interaction \(HMI\) space\. There will be a wide range of use cases, but as with most emerging tech, the initial use cases are likely going to be in gaming that function as the wedge for these companies to use consumer spend to fund their R&D, similar to Nvidia\. 
	- Apple Vision Pro made huge progress this year with eye tracking\. And in addition, we are going to see brain\-computer interface \(BCI\) type

## Key Claims

1. The 2020s will be defined by interactive 3D and gaming technology being used in the enterprise.
2. The 1990s internet era was defined by text, the 2000s by images, and the 2010s by video.
3. Games are virtual simulations originally designed for entertainment but increasingly applicable to real-world enterprise use cases.
4. Virtual simulations are being used as training grounds for robotics and other autonomous systems.
5. NVIDIA originated primarily as a gaming company, with early revenue driven by gaming graphics cards.
6. GPUs were originally designed for computationally intense matrix multiplications used in rendering images and animations.
7. GPU-based accelerated computing is now used across domains including digital biology and cryptocurrency mining.
8. NVIDIA's earliest branding included the tagline 'the future is 3D', predating widespread real-time 3D adoption by 25 years.
9. The gaming industry acts as a crucible for technological innovation because consumer spending funds R&D.
10. Multiplayer gaming technology was a foundational innovation that later enabled collaborative software products like Figma.

## Capabilities

- AI systems can generate 3D assets (models, textures) to supplement or replace manual creation by specialized artists
- Photorealistic 3D capture of physical spaces using Gaussian Splatting technology that is efficient, immediate, and accessible to non-experts
- AI-controlled NPCs that can observe their environment, reason about situations, plan actions, and act autonomously rather than executing pre-scripted behaviors
- Virtual simulations used to train autonomous systems (vehicles, robots) at scale, generating edge cases and rare scenarios that would be impractical to capture in the real world
- Real-time 3D visualization systems for automotive and architectural design, enabling virtual test driving, design review, and error detection before physical construction

## Limitations

- Creating high-quality 3D assets for virtual simulations requires specialized 3D artists and represents over 50% of development costs, preventing adoption by enterprises without in-house 3D teams
- Photorealism requirements significantly increase asset creation costs; applications that can tolerate lower visual fidelity have dramatically lower cost barriers
- Current XR headsets still require significant improvement in weight, form factor, eye tracking quality, and overall ergonomics; technology trajectory suggests ongoing development needed
- Gap between technical feasibility and consumer product development: technologies exist to build AI-powered interior design applications, but no consumer-facing products have been deployed
- Brain-Computer Interface technology for controlling virtual environments through brain signals is not yet mature enough for consumer or enterprise deployment
- Full-body haptic feedback technology exists only in limited forms (ring-based); widespread integration into XR experiences not yet achieved despite being recognized as valuable for immersion

## Bottlenecks

- Over 50% of game development costs go to content creation; non-gaming enterprises lack in-house 3D teams, blocking adoption of virtual simulation technologies for training, visualization, and design
- Consumer-facing 3D spatial design and visualization applications do not exist despite all required technical components being available
- XR hardware still requires significant development in weight reduction, eye tracking accuracy, and overall ergonomics to enable mass-market immersive applications
- Brain-Computer Interface technology for virtual environment control is still in research/early demo phase and not ready for commercial deployment

## Breakthroughs

- Gaussian Splatting technology enables photorealistic 3D capture of physical spaces that is efficient, immediate, and accessible to non-specialist users
- AI systems can now generate 3D assets, reducing the need for manual creation by specialized artists
- AI-controlled NPCs can observe environments, reason about situations, plan actions, and act autonomously, replacing hard-coded scripted behavior
- Autonomous systems can be trained using virtual simulations to generate edge cases and rare scenarios at previously impossible scale
