# Thinking vs. Doing: Agents that Reason by Scaling Test-Time Interaction

### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- The dominant paradigm of test-time scaling focuses on generating longer reasoning traces ("thinking more") per step, but this internal computation cannot acquire new information from the environment or adapt behavior in response to external feedback.
  - Chain-of-thought and budget forcing simply reorganize information the agent already holds; they cannot resolve partial observability or non-stationarity (e.g., fluctuating prices, pages with incomplete visible content).
  - In controlled experiments on WebArena, forcing the agent to think longer (budget forcing) raised success rate by less than 3%, while interaction scaling achieved a 5%+ gain under equivalent token budgets.
- Post-training methods (SFT on expert demonstrations, standard RL with task rewards) produce reactive agents that predict a single best action per step and struggle with evolving or uncertain task dynamics.
  - These methods optimize for the locally best action given the agent's internal state, which is insufficient when information-seeking behavior — only achievable through additional environment interaction — is required to solve the task.
- Existing curriculum learning approaches for web agents (AutoWebGLM, WebRL) define curricula by estimated task difficulty from LLM-generated instruction complexity, requiring explicit difficulty labeling and not addressing the interaction-horizon dimension directly.

---

### Proposed Approach
- The paper proposes **interaction scaling** as a new, orthogonal axis of test-time scaling: increasing the number of environment interaction steps (the rollout horizon ℎ) rather than the depth of reasoning within each step, enabling behaviors like exploration, backtracking, and dynamic re-planning within a single rollout.
  - Unlike per-step compute scaling (CoT, best-of-n, budget forcing), each additional interaction step provides genuinely new observations from the environment, enriching the agent's context with information that internal reasoning cannot generate.
- The core trained method, **TTI (Test-Time Interaction)**, is a curriculum-based online RL approach using filtered behavior cloning (online STaR): only successful trajectories are retained, and the agent maximizes log-likelihood of actions conditioned on those high-reward rollouts.
  - The curriculum schedules the maximum allowed interaction horizon ℎ across training iterations, starting short and expanding according to either an additive schedule (ℎᵢ = clip(ℎ_min + i, ℎ_max)) or a multiplicative schedule (ℎᵢ = clip(ℎ_min · i, ℎ_max)); the multiplicative schedule is adopted as default after ablation shows it outperforms additive by ~2.75% on WebArena.
  - The motivation for the curriculum is that training at a fixed long horizon causes slow, noisy learning due to poor credit assignment and agents associating random exploratory actions with rewards; training at a fixed short horizon causes premature termination and prevents exploratory behavior from emerging.
- At inference time, interaction scaling can also be applied via prompting alone — a "check-again" mechanism that prompts the agent to reconsider after issuing a stop action — though this is shown to be insufficient without training because it cannot instill nuanced mid-rollout exploration/exploitation switching.
- TTI is scaled to large synthetic training sets (128K tasks for WebVoyager, 11K for WebArena) generated by an exploratory agent browsing real websites, with a Gemma 3 27B model used as a prompting-based verifier (88.9% accuracy vs. WebArena ground truth) to label rollout success.

### Results & Capabilities
- TTI trained on Gemma 3 12B achieves **64.8% task success rate on WebVoyager**, setting a new state-of-the-art among open-weight agents trained exclusively on open synthetic data — a 30% relative improvement over the best prior open-model/open-data approach (PAE-LLaVa-34B at 33.0%), and a 9% absolute improvement over the zero-shot Gemma 3 12B baseline (55.8%).
  - TTI achieves the highest success rate in 8 of 13 WebVoyager domains; notable gains include Allrecipes (+31.4% over zero-shot) and Cambridge Dictionary (+15.6%), both information-dense domains benefiting most from extended exploration.
- On full **WebArena** (812 tasks), TTI achieves **26.1% success rate**, the best among fully open-source self-improvement agents, improving 7.8% over the zero-shot baseline (18.3%) and outperforming fixed ℎ=10 (23.8%) and fixed ℎ=30 (19.0%) baselines.
  - Fixed ℎ=30 training actually underperforms fixed ℎ=10 on WebArena, confirming that naive long-horizon training without curriculum degrades performance.
- Prompting-based interaction scaling alone (without training) improves WebArena success from 23.81% to ≥28%, while equivalent compute spent on budget forcing yields <3% gain and best-of-n (n∈{3,5,7}) yields even less — establishing interaction scaling as the most compute-efficient axis among those tested.
- TTI agents learn to **automatically trade off interaction length for per-step reasoning depth**: as the curriculum expands the horizon, average tokens per step *decrease*, while GoBack and Bing search actions increase in frequency, indicating the model learns to substitute external information gathering for internal deliberation.
  - This tradeoff emerges without explicit training pressure toward shorter CoTs; it appears as a consequence of the interaction-length curriculum.
- Combining TTI with inference-time re-checking is complementary: re-checking improves performance at all training stages, with larger gains early in training (when the agent has a bias toward premature termination) and diminishing but nonzero gains in later stages.
- TTI's training data is generated entirely by the base model (Gemma 3 12B

## Key Claims

1. The current paradigm of test-time scaling relies on generating long reasoning traces before producing a response, which does not allow agents to acquire new information from the environment or adapt t
2. Scaling test-time interaction (increasing interaction steps) is an untapped dimension of test-time scaling that is orthogonal to chain-of-thought reasoning.
3. A prompting-based 'check-again' mechanism improves task success rate on WebArena from 23% to at least 28% without any training.
4. Interaction scaling achieves higher task success rates than budget forcing or best-of-n under equivalent compute budgets measured in tokens per trajectory.
5. Spending compute on per-step budget forcing yields less than 3% gain on WebArena, while interaction scaling achieves a larger gain for equivalent compute.
6. The check-again prompting mechanism causes the agent to change its action approximately 25% of the time after double-checking.
7. Repeatedly prompting an agent to re-check can cause confusion or hallucination, leading it to revise correct answers into incorrect ones.
8. Prompting alone is insufficient for interaction scaling because it does not enable nuanced behaviors such as switching between exploration and exploitation in the middle of a rollout.
9. Training agents with fixed short horizons (h=5) leads to quick learning but rapid overfitting and premature termination during evaluation.
10. Training agents with fixed long horizons (h=20) causes noisy credit assignment, slower learning, and agents initially associating exploratory actions with high rewards without making task progress.

## Capabilities

- Curriculum-based online RL (TTI) trains web agents to adaptively scale interaction horizon, achieving 64.8% on WebVoyager and 26.1% on WebArena — SOTA among open-source agents trained on open data using only a 12B model
- Interaction scaling (taking more environment steps) outperforms per-step compute scaling (thinking longer) for web agents under equivalent total token budgets
- Prompting-based 'check-again' interaction scaling improves web task success from 23% to ≥28% on WebArena without any training — more effective than equivalent compute spent on budget forcing or best-of-n
- TTI-trained agents learn adaptive exploration-exploitation balance: backtracking and initiating new searches in complex information-dense tasks while following efficient direct paths on simple deterministic tasks
- Self-improvement loop where the base model (Gemma 3 12B) generates its own synthetic training data for web agent RL, achieving SOTA open-data performance without human annotation or proprietary model distillation
- Automated prompting-based verifier using action histories and screenshots achieves 88.9% accuracy vs. ground-truth evaluator, enabling scalable online RL training for web agents without manual labeling

## Limitations

- Prompting-based interaction scaling causes confusion and hallucination with excessive re-checks — agents revise correct answers into wrong ones, causing net performance drops in some domains (e.g., Map)
- Reactive agent policies (trained by SFT or standard per-step RL) are fundamentally suboptimal in partially observable or non-stationary environments where adaptive information-seeking is required
- Training with fixed long interaction horizons causes noisy credit assignment and slow learning — agents initially associate exploratory actions (random link clicks) with high rewards, requiring many iterations to recover
- TTI agents over-rely on search resets (returning to Bing) as a universal fallback rather than performing domain-specific error recovery — including repeated resets within a single trajectory indicating no adaptive error recovery
- TTI agents have critically limited self-verification: when explicitly recognising a mismatch between task goal and obtained result, they still submit the wrong answer — decoupling between stated reasoning and final action
- Context window exhausted rapidly in long-horizon interactions — observation history must be truncated to the 3 most recent steps, creating short-term amnesia that prevents learning from earlier mistakes in the same rollout
- Interaction scaling backfires in knowledge-rich domains (Amazon, GitHub) where exploration exposes the agent to noisy external information, overriding domain-specific knowledge and reducing task success
- Best-of-n action sampling is compute-prohibitive for multi-step interactive settings (n×h more expensive per rollout) and exhibits diminishing returns — making it poorly suited as a test-time scaling strategy for agents
- Context length overflow is a hard blocking constraint on further interaction horizon scaling — explicitly flagged as requiring new memory/compression solutions before the approach can scale further
- Synthetic training verifier introduces ~11% labeling error rate — injecting noise into the RL training signal that could corrupt policy updates, though the 88.9% accuracy is implicitly assumed sufficient
- Separate TTI agents must be trained per benchmark to avoid cross-domain contamination — the approach does not generalise a single policy across fundamentally different web environments, limiting practical deployment scope
- TTI validated only on web navigation — applicability to other high-value agentic domains (robotics, OS control, code execution) is asserted but entirely undemonstrated
- Optimal balance between per-step reasoning depth and interaction step count is theoretically unsolved — TTI training inadvertently compresses CoT length but whether this is optimal for general tasks remains unknown
- TTI underperforms proprietary agents by a wide margin (64.8% vs 84.1–87.0% on WebVoyager) — open synthetic data remains significantly inferior to human-annotated proprietary data for web agent training
- WebArena benchmark contains environment-specific bugs that invalidate valid real-world actions — benchmark scores are not reliable proxies for real-world web agent performance
- Per-step budget forcing changes agent actions only 15% of the time — mostly reordering subtasks, not exploring alternatives or correcting errors — making extended per-step thinking largely ineffective for agentic tasks
- Agents trained at short fixed horizons overfit and show declining performance as training continues — learning exploitative shortcuts that prevent generalisation to longer evaluation horizons

## Bottlenecks

- Long-horizon credit assignment for interactive agent RL: as interaction horizons grow, sparse binary rewards must be attributed across increasingly long action sequences, causing noisy gradients, slow learning, and premature policy convergence to random exploration strategies
- Context window overflow blocks further scaling of agent interaction horizons — truncating observation history to 3 steps already creates information loss; further horizon scaling will overflow context length entirely
- Absence of high-quality open training data comparable to proprietary human-annotated datasets caps open-source web agent performance ~20 percentage points below proprietary model-based agents

## Breakthroughs

- Interaction scaling established as a new, orthogonal axis of test-time scaling: increasing environment interaction steps provides new information that fundamentally cannot be obtained by reasoning longer at each step — outperforming per-step compute scaling under equivalent token budgets
- TTI (Test-Time Interaction) curriculum RL achieves 30% improvement over prior best open-model open-data web agent on WebVoyager (64.8%) using a 12B model trained entirely on self-generated synthetic data — a practical self-improvement loop without human annotation
