### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- Training generalist robot foundation models is blocked by a fundamental data scarcity problem: unlike language and vision, no internet-scale humanoid robot dataset exists, making any single hardware platform's data "orders of magnitude too small" for large-scale pre-training.
  - Cross-embodiment efforts like Open X-Embodiment partially mitigated this by pooling multi-robot data, but variability in embodiments, sensors, actuator DoF, and control modes created an "archipelago of data islands" rather than a coherent training corpus.
- Prior VLA approaches either used pre-trained foundation models as black-box high-level planners requiring separately trained low-level policies, or fine-tuned foundation models directly on robot data without effective strategies for heterogeneous data mixture at scale.
  - Approaches like π0 (Black et al., 2024) used mixture-of-experts architectures to bridge the VLM and action generation model, limiting flexibility in component choice and cross-embodiment generalization.
- Synthetic data generation via simulation faced the sim-to-reality gap, and prior neural augmentation methods were limited to image in-painting or text-to-image diffusion — no prior work had generated entire neural video trajectories at scale for robot training.
  - Real-world teleoperation data scales linearly with human labor, making large-scale collection prohibitively expensive for dexterous humanoid tasks requiring simultaneous bimanual and dexterous hand control.

---

### Proposed Approach
- GR00T N1 is a Vision-Language-Action (VLA) model with a dual-system compositional architecture inspired by Kahneman's System 1/System 2 cognitive framework: a Vision-Language Model (System 2) handles semantic reasoning at 10Hz, while a Diffusion Transformer (System 1) generates closed-loop motor actions at 120Hz.
  - The System 2 module uses Eagle-2 (Li et al., 2025), a VLM built on SmolLM2 and SigLIP-2, pretrained on internet-scale vision-language data; images are encoded at 224×224 with pixel shuffle to produce 64 tokens per frame.
  - The System 1 module is a DiT using flow-matching (action flow matching with Beta-distributed timestep sampling), with alternating cross-attention and self-attention blocks; cross-attention conditions on VLM token embeddings, and embodiment-specific MLP encoders/decoders handle variable state and action dimensions. Critically, representations from the 12th (middle) layer of the VLM outperform final-layer embeddings for downstream policy success.
  - The two systems are jointly trained end-to-end via a unified flow-matching loss plus an auxiliary object detection loss that supervises bounding-box localization from OWL-v2 annotations, enhancing spatial grounding.
- The training corpus is structured as a "data pyramid": human egocentric videos and web data at the base (broad behavioral priors, no action labels), synthetic data in the middle (simulation via DexMimicGen + neural trajectories from fine-tuned video generation models), and real robot teleoperation at the apex (highest embodiment specificity, smallest volume).
  - Action-less data (human videos, neural trajectories) is handled via two complementary techniques: (1) a VQ-VAE latent action codebook (LAPA, Ye et al., 2025) that learns cross-embodiment latent actions from consecutive frame pairs, enabling consistent latent action representations across 8+ embodiments including humans; and (2) an inverse dynamics model (IDM) trained on real robot data using a DiT with SigLIP-2 embeddings to predict pseudo-action chunks from image pairs.
  - Neural trajectories are generated by fine-tuning WAN2.1-I2V-14B with LoRA on 88 hours of in-house teleoperation data, then prompting it with counterfactual language instructions to generate novel trajectories; a commercial multimodal LLM filters outputs for instruction adherence before IDM or LAPA labeling.
  - Simulation trajectories are generated via DexMimicGen, which segments human demonstrations into object-centric subtasks, transforms them to novel object configurations, and verifies success, generating 780,000 trajectories (6,500 hours equivalent) in just 11 hours of compute time.
- The publicly released GR00T-N1-2B model has 2.2B parameters (1.34B in the VLM), was pre-trained on ~592.9M frames (~8,376 hours) of heterogeneous data using up to 1,024 H100 GPUs (~50,000 H100 GPU hours), and supports cross-embodiment inference from single-arm tabletop manipulators to bimanual dexterous humanoids.

### Results & Capabilities
- GR00T-N1-2B outperforms both BC-Transformer and Diffusion Policy baselines across all three simulation benchmarks at all dataset sizes (30, 100, 300 demonstrations per task), with the largest gains on the GR-1 Tabletop benchmark where it exceeds Diffusion Policy by 17.3% at 100 demonstrations (50.0% vs. 32.7%).
  - On the DexMimicGen Cross-Embodiment Suite (9 bimanual dexterous tasks), GR00T N1 achieves 66.5% average success at 100 demonstrations vs. 56.1% for Diffusion Policy and 53.9% for BC-Transformer; on RoboCasa Kitchen (24 tasks), GR00T N1 achieves 32.1% vs. 25.6% and 26.3%.
- On real GR-1 humanoid robot tasks, GR00T-N1-2B achieves 76.8% average success with full data vs. 46.4% for Diffusion Policy — a 30.4% absolute improvement — and 42.6% with only 10% of data vs. 10.2% for Diffusion Policy with 10% data, a 32.4% improvement.
  - Critically, GR00T-N1-2B trained on 10% of data (42.6%) nearly matches Diffusion Policy trained on the full dataset (46.4%), a gap of only 3.8%, demonstrating strong data efficiency from pre-training.
  - Task-specific highlights include: Articulated Object Manipulation at 70.9% (full) vs. 38.6%; Coordination tasks at 82.5% (full) vs. 62.5%; and Pick-and-Place at 82.0% (full) vs. 36.0%.
- The pre-trained checkpoint (without any task-specific fine-tuning) achieves 76.6

## Key Claims

1. GR00T N1 outperforms state-of-the-art imitation learning baselines (BC-Transformer and Diffusion Policy) on standard simulation benchmarks across multiple robot embodiments.
2. GR00T N1 uses a data pyramid training strategy with web data and human videos at the base, synthetic data in the middle, and real-world robot data at the top.
3. The GR00T-N1-2B model has 2.2 billion total parameters, with 1.34 billion in the VLM component, and takes 63.9ms to sample a chunk of 16 actions on an L40 GPU using bf16.
4. GR00T N1 uses the NVIDIA Eagle-2 VLM as its vision-language backbone, which is finetuned from SmolLM2 LLM and a SigLIP-2 image encoder.
5. Using middle-layer rather than final-layer LLM embeddings results in both faster inference speed and higher downstream policy success rate for GR00T N1.
6. GR00T N1 uses action flow-matching with K=4 inference denoising steps, which works well across all embodiments.
7. Neural trajectory generation using fine-tuned video generation models expanded 88 hours of in-house teleoperation data to 827 hours — approximately a 10x augmentation.
8. Latent actions are extracted from action-less videos using a VQ-VAE model that takes current and future frames and outputs a latent action, enabling treatment of human videos and neural trajectories a
9. The VQ-VAE latent action model learns cross-embodiment generalizable action representations, with similar latent actions retrieved across 8 distinct embodiments including both robot and human.
10. DexMimicGen generated 780,000 simulation trajectories equivalent to 6,500 hours of human demonstration data in just 11 hours of compute time.

## Capabilities

- Generalist VLA foundation model (GR00T N1) for humanoid robots achieves 76.8% average success rate across real-world tabletop manipulation tasks (pick-and-place, articulated objects, industrial, multi-agent coordination) on GR-1 humanoid with full training data
- Single VLA model with cross-embodiment support generates diverse manipulation behaviors across single-arm, bimanual, and full humanoid robot embodiments using a unified set of weights and embodiment-specific encoder/decoder MLPs
- Pretrained VLA foundation model achieves high data efficiency in post-training: GR00T N1 with only 10% of task data (42.6% avg success) nearly matches Diffusion Policy trained on full data (46.4%), outperforming it by 32.4 percentage points at equivalent 10% data setting
- Latent action codebook (VQ-VAE-based) enables extracting pseudo-action labels from action-less human egocentric videos and neural-generated videos, unifying them with real robot data under a shared latent action space for cross-embodiment VLA training
- Video generation models finetuned on real robot teleoperation data can synthesize diverse counterfactual robot trajectories with novel language prompts, expanding 88 hours of real teleoperation data to 827 hours (~10x) for VLA training
- DexMimicGen automated simulation trajectory synthesis generates 780,000 demonstration trajectories equivalent to 6,500 hours of human demonstration data in just 11 hours of compute, starting from a few dozen human demonstrations
- Dual-system VLA architecture generates closed-loop motor actions at 120Hz (System 1 diffusion transformer) conditioned on scene understanding computed at 10Hz (System 2 VLM), with 63.9ms inference for 16-action chunk on L40 GPU
- Pretrained GR00T N1 zero-shot generalizes to novel object-container combinations and emergent bimanual handover strategies not seen during pretraining, achieving 76.6% on coordinated bimanual tasks and 73.3% on novel object placement
- Neural trajectory co-training during post-training provides consistent additive gains over real-data-only finetuning: +4.2% to +8.8% in simulation and +5.8% on real humanoid across 8 tasks

## Limitations

- GR00T N1 is limited to short-horizon tabletop manipulation tasks; long-horizon loco-manipulation (combining locomotion and manipulation over extended task sequences) is explicitly out of scope and requires further advances in hardware, architecture, and training data
- Post-training on narrow task distributions causes catastrophic forgetting of general pre-trained capabilities: a post-trained model that saw only right-hand tasks completely loses the emergent bimanual handover behavior present in the pretrained checkpoint
- Synthetic neural trajectory generation struggles to produce physically plausible, diverse counterfactual scenarios — generated videos can violate physics laws, limiting quality and diversity of synthetic training data
- Real-world humanoid robot data collection scales linearly with human labor — no internet-scale humanoid dataset exists, leaving available data per hardware platform orders of magnitude below what's needed for large-scale pretraining without synthetic augmentation
- Pretraining requires massive compute: GR00T-N1-2B consumed ~50,000 H100 GPU hours, and neural trajectory generation required ~105,000 L40 GPU hours on 3,600 GPUs — inaccessible to most research groups despite open-source weights release
- Cross-embodiment data pooling produces 'data islands' rather than a coherent unified dataset — large variability in robot embodiments, sensors, actuator DoF, and control modes prevents seamless cross-robot generalization at internet scale
- Pre-training benefits diminish with large fine-tuning datasets — the advantage of the generalist pretrained model over task-specific baselines shrinks as task-specific data volume increases, suggesting limited asymptotic benefit of pretraining in data-rich regimes
- Long-horizon and liquid/articulated object neural trajectory generation has no quantitative evaluation — capabilities are demonstrated qualitatively only, leaving actual policy training benefits unknown
- IDM (Inverse Dynamics Model) pseudo-action label quality is poor in low-data regimes — LAPA latent actions outperform IDM labels when fewer than ~30 demonstrations are available, with the gap only closing as more real-robot data is available to supervise IDM training
- Pretrained model exhibits jerky, imprecise motion compared to post-trained model even when generalizing successfully to novel tasks — suggesting the pretrained behavioral priors from human video and synthetic data have motion quality gaps relative to task-specific robot demonstration data
- Novel initial frame generation for real-robot neural trajectory post-training requires manual object pose randomization by human operators — automatic generation via img2img diffusion is left as future work, constraining data diversity
- No evaluation of safety, failure modes, or out-of-distribution robustness in real-world deployment — success rate metrics over 10 trials per task do not characterize tail behaviors, collision risks, or graceful degradation under unexpected conditions

## Bottlenecks

- Absence of internet-scale humanoid robot demonstration data: no shared, standardized large-scale dataset across humanoid hardware platforms exists, forcing reliance on expensive teleoperation, synthetic augmentation, and cross-embodiment pooling strategies
- Physics-plausible synthetic robot trajectory generation: video generation models cannot reliably produce counterfactual robot demonstrations that respect physical constraints (object dynamics, contact, deformable/liquid objects), limiting synthetic data quality and diversity
- Long-horizon loco-manipulation integration: extending VLA models from short-horizon tabletop tasks to mobile, whole-body manipulation over extended time horizons requires simultaneous advances in humanoid hardware, model architecture for temporal abstraction, and appropriate training corpora
- Post-training catastrophic forgetting in generalist robot models: task-specific finetuning on narrow demonstration distributions overwrites the general behavioral capabilities acquired during pretraining, with no established mitigation strategy for robot VLA models

## Breakthroughs

- GR00T N1: First open-source generalist VLA foundation model for humanoid robots demonstrating strong real-world performance, cross-embodiment generalization, and high data efficiency via a data pyramid training strategy combining human video, synthetic, and real robot data
- Data pyramid strategy combining latent-action-labeled human egocentric video + physics simulation trajectories + neural video generation enables training generalist robot VLA models without requiring internet-scale robot-specific datasets
