AI Scaling Laws

- AI scaling laws have been a popular topic recently and specifically whether we are beginning to see diminishing returns, especially when it comes pre-training. 
	- There were headlines that suggested that OpenAI, Google and Anthropic struggled to build more advanced AI, going on to say that the next generation of frontier models are falling short of internal pre-training targets. 
	- And the CEO of Anthropic was quoted saying scaling laws are not actually laws of the universe, but simply empirical regularities. Ilya was also quoted as questioning the scaling laws. 
		- So there may be a disagreement between some people, but the breadth of the feedback suggests there is something worth paying attention to. 
- There were people that raised this question early on, specifically whether LLMs would run out of steam, which was tied to 3 different things:
	- A question about whether or not the parameter count runs out. 
		- Historically, mathematical algorithms that do some type of fit, which this is a very sophisticated form of, when you take the variables up to a certain level, it stops adding value as you get too close to the fit. 
	- A question about the size of the context window. 
	- A question about whether or not we are going to run out of data. 
		- There was some pushback in the form of synthetic data, but there were initially some papers that questioned the effectiveness of synthetic data. 
- However, there were statements being made by the likes of OpenAI and Anthropic around spending on the models that implied some form of linear scaling. And so if the some of the comments questioning the scaling law is true, there are implications. 
	- It doesn't mean AI is in trouble or AI is done, but it may mean we're shifting directions, and it's worth thinking about the implications. 
	- This is not the expectation that that OpenAI and Anthropic had 12 months ago because they were promoting a thesis that it was just going to keep going up and they were willing to put 10x over and over again on the size of the cluster they were going to train on. And if it is a shift, even if it leads to insane innovation and continued growth, it is still an unexpected shift. 
- This, in turn, raises some questions. 
	- The Nvidia differentiation is greatest at the largest cluster size. So if LLM pre-training has hit a cap, does that then impact Nvidia demand at all for the bigger systems? 
	- And then there's a differentiation question, which is if the world shifts to inference sooner or faster and if the path of differentiation for the language model companies becomes more inference based than pre-training based, what does that mean for the hardware systems that people are using? 
	- And then the third one is does a ceiling on pre-training allow other people to catch up faster with OpenAI? There was an article out today about DeepSeek that was performing at or near the levels of the latest o1 models. 
- Foundation model pre-training is essentially only one vector of scaling. In addition, there is scaling associated with post-training and now inference time compute. Data and data quality will also scale. 
	- In the early phases over the last 24 months, everybody was mesmerised by the evals achieved by pre-training. But it is important to recognise that as pre-training scaled up, a lot of the low hanging fruit was plucked, and so it makes sense that there is a deceleration in the rate of improvement. 
		- The second derivative definitely seems to be slowing, but there are still gains. The suggestion that a wall has been hit wall suggests no gains and that there are no different vectors of scaling that intelligence. 
		- Looking at 2025, there is still going to be improvements in the models. It won't all come from pre-training and there will be an increasing amount coming from post-training, data quality, and inference time reasoning. 
	- And a lot of people mistakenly thought that if the pre-training ever slows down, this is bad news for Nvidia because the only reason the stock is going up is because people are buying these big clusters to do pre-training, which is not entirely true. 
		- On the Nvidia call today, they called Hopper demand exceptional and is set to continue well into next year, and interestingly, they called Blackwell demand staggering and they are going to increase production even against their prior predictions.
- We essentially watched what appeared to be the increasing commoditisation of the pre-training advantage. Everybody was quickly catching up, even people who did not necessarily appear to be in the lead pack. So if we are in fact seeing a deceleration in the rate of improvement, then it shifts the balance of power to post-training, which relies on quality of data and other vectors of scaling intelligence like reasoning and inference time compute. 
	- Those things may be less of a commodity either because you have a data flywheel that's better than your competitors or because you have just had some breakthroughs architecturally on other ways to scale intelligence. And so, 2025 is going to cast more light on those things. 
- If you assumed that these companies were just going to train on increasingly bigger systems, that may not happen anymore. There was this notion that there was just going to be a constantly building larger model for pre-training LLM, but that may be off the table if there is some scaling wall with pre-training. 
	- And that may not be a bad thing from an investor’s perspective. There were questions around returns and where the money was going to come from. 
		- While the labs may all be hyper focused on these evals, the real world people are focused on use cases. Microsoft has said of its $10 billion in AI revenue, almost all from inference, and Nvidia has said half of their revenue is inference and expects it to be higher in the upcoming years. This in turn implies that more people are getting benefits 

## Key Claims

1. Bloomberg reported that OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic are struggling to build more advanced AI, with GPT-5, Gemini, and Claude 3.5 Opus falling short of internal pre-training targets.
2. Dario Amodei stated that scaling laws are empirical regularities, not laws of the universe, and expressed uncertainty about their continuation.
3. Ilya Sutskever also questioned whether scaling laws would continue.
4. Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz at Andreessen Horowitz made the same statement questioning whether scaling laws would continue.
5. Dario Amodei expressed positivity on scaling laws in a five-hour interview with Lex Fridman, apparently contradicting other public statements.
6. Three potential causes of LLM scaling limits are: parameter count hitting diminishing returns, limits on context window size, and exhaustion of training data.
7. Synthetic data has been shown to introduce significant noise or errors, undermining its value as a substitute for real training data.
8. OpenAI and Anthropic previously promoted a thesis of continuous improvement by training on ever-larger clusters, promising 10x increases in cluster size iteratively.
9. Jensen Huang stated there is no slowdown in AI, but specified that foundation model pre-training is only one vector of scaling.
10. Multiple vectors of scaling intelligence exist beyond pre-training: post-training, inference-time reasoning, and data quality.

## Capabilities

- End-to-end imitation learning for autonomous driving, replacing deterministic hand-coded models in FSD 12.3+
- Persistent memory in conversational AI assistants (Google Gemini, Microsoft models)
- Computer use agents capable of GUI automation and tool interaction (Anthropic computer use, multi-company actions/tools)
- Post-training scaling as viable vector for model improvement independent of pre-training scale
- Inference-time reasoning as a scaling vector for improving model reasoning performance
- Broad consumer deployment of conversational AI assistants with growing user adoption

## Limitations

- Pre-training scaling may be hitting fundamental limits as models fall short of internal targets despite increased compute investment
- Synthetic data quality degrades model performance, limiting ability to circumvent natural data scarcity
- Natural language pre-training data may be finite, with limited high-quality text available for continued scaling
- FSD systems lack extensive real-world deployment experience with driverless vehicles compared to Waymo, limiting confidence in actual safety performance
- FSD 13 requires safer driving modes for robo-taxi deployment rather than unrestricted autonomy, indicating incomplete autonomous capability
- Elon's FSD development priorities emphasize safety improvements ('safety safety safety'), implying current models may not handle diverse driver modalities and situations adequately
- Uncertainty about consumer willingness to allow vehicles to operate driverless in robo-taxi mode, with Turo (comparable service) remaining 'tiny' despite years of operation
- Parameter count scaling has inherent ceiling where fitting mathematical models stop gaining value as variable count increases
- Industry consensus shifting: multiple high-level leaders (Dario Amodei, Ilya Sutskever, Andreessen Horowitz partners) expressing uncertainty about continuation of pre-training scaling laws
- Waymo may still require data center oversight for remote interventions during autonomous driving, suggesting incomplete autonomous capability

## Bottlenecks

- Pre-training scaling hitting diminishing returns, blocking achievement of expected exponential capability improvements and requiring shift to alternative scaling vectors
- High-quality training data scarcity (both natural and synthetic) limiting ability to scale language models through data multiplication
- Real-world autonomous driving validation and operational experience gap at scale limiting deployment of Tesla FSD to robo-taxi service
- State and federal regulatory approval requirements blocking robo-taxi deployment, with variable approval processes across jurisdictions
- Nvidia's competitive advantage concentrated at largest cluster scales, so pre-training scaling plateau may reduce demand for massive GPU clusters and affect Nvidia's growth lever

## Breakthroughs

- End-to-end imitation learning replacing hand-coded deterministic logic in FSD, enabling step-function capability improvements and 10x+ acceleration in development cadence
- Industry shift from pre-training scaling as primary capability lever to multi-vector scaling including post-training, inference-time reasoning, memory, and actions
