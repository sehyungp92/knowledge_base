### Motivation & Prior Limitations
- RLVR has proven effective for mathematical reasoning and code generation but was almost entirely untested in broader, less structured domains such as medicine, chemistry, psychology, economics, and education, where the paper's core contribution lies.
  - Prior RLVR work assumed the availability of precisely structured, easily verifiable answers; benchmarks like GSM8K and MATH have reference answers averaging 1–1.3 words, while real-world multi-domain answers are free-form and often hundreds of tokens long.
  - Rule-based binary verifiers are fundamentally ill-suited to free-form scenarios: only 60.3% of mathematical problems in the authors' dataset have single-term numerical answers verifiable by pattern matching, dropping to 45.4% for complex multi-domain queries.
- Training strong reward models for multi-domain RLVR was believed to require large-scale domain-specific annotation, creating a practical barrier to generalization.
  - Prior work on reference-based reward models for math required 800k domain-specific instances even within a single subject.
- Soft/generative reward signals had been explored in preference-based RLHF but had not been systematically applied within reference-based RLVR to handle unstructured, noisy-label settings across diverse domains.

---

### Proposed Approach
- The paper proposes a cross-domain RLVR framework built around a generative reward model that produces soft, probability-based reward signals rather than hard binary labels, enabling training on free-form answers without rule-based pattern matching.
  - The soft reward is computed as the probability assigned to the positive judgment token (1) by a generative verifier: if the verifier outputs "1", the reward equals P(1|context); if it outputs "0", the reward equals 1 − P(0|context). This keeps rewards bounded in [0,1] while preserving graded signal.
  - Unlike prior work that uses closed-source LLMs (e.g., GPT-4o) directly as verifiers at inference time, the authors distill a compact 7B reward model from a 72B teacher (Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct) using only 160k examples collected during RL exploration, without requiring any CoT rationales or domain-specific annotation.
- The reward model training data is generated by running RL exploration with the improving actor policy and collecting (prompt, reference answer, response, judgment) quadruples, which introduces response variability and formatting noise that improves robustness compared to distilling from a fixed generator.
  - The pipeline has three stages: (1) use the base model with a teacher grader to collect exploration data; (2) fine-tune a 7B reward model via supervised learning on binary judgments; (3) run RLVR on the target policy using soft rewards from the trained 7B RM.
- Z-score reward normalization is applied within each batch, and a KL divergence penalty (β=0.01) against the reference policy is used to stabilize training; three RL algorithms are validated (REINFORCE, REINFORCE++, RLOO).

### Results & Capabilities
- A 7B base model (Qwen2.5-7B) trained with the proposed framework using RLOO and RM-7B soft rewards achieves 62.9% average accuracy on math and 31.2% on multi-subject tasks with REINFORCE, outperforming Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct (47.4% math, 22.6% multi-subject) and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B (26.6% math, 21.7% multi-subject) — models 10× and 4.5× larger respectively.
  - Performance boosts of up to 8.0% accuracy over state-of-the-art open-source aligned models are reported in diverse free-form reasoning tasks.
- The 7B distilled reward model (RM-7B) achieves competitive performance against the 72B teacher it was distilled from: in REINFORCE multi-subject evaluation, the RM-7B-trained policy scores 31.2% vs. 30.3% for the Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct reward model, despite a 10× parameter gap.
- Model-based rewards demonstrate substantially better scaling behavior than rule-based rewards: rule-based reward peaks around 40–60k training samples and degrades to 16.9% average multi-subject accuracy at 100k, while RM-7B continues improving to 35.0% at 100k — a 2× divergence in outcome at scale.
- Out-of-distribution generalization is confirmed on NaturalReasoning and WebInstruct benchmarks, where RM-7B (39.8%, 44.0%) significantly outperforms rule-based reward (29.4%, 33.9%) without any domain-specific fine-tuning.
- Binary verification judgments from different LLMs (Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct vs. GPT-4o) exhibit near-perfect inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ ≥ 0.86 for math, ≥ 0.88 for multi-subject) when expert-written reference answers are available, validating that reference-based evaluation is tractable without domain-specialist annotation.
- Soft model-based rewards outperform binary model-based rewards specifically in high-ambiguity multi-domain settings (e.g., 31.2% vs. 27.3% with REINFORCE + RM-7B on multi-subject), while for structured math tasks the two are roughly equivalent, suggesting soft rewards add the most value where judgment confidence is naturally lower.

### Implications
- RLVR is no longer confined to math and code: this work establishes a practical pathway for applying RL-based reasoning improvement to the full breadth of knowledge-intensive domains, provided expert-written reference answers exist — which is a much weaker requirement than structured, parseable answers.
- The finding that a 7B reward model distilled from 160k noisy, online-collected samples matches or exceeds a 72B teacher model challenges assumptions about annotation scale requirements and democratizes reward model training for multi-domain RLVR.
- The soft reward formulation — deriving signal from generative verifier token probabilities rather than binary classification — offers a general design principle f

## Capabilities

- RLVR can be extended to diverse free-form domains (medicine, chemistry, psychology, economics, education) using generative soft reward models, outperforming 72B-scale aligned models with a 7B policy trained on 30k examples
- Compact 7B generative reward models distilled from a 72B teacher via RL exploration data — without any domain-specific annotation — match or exceed the 72B model as verifiers in cross-domain RLVR (31.2% vs 30.3% on multi-subject, REINFORCE)
- Soft model-based reward signals (derived from generative verifier token probabilities) show monotonically increasing performance with training data scale up to 100k examples, while rule-based binary rewards degrade after ~60k examples

## Limitations

- Only 60.3% of real-world math problems and 45.4% of complex multi-domain queries have single-term answers verifiable by rule-based methods — the majority of practical RLVR training data is structurally incompatible with binary rule-based verifiers
- Rule-based binary RLVR training degrades severely at scale: performance on multi-subject tasks drops from 26.3% at 20k examples to 16.9% at 100k examples — scaling rule-based RLVR beyond narrow-task data is fundamentally unstable
- Frontier models (Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B) achieve only ~22% accuracy on broad multi-domain knowledge tasks — current alignment and reasoning improvements have not transferred to diverse knowledge domains
- Rule-based soft rewards (Jaccard similarity) consistently underperform binary rewards because surface overlap metrics penalise correct answers that use different wording than the reference, producing systematically incorrect reward signals on free-form text
- Cross-domain RLVR requires expert-written reference answers to achieve reliable reward signals — domains without pre-existing objective reference corpora (most open-ended creative, advisory, or novel-problem domains) remain inaccessible
- Prior single-domain reward models require ~800k domain-specific training examples and cannot transfer across domains — the data annotation bottleneck has blocked RLVR from scaling to heterogeneous professional domains
- Process reward modeling for broad domains remains entirely unaddressed — the paper provides only outcome-level rewards, and how to assign step-level rewards without intermediate supervision in complex multi-domain tasks is an open problem
- Whether CoT rationale generation is necessary for accurate semantic equivalence judgement in generative reward models remains theoretically unresolved — the paper's no-CoT approach works empirically but has no principled justification
- The evaluation data is entirely from Chinese educational sources machine-translated to English via GPT-4o-mini — the approach has not been validated on natively English or multilingual free-form domain data, leaving generalisation across languages and cultures unverified
- The 7B reward model exhibits a raw performance gap of ~3.9 points versus the 72B teacher before distillation-gap narrowing, indicating that reward quality is still model-size-dependent in a way that may compound across many RL training steps
- Format-based reward signals are entirely absent from this framework — while this reduces annotation burden, it also removes a known training stabiliser used by DeepSeek-R1 and other RLVR systems, with unknown effects on output quality in deployment

## Bottlenecks

- Absence of scalable, domain-agnostic verifiers for free-form open-domain tasks blocks RLVR from extending beyond math and code — binary rule-based methods fail on unstructured answers, and large-scale domain annotation is impractical
- Rule-based binary reward signals become training-unstable and then regressive at data scale — preventing RLVR from benefiting from larger training corpora in any domain where structured answers are unavailable

## Breakthroughs

- RLVR demonstrated to be effective across diverse free-form professional domains (medicine, chemistry, psychology, economics, education) using a 7B generative reward model trained without domain-specific annotation — previously considered infeasible due to the verification problem
